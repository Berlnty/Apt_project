First, there are  the alternating daily extremes of climate that often range from hot,   sunburning   days to freezing nights. &nbsp;In addition, winds are often strong and humidity   low, resulting in rapid dehydration.&nbsp; Second, the air pressure is lower.&nbsp;   This is usually the most significant limiting factor   in high mountain regions.</font></p>  <table border="0" align="right" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111">    <tr>      <td align="center" width="8">      &nbsp;</td>      <td align="center">      <p align="left"><font face="Arial"><small>Air<span lang="en-us"> </span>		pressure<br>		decreases<br>		as<span lang="en-us"> </span>altitude<br>		increases</small></font></td>      <td align="center" width="5">      &nbsp;</td>      <td align="center" width="8">      <img border="0" src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/air_pressure.gif" hspace="0" alt="drawing of the earth's surface and the atmosphere above it showing that with increasing distance from the earth, the gas molecules in the atmosphere are farther apart and the air pressure is lower" width="244" height="167"></td>    </tr>    <tr>      <td align="center" rowspan="3" width="8">      &nbsp;</td>      <td align="center" colspan="3">        <hr size="1" align="left">      </td>    </tr>    <tr>      <td align="center" colspan="3">      <p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">      <a href="atmospheric_gasses.htm">Click  here for more information<span lang="en-us"> </span>about <span lang="en-us">the earth's </span>atmosphere</a><span lang="en-us">&nbsp;		</span></font></td>    </tr>    <tr>      <td align="center" colspan="3">        <hr size="1" align="left">      </td>    </tr>  </table>  <p><font size="5"><strong><font face="Times New Roman"><a name="atmosphere"></a></font></strong></font>	<font size="3" face="Arial">The  percentage of oxygen in the air at two miles   (3.2 km.)  is <span lang="en-us">essentially </span>the same as at sea level (21%).<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; </span>However, the air pressure is<span lang="en-us">	</span>30% lower at the higher altitude due to the fact that the  atmosphere is less dense--that is, the air molecules are farther apart.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">When we  breathe in air at sea level, the atmospheric pressure of about 14.7 pounds per square inch (1.04 kg. per cm.</font><font face="Arial" size="2"><sup>2</sup></font><font size="3" face="Arial">)  causes oxygen to easily pass through <a href="glossary.htm#selectively_permeable">selectively  permeable</a> <a href="sounds/selectively_permeable.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a> lung membranes into the blood.&nbsp; At high altitudes, the lower air  pressure makes it more difficult for oxygen to enter our vascular systems.&nbsp; The  result is <font color="#333333"><b>hypoxia</b></font> <a href="sounds/hypoxia.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>, or oxygen  deprivation.&nbsp; Hypoxia usually begins with the inability to do normal physical  activities, such as climbing a short flight of stairs without fatigue.&nbsp; Other early  symptoms<span lang="en-us"> of &quot;high altitude sickness&quot;</span> include a lack of appetite,<span lang="en-us"> 	vomiting, headache, </span>distorted vision,<span lang="en-us"> fatigue,</span> and difficulty with memorizing and thinking clearly.  &nbsp;In serious cases, pneumonia-like symptoms (pulmonary edema <a href="sounds/pulmonary_edema.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>)<span lang="en-us"> 	due to hemorrhaging in the lungs</span>   and an abnormal accumulation of fluid around the brain (cerebral edema   <a href="sounds/cerebral_edema.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>) 	develop<span lang="en-us">.&nbsp; Pulmonary and cerebral edema usually 	results in</span> death within a few  days if there is not a return to normal air pressure levels.&nbsp;   There is also an increased risk of heart failure due to the added stress   placed on the lungs, heart, and arteries at high altitudes.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">When we  travel to high <span lang="en-us">mountain areas</span>, our bodies initially develop inefficient <a href="glossary.htm#physiology">physiological</a> responses.&nbsp; There is an increase in  breathing and heart rate to as much as double<span lang="en-us">,</span> even while   resting. &nbsp; Pulse rate and blood pressure go up  sharply as our hearts pump harder to get more oxygen to the cells.&nbsp; These are  stressful changes, especially for people with weak hearts.</font></p>  <div align="center"><center>    <table border="0" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111">    <tr>      <td align="center">		<p align="left"><font face="Arial"><small>Initial inefficient<br>		response<span lang="en-us"> </span>to low<br>		oxygen pressure</small></font></td>      <td width="30" align="center">&nbsp;</td>      <td align="center"><font face="Times New Roman">      <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/high_altitude_response_1.gif" alt="graph illustrating initial inefficient physiological response to low oxygen pressure" vspace="3" width="222" height="169"></font></td>    </tr>    </table>  </center></div><p><font size="3" face="Arial">Later, a more efficient response normally develops as <a href="glossary.htm#acclimatization">acclimatization</a> takes place. &nbsp;<span lang="en-us">Additional</span> red blood  cells and <a href="glossary.htm#capillaries">capillaries</a> <a href="sounds/capillaries.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>  are produced to carry more oxygen.&nbsp;  The lungs increase in size to facilitate the <a href="glossary.htm#osmosis">osmosis</a> <a href="sounds/osmosis.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>  of oxygen and carbon dioxide.&nbsp; There  is also an increase in the vascular network of muscles which enhances the transfer of  gases.</font></p>  <div align="center"><center>    <table border="0" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111">    <tr>      <td align="center">		<p align="left"><font face="Arial"><small>Beginning of<br>		successful<br>		acclimatization<br>		to low oxygen<br>		pressure</small></font></td>      <td width="30" align="center">&nbsp;</td>      <td align="center"><font face="Times New Roman">      <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/high_altitude_response_2.gif" alt="graph illustrating beginning of successful acclimatization to low oxygen pressure after initial decline in fitness" vspace="3" width="310" height="144"></font></td>    </tr>    </table>  </center></div><p><font size="3" face="Arial">However, successful acclimatization rarely results in the same level of physical  and mental fitness that was typical of altitudes close to sea level.&nbsp; Strenuous  exercise and memorization tasks still remain more difficult.&nbsp; In addition, the rate  of miscarriages is usually higher at altitudes above two miles<span lang="en-us"> 	because fetuses receive less oxygen from their mothers</span>.</font></p>  <div align="center"><center>    <table border="0" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111">    <tr>      <td align="center">		<p align="left"><font face="Arial"><small>        <span lang="en-us">Increased f</span>itness<br>		level after successful<br>		acclimatization to<br>		low oxygen pressure</small></font></td>      <td width="30" align="center">&nbsp;</td>      <td align="center"><font face="Times New Roman">      <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/high_altitude_response_3.gif" alt="graph illustrating fitness level after successful acclimatization to low oxygen pressure--it is somewhat lower than it was at sea level" vspace="3" width="301" height="143"></font></td>    </tr>    </table>  </center></div>  <p><span lang="en-us"><font face="Arial">On returning to sea level after 	successful acclimatization to high altitude, the body usually has more red 	blood cells and greater lung expansion capability than needed.&nbsp; Since 	this provides athletes in endurance sports with a competitive advantage, the 	U.S. maintains an Olympic training center in the mountains of Colorado.&nbsp; 	Several other nations also train their athletes at high altitude for this 	reason.&nbsp; 	However, the physiological changes that result in increased fitness are 	short term at low altitude.&nbsp; In a matter of weeks, the body returns to 	a normal fitness level.&nbsp;	</font></span></p>	<div align="center">		<table border="0" id="table2">			<tr>				<td>				<font face="Arial"><small>Enhanced fitness<br>				level for a short<br>				period of time<br>				after<span lang="en-us"> </span>returning to<br>				low altitude</small></font></td>				<td width="30">				&nbsp;</td>				<td>				<p align="center">				<img border="0" src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/high_altitude_response_4.gif" alt="graph illustrating enhanced fitness level after returning to sea level" width="398" height="160"></td>			</tr>			</table>	</div>	<p align="center"><font face="Arial"><span lang="en-us"><br>	</span><b><font color="#808080" size="5">Who <span lang="en-us">Is Most 	Likely to Have</span> <span lang="en-us">High Altitude Sickness?</span></font></b></font></p>	<p><font size="3" face="Arial">Most lowland  people begin to develop hypoxia symptoms at 1-2 miles altitude.&nbsp; However, there are  some permanent settlements in the Andes Mountains in South 	America and the Himalaya Mountains in Asia that are at altitudes of  3 miles.&nbsp; Mountain climbers have reached peaks that are   over 5 miles high, but only <span lang="en-us">rarely without</span> using 	tanks of oxygen to assist in breathing.&nbsp; The highest peaks are too high 	for any human to acclimatize <span lang="en-us">to the point that they could 	stay there for prolonged periods</span>.</font></p>  <div align="center"><center>    <table border="0" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111" id="table1">    <tr>      <td align="center">		<p align="left"><font size="2" face="Arial">Climbers at the<span lang="en-us">		top<br>		</span>of Mt.      Logan<span lang="en-us">, </span>Yukon<br>		Territory, 		Canada<span lang="en-us"><br>		</span>(<span lang="en-us">altitude </span>19,850 feet)</font></td>      <td width="20" align="center">&nbsp;</td>      <td align="center"><font size="3" face="Times New Roman">		<strong>      <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/climbers_at_Mt._Logan,_Yukon.jpg" alt="photo of climbers at the peak of the snow covered Mt. Logan, Yukon Territory, Canada (19,850 feet altitude)" width="193" height="153"></strong></font></td>    </tr>    </table>  </center></div>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">There is considerable variability 	between individuals and between populations in their ability to adjust to 	the environmental stresses of high mountain  regions.&nbsp; Usually, the populations that are most successful are those whose ancestors  have lived at high altitudes for thousands of years.&nbsp; This is the case with 	some of the indigenous peoples living in the Andes 	Mountains of Peru and Bolivia as well as the Tibetans and Nepalese in the 	Himalaya Mountains.&nbsp; The ancestors of many people in each of these populations have lived above 	13,000 feet (ca. 4000 meters) for <span lang="en-us">at least 2,7</span>00 years.</font></p>	<div align="center"><center>    <table border="0" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111">    <tr>      <td align="center">		<p align="left"><span lang="en-us">		<font face="Arial" style="font-size: smaller">Peruvian Indian</font></span><br>		<font face="Arial"><small> woman and<span lang="en-us"><br>		Tibetan</span> man<br>		<span lang="en-us">(both from<br>		high altitude<br>		populations)</span></small></font></td>      <td align="center">&nbsp;</td>      <td align="center"><font face="Times New Roman">      <img alt="photos of an Andean woman and a Himalayan man" src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/Andean_woman_and_Himalayan_man.jpg" width="251" height="156"></font></td>    </tr>    <tr>      <td align="center" valign="top">      &nbsp;</td>      <td align="center" valign="top">      &nbsp;</td>      <td align="center" valign="top">      <p align="left"><i><font face="Arial"><small><span lang="en-us">		(Her  cheeks are       red primarily due to increased<br>		blood		flow near the skin surface.&nbsp;		More red<br>		blood cells help		her get oxygen to the tissues<br>		of her body.)</span></small></font></i></td>    </tr>  </table>  </center></div><p><font size="3" face="Arial">The implication is that <a href="glossary.htm#natural_selection">natural selection</a>  over thousands of years results in some populations being genetically more suited to the  stresses at high altitude.&nbsp; However, different 	populations respond physiologically to low oxygen pressure in somewhat 	different ways.&nbsp; The primary solution of Indians from the high mountain 	valleys in Peru and Bolivia has been to produce more <a href="glossary.htm#hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> <a href="sounds/hemoglobin.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106210055im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>  in 	their blood and to increase their lung expansion 	capability.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; Both result in an increase of oxygen 	carried by the blood.</span>&nbsp; <span lang="en-us">In contrast, t</span>he common solution of Tibetans and Nepalese who live at 	high a<span lang="en-us">l</span>titudes generally has been to breathe 	faster in order to take in more oxygen<span lang="en-us"> and to have 	broader arteries and capillaries, thereby allowing much higher rates of 	blood flow and subsequently greater amounts of oxygen delivered to their 	muscles, despite the fact that they have relatively normal hemoglobin levels.&nbsp; 	A recent study of Tibetan villagers who live their lives at around 15,000 	feet has shown that they have 10 oxygen-processing genes not commonly found 	in lowland populations.&nbsp; The EPAS1 gene is particularly important in 	adapting to environments with consistently low oxygen pressure.</span></font></p>	<p><span lang="en-us"><font face="Arial">Whether you personally will 	experience high altitude sickness in the future may be at least partly a 	consequence of your genetic inheritance.&nbsp;
