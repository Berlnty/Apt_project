class="md-byline">	<div class="written-by">			<span>Written By: </span>			<ul>				<li><a href="/contributor/William-Morton-Pottenger/4753">William Morton Pottenger</a></li>				<li><a Swaine</a></li>				</ul>			</div>	<div class="last-updated hidden-xs">			<span>Last Updated: </span>				<time datetime="2018-03-27">3-27-2018</time>			<a data-type="accordion-link" href="#accordion-article-history" rel="nofollow">See Article History</a>			</div>	</div><div class="grid">				<div class="grid-sm">					<div class="md-alternate-titles">							<strong>Alternative Title:</strong>							computer system</div>					<article id="article-content" class="content"><section id="ref1" data-level="1"><p><strong><span id="ref723556"></span>Computer</strong>,  device for processing, storing, and displaying information.</p><!--[P1]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="231796"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/77/170477-004-B774BDDF.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/77/170477-004-B774BDDF.jpg" alt="computer" data-width="550" data-height="377"></div></div><figcaption><strong class="md-assembly-title">computer</strong><span class="md-assembly-caption">Laptop computer.</span><cite>© Fatman73/Fotolia</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML1]--><p><em>Computer</em> once meant a person who did computations, but now the term almost universally refers to automated electronic machinery. The first section of this article focuses on modern digital electronic computers and their design, <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituent" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="constituent">constituent</a> parts, and applications. The second section covers the history of computing. For details on <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-architecture" class="md-crosslink autoxref">computer architecture</a>, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/software" class="md-crosslink">software</a>, and theory, <em>see</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/computer-science" class="md-crosslink">computer science</a>.</p><!--[P2]--></section><div class="major-reference-upsell">			<iframe src="/GPTIframe/ajax/sync/EB_HOUSE_DESKTOP" scrolling="no" width="640" height="106"></iframe>		</div><section id="ref235887" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">Computing basics</h2><p>The first computers were used primarily for numerical calculations. However, as any information can be numerically encoded, people soon realized that computers are capable of general-purpose <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/information-processing" class="md-crosslink">information processing</a>. Their capacity to handle large amounts of data has extended the range and accuracy of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/weather-forecasting" class="md-crosslink autoxref">weather forecasting</a>. Their speed has allowed them to make decisions about routing telephone connections through a network and to <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/control-system" class="md-crosslink autoxref">control</a> mechanical systems such as automobiles, nuclear reactors, and robotic surgical tools. They are also cheap enough to be embedded in everyday appliances and to make <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/dress-clothing" class="md-crosslink autoxref">clothes</a> dryers and rice cookers “smart.” Computers have allowed us to pose and answer questions that could not be pursued before. These questions might be about <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/DNA" class="md-crosslink">DNA</a> sequences in genes, patterns of activity in a consumer market, or all the uses of a word in texts that have been stored in a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/database" class="md-crosslink">database</a>. Increasingly, computers can also learn and adapt as they operate.</p><!--[P3]--><p>Computers also have limitations, some of which are theoretical. For example, there are undecidable propositions whose truth cannot be determined within a given set of rules, such as the logical structure of a computer. Because no universal <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/algorithm" class="md-crosslink">algorithmic</a> method can exist to identify such propositions, a computer asked to obtain the truth of such a proposition will (unless forcibly interrupted) continue indefinitely—a condition known as the “halting problem.” (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Turing-machine" class="md-crosslink">Turing machine</a>.) Other limitations reflect current technology. Human minds are skilled at recognizing spatial patterns—easily distinguishing among human faces, for instance—but this is a difficult task for computers, which must process information sequentially, rather than grasping details overall at a glance. Another problematic area for computers involves natural language interactions. Because so much common knowledge and contextual information is assumed in ordinary human communication, researchers have yet to solve the problem of providing relevant information to general-purpose natural language programs.</p><!--[P4]--><section id="ref235888" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref829429"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/analog-computer" class="md-crosslink">Analog computers</a></h2><p><a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Analog" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="Analog">Analog</a> computers use continuous physical magnitudes to represent quantitative information. At first they represented quantities with mechanical components (<em>see</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/differential-analyzer" class="md-crosslink">differential analyzer</a> and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/integrator" class="md-crosslink">integrator</a>), but after <a href="https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II" class="md-crosslink">World War II</a> voltages were used; by the 1960s digital computers had largely replaced them. Nonetheless, analog computers, and some hybrid digital-analog systems, continued in use through the 1960s in tasks such as aircraft and spaceflight simulation.</p><!--[P5]--><p>One advantage of analog computation is that it may be relatively simple to design and build an <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/analog-computer" class="md-crosslink autoxref">analog computer</a> to solve a single problem. Another advantage is that analog computers can frequently represent and solve a problem in “real time”; that is, the computation proceeds at the same rate as the system being modeled by it. Their main disadvantages are that analog representations are limited in precision—typically a few decimal places but fewer in complex mechanisms—and general-purpose devices are expensive and not easily programmed.</p><!--[P6]--></section><section id="ref235889" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref829432"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/digital-computer" class="md-crosslink">Digital computers</a></h2><p>In contrast to analog computers, digital computers represent information in discrete form, generally as sequences of 0s and 1s (binary digits, or bits). The modern era of digital computers began in the late 1930s and early 1940s in the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States" class="md-crosslink">United States</a>, Britain, and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany" class="md-crosslink">Germany</a>. The first devices used switches operated by <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/electromagnet" class="md-crosslink">electromagnets</a> (relays). Their programs were stored on punched paper tape or cards, and they had limited internal data storage. For historical developments, <em>see</em> the section <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer/History-of-computing#ref216032" class="md-crosslink">Invention of the modern computer</a>.</p><!--[P7]--><span id="ref235889" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235890" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829434"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/mainframe" class="md-crosslink">Mainframe computer</a></h2><p>During the 1950s and ’60s, Unisys (maker of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/UNIVAC" class="md-crosslink">UNIVAC</a> computer), <span id="ref940315"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/International-Business-Machines-Corporation" class="md-crosslink">International Business Machines Corporation</a> (IBM), and other companies made large, expensive computers of increasing <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/power-physics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">power</a>. They were used by major corporations and government research laboratories, typically as the sole computer in the organization. In 1959 the <span id="ref829435"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/IBM-1401" class="md-crosslink">IBM 1401</a> computer rented for $8,000 per month (early IBM machines were almost always leased rather than sold), and in 1964 the largest IBM S/360 computer cost several million dollars.</p><!--[P8]--><p>These computers came to be called mainframes, though the term did not become common until smaller computers were built. Mainframe computers were characterized by having (for their time) large storage capabilities, fast components, and powerful computational abilities. They were highly reliable, and, because they frequently served vital needs in an organization, they were sometimes designed with <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/redundant" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="redundant">redundant</a> components that let them survive partial failures. Because they were complex systems, they were operated by a staff of systems programmers, who alone had access to the computer. Other users submitted “batch jobs” to be run one at a time on the mainframe.</p><!--[P9]--><p>Such systems remain important today, though they are no longer the sole, or even primary, central computing resource of an organization, which will typically have hundreds or thousands of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/personal-computer" class="md-crosslink">personal computers</a> (PCs). Mainframes now provide high-capacity data storage for <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet" class="md-crosslink">Internet</a> servers, or, through time-sharing techniques, they allow hundreds or thousands of users to run programs simultaneously. Because of their current roles, these computers are now called <span id="ref829437"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/server" class="md-crosslink">servers</a> rather than mainframes.</p><!--[P10]--></section><span id="ref235889" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235891" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829440"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/supercomputer" class="md-crosslink">Supercomputer</a></h2><p>The most powerful computers of the day have typically been called <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/supercomputer" class="md-crosslink">supercomputers</a>. They have historically been very expensive and their use limited to high-priority computations for government-sponsored research, such as nuclear simulations and weather modeling. Today many of the computational techniques of early supercomputers are in common use in PCs. On the other hand, the design of costly, special-purpose processors for supercomputers has been supplanted by the use of large arrays of commodity processors (from several dozen to over 8,000) operating in parallel over a high-speed communications network.</p><!--[P11]--></section><span id="ref235889" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235892" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829441"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/minicomputer" class="md-crosslink">Minicomputer</a></h2><p>Although <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/minicomputer" class="md-crosslink">minicomputers</a> date to the early 1950s, the term was introduced in the mid-1960s. Relatively small and inexpensive, minicomputers were typically used in a single department of an organization and often dedicated to one task or shared by a small group. Minicomputers generally had limited computational power, but they had excellent compatibility with various laboratory and industrial devices for collecting and inputting data.</p><!--[P12]--><p>One of the most important manufacturers of minicomputers was <span id="ref829443"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Digital-Equipment-Corporation" class="md-crosslink">Digital Equipment Corporation</a> (DEC) with its <span id="ref829444"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Programmed-Data-Processor" class="md-crosslink">Programmed Data Processor</a> (PDP). In 1960 DEC’s PDP-1 sold for $120,000. Five years later its PDP-8 cost $18,000 and became the first widely used minicomputer, with more than 50,000 sold. The DEC PDP-11, introduced in 1970, came in a variety of models, small and cheap enough to control a single <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/manufacturing" class="md-crosslink autoxref">manufacturing</a> process and large enough for shared use in university computer centres; more than 650,000 were sold. However, the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/microcomputer" class="md-crosslink">microcomputer</a> overtook this market in the 1980s.</p><!--[P13]--></section><span id="ref235889" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235893" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829446"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/microcomputer" class="md-crosslink">Microcomputer</a></h2><p>A microcomputer is a small computer built around a <span id="ref829447"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/microprocessor" class="md-crosslink">microprocessor</a> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/integrated-circuit" class="md-crosslink">integrated circuit</a>, or <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-chip" class="md-crosslink autoxref">chip</a>. Whereas the early minicomputers replaced <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/electron-tube" class="md-crosslink">vacuum tubes</a> with discrete <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/transistor" class="md-crosslink">transistors</a>, microcomputers (and later minicomputers as well) used microprocessors that <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrated" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="integrated">integrated</a> thousands or millions of transistors on a single chip. In 1971 the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Intel" class="md-crosslink">Intel Corporation</a> produced the first microprocessor, the <span id="ref829449"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Intel-4004" class="md-crosslink">Intel 4004</a>, which was powerful enough to <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/function-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">function</a> as a computer although it was produced for use in a Japanese-made <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/calculator" class="md-crosslink autoxref">calculator</a>. In 1975 the first <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/personal-computer" class="md-crosslink autoxref">personal computer</a>, the Altair, used a successor chip, the Intel 8080 microprocessor. Like minicomputers, early microcomputers had relatively limited storage and data-handling capabilities, but these have grown as storage technology has improved alongside processing power.</p><!--[P14]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="68190"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/61/74061-004-9EAD20C7.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/61/74061-004-9EAD20C7.jpg" alt="Personal computer and peripheralsClick on the images of the inkjet printer, laser printer, computer internal layout, hard drive, and mouse components to display more detailed images." data-width="480" data-height="400"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">Personal computer and peripheralsClick on the images of the inkjet printer, laser printer, computer internal layout, hard drive, and mouse components to display more detailed images.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML14]--><p>In the 1980s it was common to distinguish between microprocessor-based scientific <span id="ref829672"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/workstation" class="md-crosslink">workstations</a> and personal computers. The former used the most powerful microprocessors available and had high-performance colour graphics capabilities costing thousands of dollars. They were used by scientists for computation and data visualization and by engineers for <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-aided-engineering" class="md-crosslink">computer-aided engineering</a>. Today the distinction between <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/workstation" class="md-crosslink autoxref">workstation</a> and PC has virtually vanished, with PCs having the power and display capability of workstations.</p><!--[P15]--></section><span id="ref235889" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235894" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829453"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/embedded-processor" class="md-crosslink">Embedded processors</a></h2><p>Another class of computer is the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/embedded-processor" class="md-crosslink autoxref">embedded processor</a>. These are small computers that use simple microprocessors to control electrical and mechanical functions. They generally do not have to do elaborate computations or be extremely fast, nor do they have to have great “input-output” capability, and so they can be inexpensive. Embedded processors help to control aircraft and industrial <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/automation" class="md-crosslink autoxref">automation</a>, and they are common in automobiles and in both large and small household appliances. One particular type, the <span id="ref829454"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/digital-signal-processor" class="md-crosslink">digital signal processor</a> (DSP), has become as prevalent as the microprocessor. DSPs are used in wireless telephones, digital telephone and cable modems, and some stereo equipment.</p><!--[P16]--></section></section><section id="ref235895" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref829455"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hardware-computing" class="md-crosslink">Computer hardware</a></h2><p>The physical elements of a computer, its hardware, are generally divided into the central processing unit (CPU), main memory (or <span id="ref829459"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/RAM-computing" class="md-crosslink">random-access memory</a>, RAM), and <span id="ref829460"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/input-output-device" class="md-crosslink">peripherals</a>. The last class <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompasses" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="encompasses">encompasses</a> all sorts of input and output (I/O) devices: keyboard, display monitor, printer, disk drives, network connections, scanners, and more.</p><!--[P17]--><p>The CPU and RAM are <span id="ref940318"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/integrated-circuit" class="md-crosslink">integrated circuits</a> (ICs)—small silicon wafers, or chips, that contain thousands or millions of transistors that function as electrical switches. In 1965 <span id="ref829461"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gordon-Moore" class="md-crosslink">Gordon Moore</a>, one of the founders of Intel, stated what has become known as <span id="ref829462"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Moores-law" class="md-crosslink">Moore’s law</a>: the number of transistors on a chip doubles about every 18 months. Moore suggested that financial constraints would soon cause his law to break down, but it has been remarkably accurate for far longer than he first <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/envisioned" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="envisioned">envisioned</a>. It now appears that technical constraints may finally invalidate Moore’s law, since sometime between 2010 and 2020 transistors would have to consist of only a few atoms each, at which point the laws of <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/quantum" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="quantum">quantum</a> physics imply that they would cease to function reliably.</p><!--[P18]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="68188"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/67/74067-004-02138267.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/67/74067-004-02138267.jpg" alt="Moore's lawIn 1965 Gordon E. Moore observed that the number of transistors on a computer chip was doubling about every 18–24 months. As shown in the logarithmic graph of the number of transistors on Intel's processors at the time of their introduction, his “law” is still being obeyed." data-width="480" data-height="390"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">Moore's lawIn 1965 Gordon E. Moore observed that the number of transistors on a computer chip was doubling about every 18–24 months. As shown in the logarithmic graph of the number of transistors on Intel's processors at the time of their introduction, his “law” is still being obeyed.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML18]--><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235896" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829463"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/central-processing-unit" class="md-crosslink">Central processing unit</a></h2><p>The CPU provides the circuits that <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implement" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="implement">implement</a> the computer’s instruction set—its <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/machine-language" class="md-crosslink autoxref">machine language</a>. It is composed of an <span id="ref829464"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/arithmetic-logic-unit" class="md-crosslink">arithmetic-logic unit</a> (ALU) and control circuits. The ALU carries out basic <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/arithmetic" class="md-crosslink autoxref">arithmetic</a> and logic operations, and the control section determines the sequence of operations, including branch instructions that transfer control from one part of a program to another. Although the main memory was once considered part of the CPU, today it is regarded as separate. The boundaries shift, however, and CPU chips now also contain some high-speed <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cache" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="cache">cache</a> memory where data and instructions are temporarily stored for fast access.</p><!--[P19]--><p>The ALU has circuits that add, subtract, multiply, and divide two arithmetic values, as well as circuits for logic operations such as AND and OR (where a 1 is interpreted as true and a 0 as false, so that, for instance, 1 AND 0 = 0; <em>see</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Boolean-algebra" class="md-crosslink">Boolean algebra</a>). The ALU has several to more than a hundred registers that temporarily hold results of its computations for further arithmetic operations or for transfer to main memory.</p><!--[P20]--><p>The circuits in the CPU control section provide <span id="ref829467"></span>branch instructions, which make elementary decisions about what instruction to execute next. For example, a branch instruction might be “If the result of the last ALU operation is negative, jump to location A in the program; otherwise, continue with the following instruction.” Such instructions allow “if-then-else” decisions in a program and execution of a sequence of instructions, such as a “while-loop” that repeatedly does some set of instructions while some condition is met. A related instruction is the <span id="ref829466"></span>subroutine call, which transfers execution to a subprogram and then, after the subprogram finishes, returns to the main program where it left off.</p><!--[P21]--><p>In a <span id="ref829468"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/stored-program-concept" class="md-crosslink">stored-program computer</a>, programs and data in memory are indistinguishable. Both are <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/bit-communications" class="md-crosslink autoxref">bit</a> patterns—strings of 0s and 1s—that may be interpreted either as data or as program instructions, and both are fetched from memory by the CPU. The CPU has a program counter that holds the memory address (location) of the next instruction to be executed. The basic operation of the CPU is the “<span id="ref829469"></span>fetch-decode-execute” cycle:</p><!--[P22]--><ul><li><div>Fetch the instruction from the address held in the program counter, and store it in a register.</div></li><li><div>Decode the instruction. Parts of it specify the operation to be done, and parts specify the data on which it is to operate. These may be in CPU registers or in memory locations. If it is a branch instruction, part of it will contain the memory address of the next instruction to execute once the branch condition is satisfied.</div></li><li><div>Fetch the operands, if any.</div></li><li><div>Execute the operation if it is an ALU operation.</div></li><li><div>Store the result (in a register or in memory), if there is one.</div></li><li><div>Update the program counter to hold the next instruction location, which is either the next memory location or the address specified by a branch instruction.</div></li></ul><p>At the end of these steps the cycle is ready to repeat, and it continues until a special halt instruction stops execution.</p><!--[P23]--><p>Steps of this cycle and all internal CPU operations are regulated by a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/clock" class="md-crosslink autoxref">clock</a> that oscillates at a high frequency (now typically measured in gigahertz, or billions of cycles per second). Another factor that affects performance is the “word” size—the number of bits that are fetched at once from memory and on which CPU instructions operate. <span id="ref829673"></span>Digital words now consist of 32 or 64 bits, though sizes from 8 to 128 bits are seen.</p><!--[P24]--><p>Processing instructions one at a time, or serially, often creates a bottleneck because many program instructions may be ready and waiting for execution. Since the early 1980s, CPU design has followed a style originally called reduced-instruction-set computing (<span id="ref829471"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/RISC" class="md-crosslink">RISC</a>). This design minimizes the transfer of data between memory and CPU (all ALU operations are done only on data in CPU registers) and calls for simple instructions that can execute very quickly. As the number of transistors on a chip has grown, the RISC design requires a relatively small portion of the CPU chip to be devoted to the basic instruction set. The remainder of the chip can then be used to speed CPU operations by providing circuits that let several instructions execute simultaneously, or in parallel.</p><!--[P25]--><p>There are two major kinds of <span id="ref829473"></span>instruction-level parallelism (ILP) in the CPU, both first used in early supercomputers. One is the <span id="ref829474"></span>pipeline, which allows the fetch-decode-execute cycle to have several instructions under way at once. While one instruction is being executed, another can obtain its operands, a third can be decoded, and a fourth can be fetched from memory. If each of these operations requires the same time, a new instruction can enter the pipeline at each phase and (for example) five instructions can be completed in the time that it would take to complete one without a pipeline. The other sort of ILP is to have multiple execution units in the CPU—duplicate arithmetic circuits, in particular, as well as specialized circuits for graphics instructions or for <span id="ref940320"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/floating-point-calculation" class="md-crosslink">floating-point calculations</a> (arithmetic operations involving noninteger numbers, such as 3.27). With this “<span id="ref829476"></span>superscalar” design, several instructions can execute at once.</p><!--[P26]--><p>Both forms of ILP face complications. A branch instruction might render preloaded instructions in the pipeline useless if they entered it before the branch jumped to a new part of the program. Also, superscalar execution must determine whether an arithmetic operation depends on the result of another operation, since they cannot be executed simultaneously. CPUs now have additional circuits to predict whether a branch will be taken and to analyze instructional dependencies. These have become highly sophisticated and can frequently rearrange instructions to execute more of them in parallel.</p><!--[P27]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235897" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829478"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/main-memory" class="md-crosslink">Main memory</a></h2><p>The earliest forms of computer main memory were mercury delay lines, which were tubes of mercury that stored data as ultrasonic waves, and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/cathode-ray-tube" class="md-crosslink">cathode-ray tubes</a>, which stored data as charges on the tubes’ screens. The magnetic drum, invented about 1948, used an iron oxide coating on a rotating drum to store data and programs as magnetic patterns.</p><!--[P28]--><p>In a binary computer any bistable device (something that can be placed in either of two states) can represent the two possible bit values of 0 and 1 and can thus serve as <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-memory" class="md-crosslink autoxref">computer memory</a>. <span id="ref829481"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/magnetic-core-storage" class="md-crosslink">Magnetic-core memory</a>, the first relatively cheap RAM device, appeared in 1952. It was composed of tiny, doughnut-shaped <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/ferrite-iron-oxide-compound" class="md-crosslink">ferrite</a> magnets threaded on the intersection points of a two-dimensional <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/wire" class="md-crosslink autoxref">wire</a> grid. These wires carried currents to change the direction of each core’s magnetization, while a third wire threaded through the doughnut detected its magnetic orientation.</p><!--[P29]--><p>The first <span id="ref829483"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/integrated-circuit" class="md-crosslink">integrated circuit</a> (IC) memory chip appeared in 1971. IC memory stores a bit in a transistor-capacitor combination. The <span id="ref829675"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/capacitance" class="md-crosslink">capacitor</a> holds a charge to represent a 1 and no charge for a 0; the transistor switches it between these two states. Because a capacitor charge gradually decays, IC memory is <span id="ref829484"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/dynamic-random-access-memory" class="md-crosslink">dynamic RAM</a> (DRAM), which must have its stored values refreshed periodically (every 20 milliseconds or so). There is also <span id="ref829485"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/static-random-access-memory" class="md-crosslink">static RAM</a> (SRAM), which does not have to be refreshed. Although faster than DRAM, SRAM uses more transistors and is thus more costly; it is used primarily for CPU internal registers and cache memory.</p><!--[P30]--><p>In addition to main memory, computers generally have special <span id="ref829487"></span>video memory (VRAM) to hold graphical images, called <span id="ref829676"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/raster-graphics" class="md-crosslink">bitmaps</a>, for the computer display. This memory is often dual-ported—a new image can be stored in it at the same time that its current data is being read and displayed.</p><!--[P31]--><p>It takes time to specify an address in a memory chip, and, since memory is slower than a CPU, there is an advantage to memory that can transfer a series of words rapidly once the first address is specified. One such design is known as <span id="ref829488"></span>synchronous DRAM (SDRAM), which became widely used by 2001.</p><!--[P32]--><p>Nonetheless, data transfer through the “bus”—the set of wires that connect the CPU to memory and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/input-output-device" class="md-crosslink autoxref">peripheral</a> devices—is a bottleneck. For that reason, CPU chips now contain <span id="ref829490"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/cache-memory" class="md-crosslink">cache memory</a>—a small amount of fast SRAM. The cache holds copies of data from blocks of main memory. A well-designed cache allows up to 85–90 percent of memory references to be done from it in typical programs, giving a several-fold speedup in data access.</p><!--[P33]--><p>The time between two memory reads or writes (cycle time) was about 17 microseconds (millionths of a second) for early core memory and about 1 microsecond for core in the early 1970s. The first DRAM had a cycle time of about half a microsecond, or 500 nanoseconds (billionths of a second), and today it is 20 nanoseconds or less. An equally important measure is the cost per bit of memory. The first DRAM stored 128 bytes (1 <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/byte" class="md-crosslink autoxref">byte</a> = 8 bits) and cost about $10, or $80,000 per megabyte (millions of bytes). In 2001 DRAM could be purchased for less than $0.25 per megabyte. This vast decline in cost made possible <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/graphical-user-interface" class="md-crosslink">graphical user interfaces</a> (GUIs), the display fonts that word processors use, and the manipulation and visualization of large masses of data by scientific computers.</p><!--[P34]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235898" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829494"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/auxiliary-storage" class="md-crosslink">Secondary memory</a></h2><p>Secondary memory on a computer is storage for data and programs not in use at the moment. In addition to punched cards and paper tape, early computers also used magnetic tape for secondary storage. Tape is cheap, either on large reels or in small cassettes, but has the disadvantage that it must be read or written sequentially from one end to the other.</p><!--[P35]--><p><span id="ref829498"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/International-Business-Machines-Corporation" class="md-crosslink">IBM</a> introduced the first <span id="ref829495"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/magnetic-disk" class="md-crosslink">magnetic disk</a>, the <span id="ref829497"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/RAMAC" class="md-crosslink">RAMAC</a>, in 1955; it held 5 megabytes and rented for $3,200 per month. Magnetic disks are platters coated with iron oxide, like tape and drums. An arm with a tiny wire coil, the read/write (R/W) head, moves radially over the disk, which is divided into concentric tracks composed of small arcs, or sectors, of data. Magnetized regions of the disk generate small currents in the coil as it passes, thereby allowing it to “read” a sector; similarly, a small current in the coil will induce a local magnetic change in the disk, thereby “writing” to a sector. The disk rotates rapidly (up to 15,000 rotations per minute), and so the R/W head can rapidly reach any sector on the disk.</p><!--[P36]--><p>Early disks had large removable platters. In the 1970s IBM introduced sealed disks with fixed platters known as <span id="ref829499"></span>Winchester disks—perhaps because the first ones had two 30-megabyte platters, suggesting the Winchester 30-30 rifle. Not only was the sealed disk protected against dirt, the R/W head could also “fly” on a thin air film, very close to the platter. By putting the head closer to the platter, the region of oxide film that represented a single bit could be much smaller, thus increasing storage capacity. This basic technology is still used.</p><!--[P37]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="68191"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/63/74063-004-1E01870E.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/63/74063-004-1E01870E.jpg" alt="computer hard drive" data-width="480" data-height="400"></div></div><figcaption><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML37]--><p>Refinements have included putting multiple platters—10 or more—in a single disk drive, with a pair of R/W heads for the two surfaces of each platter in order to increase storage and data transfer rates. Even greater gains have resulted from improving control of the radial motion of the disk arm from track to track, resulting in denser distribution of data on the disk. By 2002 such densities had reached over 8,000 tracks per centimetre (20,000 tracks per inch), and a platter the diameter of a coin could hold over a gigabyte of data. In 2002 an 80-gigabyte disk cost about $200—only one ten-millionth of the 1955 cost and representing an annual decline of nearly 30 percent, similar to the decline in the price of main memory.</p><!--[P38]--><p><span id="ref829500"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/optical-storage" class="md-crosslink">Optical storage</a> devices—<span id="ref829502"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/CD-ROM" class="md-crosslink">CD-ROM</a> (<a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/compact-disc" class="md-crosslink">compact disc</a>, read-only memory) and <span id="ref829503"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/DVD" class="md-crosslink">DVD-ROM</a> (digital videodisc, or versatile disc)—appeared in the mid-1980s and ’90s. They both represent bits as tiny pits in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/plastic" class="md-crosslink autoxref">plastic</a>, organized in a long spiral like a phonograph record, written and read with lasers. A CD-ROM can hold 2 gigabytes of data, but the inclusion of error-correcting codes (to correct for dust, small defects, and scratches) reduces the usable data to 650 megabytes. DVDs are denser, have smaller pits, and can hold 17 gigabytes with error correction.</p><!--[P39]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="17652"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/42/20542-004-771D1C10.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/42/20542-004-771D1C10.jpg" alt="The DVD player uses a laser that is higher-powered and has a correspondingly finer focus point than that of the CD player. This enables it to resolve shorter pits and narrower separation tracks and thereby accounts for the DVD's greater storage capacity." data-width="600" data-height="300"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">The DVD player uses a laser that is higher-powered and has a correspondingly finer focus point than that of the CD player. There are also writable and rewritable CD-ROMs (CD-R and CD-RW) and DVD-ROMs (DVD-R and DVD-RW) that can be used like magnetic tapes for inexpensive archiving and sharing of data.</p><!--[P40]--><p>The decreasing cost of memory continues to make new uses possible. A single CD-ROM can store 100 million words, more than twice as many words as are contained in the printed <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Encyclopaedia-Britannica-print-encyclopaedia" class="md-crosslink"><em>Encyclopædia Britannica</em></a>. A DVD can hold a feature-length <a href="https://www.britannica.com/art/motion-picture" class="md-crosslink autoxref">motion picture</a>. Nevertheless, even larger and faster storage systems, such as three-dimensional optical media, are being developed for handling data for computer simulations of nuclear reactions, astronomical data, and medical data, including X-ray images. Such applications typically require many terabytes (1 terabyte = 1,000 gigabytes) of storage, which can lead to further complications in indexing and retrieval.</p><!--[P41]--></section><span class="md-signature"><a href="/contributor/David-Hemmendinger/5383">David Hemmendinger</a></span><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235925" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829505"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/input-output-device" class="md-crosslink">Peripherals</a></h2><p>Computer <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/peripherals" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="peripherals">peripherals</a> are devices used to input information and instructions into a computer for storage or processing and to output the processed data. In addition, devices that enable the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/transmission-engineering" class="md-crosslink autoxref">transmission</a> and reception of data between computers are often classified as peripherals.</p><!--[P42]--><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235899" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref829507"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/input-computing" class="md-crosslink">Input devices</a></h2><p>A <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/plethora" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="plethora">plethora</a> of devices falls into the category of input <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/peripheral" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="peripheral">peripheral</a>. Typical examples include keyboards, mice, trackballs, pointing sticks, joysticks, digital tablets, touch pads, and scanners.</p><!--[P43]--><p><span id="ref829509"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/keyboard-information-recording" class="md-crosslink">Keyboards</a> contain mechanical or electromechanical switches that change the flow of current through the keyboard when depressed. A microprocessor embedded in the keyboard interprets these changes and sends a signal to the computer. In addition to letter and number keys, most keyboards also include “function” and “control” keys that modify input or send special commands to the computer.</p><!--[P44]--><p>Mechanical <span id="ref829510"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/mouse-computer-device" class="md-crosslink">mice</a> and <span id="ref829511"></span>trackballs operate alike, using a rubber or rubber-coated ball that turns two shafts connected to a pair of encoders that measure the horizontal and vertical components of a user’s movement, which are then translated into cursor movement on a computer monitor. Optical mice employ a light beam and camera lens to translate motion of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/mouse-computer-device" class="md-crosslink autoxref">mouse</a> into cursor movement.</p><!--[P45]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="68192"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/64/74064-004-1192BCB2.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/64/74064-004-1192BCB2.jpg" alt="mechanical mouse, optical mouse, personal computer" data-width="600" data-height="400"></div></div><figcaption><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML45]--><p><span id="ref829512"></span>Pointing sticks, which are popular on many laptop systems, employ a technique that uses a pressure-sensitive resistor. As a user applies pressure to the stick, the resistor increases the flow of electricity, thereby signaling that movement has taken place. Most joysticks operate in a similar manner.</p><!--[P46]--><p><span id="ref829603"></span>Digital tablets and touch pads are similar in purpose and functionality. In both cases, input is taken from a flat pad that contains electrical sensors that detect the presence of either a special tablet pen or a user’s finger, respectively.</p><!--[P47]--><p>A <span id="ref829515"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/optical-scanner" class="md-crosslink">scanner</a> is somewhat akin to a photocopier. A light source <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/illuminates" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="illuminates">illuminates</a> the object to be scanned, and the varying amounts of reflected light are captured and measured by an analog-to-digital converter attached to light-sensitive <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/diode" class="md-crosslink">diodes</a>. The diodes generate a pattern of binary digits that are stored in the computer as a graphical image.</p><!--[P48]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235900" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref829517"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/output-computing" class="md-crosslink">Output</a> devices</h2><p><span id="ref829518"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-printer" class="md-crosslink">Printers</a> are a common example of output devices. New multifunction peripherals that <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integrate" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="integrate">integrate</a> printing, scanning, and copying into a single device are also popular. Computer monitors are sometimes treated as peripherals. High-fidelity sound systems are another example of output devices often classified as computer peripherals. Manufacturers have announced devices that provide <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/tactile" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="tactile">tactile</a> feedback to the user—“force feedback” joysticks, for example. This highlights the complexity of classifying peripherals—a joystick with force feedback is truly both an input and an output peripheral.</p><!--[P49]--><p>Early printers often used a process known as <span id="ref829677"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/impact-printer" class="md-crosslink">impact printing</a>, in which a small number of pins were driven into a desired pattern by an electromagnetic printhead. As each pin was driven forward, it struck an inked ribbon and transferred a single dot the size of the pinhead to the paper. Multiple dots combined into a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/matrix-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">matrix</a> to form characters and graphics, hence the name <em><span id="ref829678"></span>dot matrix</em>. Another early print technology, daisy-wheel printers, made impressions of whole characters with a single blow of an electromagnetic printhead, similar to an electric <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/typewriter" class="md-crosslink autoxref">typewriter</a>. <span id="ref829679"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser-printer" class="md-crosslink">Laser printers</a> have replaced such printers in most commercial settings. Laser printers employ a focused beam of light to etch patterns of positively charged particles on the surface of a cylindrical drum made of negatively charged organic, photosensitive material. As the drum rotates, negatively charged toner particles adhere to the patterns etched by the laser and are transferred to the paper. Another, less expensive printing technology developed for the home and small businesses is <span id="ref829681"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/ink-jet-printer" class="md-crosslink">inkjet printing</a>. The majority of inkjet printers operate by ejecting extremely tiny droplets of ink to form characters in a matrix of dots—much like dot matrix printers.</p><!--[P50]--><ul class="assemblies medialist" data-type="other"><li><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="68193"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/65/74065-004-5A9ED975.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/65/74065-004-5A9ED975.jpg" alt="computer laser printer" data-width="600" data-height="400"></div></div><figcaption><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></li><li><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="68194"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/66/74066-004-DDBF4DD5.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/66/74066-004-DDBF4DD5.jpg" alt="Inkjet printerColour inkjet printers can produce nearly any colour by simultaneously heating and depositing various amounts of pigment from black, cyan, magenta, and yellow ink cartridges." data-width="600" data-height="400"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">Inkjet printerColour inkjet printers can produce nearly any colour by simultaneously heating and depositing various amounts of pigment from black, cyan, magenta, and yellow ink cartridges.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></li></ul><!--[ML50]--><p>Computer <span id="ref829682"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/display-information-recording" class="md-crosslink">display</a> devices have been in use almost as long as computers themselves. Early computer displays employed the same cathode-ray tubes (CRTs) used in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology" class="md-crosslink">television</a> and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/radar" class="md-crosslink">radar</a> systems. The fundamental principle behind CRT displays is the emission of a controlled stream of electrons that strike light-emitting phosphors coating the inside of the screen. The screen itself is divided into multiple scan lines, each of which contains a number of pixels—the rough equivalent of dots in a dot matrix printer. The resolution of a monitor is determined by its pixel size. More recent <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/liquid-crystal-display" class="md-crosslink">liquid crystal displays</a> (LCDs) rely on liquid crystal cells that realign incoming polarized light. The realigned beams pass through a filter that permits only those beams with a particular alignment to pass. By controlling the liquid crystal cells with electrical charges, various colours or shades are made to appear on the screen.</p><!--[P51]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235901" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref829521"></span>Communication devices</h2><p>The most familiar example of a communication device is the common telephone <span id="ref829522"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/modem" class="md-crosslink">modem</a> (from modulator/demodulator). Modems modulate, or transform, a computer’s digital message into an analog signal for transmission over standard telephone networks, and they demodulate the analog signal back into a digital message on reception. In practice, telephone network components limit analog <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/data-transmission" class="md-crosslink autoxref">data transmission</a> to about 48 kilobits per second. Standard <span id="ref829525"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/cable-modem" class="md-crosslink">cable modems</a> operate in a similar manner over <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/cable-television" class="md-crosslink autoxref">cable television</a> networks, which have a total transmission capacity of 30 to 40 megabits per second over each local neighbourhood “loop.” (Like Ethernet cards, cable modems are actually <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/local-area-network" class="md-crosslink autoxref">local area network</a> devices, rather than true modems, and transmission performance deteriorates as more users share the loop.) <span id="ref829526"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/asynchronous-DSL" class="md-crosslink">Asymmetric digital subscriber line</a> (ADSL) modems can be used for transmitting digital signals over a local dedicated telephone line, provided there is a telephone office nearby—in theory, within 5,500 metres (18,000 feet) but in practice about a third of that distance. ADSL is asymmetric because transmission rates differ to and from the subscriber: 8 megabits per second “downstream” to the subscriber and 1.5 megabits per second “upstream” from the subscriber to the service provider. In addition to devices for transmitting over telephone and cable wires, wireless communication devices exist for transmitting infrared, radiowave, and microwave signals.</p><!--[P52]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235902" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Peripheral <span id="ref829530"></span>interfaces</h2><p>A variety of techniques have been employed in the design of interfaces to link computers and peripherals. An interface of this nature is often termed a <em><span id="ref829534"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/bus-computer-science" class="md-crosslink">bus</a></em>. This <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/nomenclature" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="nomenclature">nomenclature</a> derives from the presence of many paths of electrical communication (e.g., wires) bundled or joined together in a single device. Multiple peripherals can be attached to a single bus—the peripherals need not be <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/homogeneous" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="homogeneous">homogeneous</a>. An example is the small computer systems interface (<span id="ref829535"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/SCSI" class="md-crosslink">SCSI</a>; pronounced “scuzzy”). This popular standard allows <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/heterogeneous" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="heterogeneous">heterogeneous</a> devices to communicate with a computer by sharing a single bus. Under the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/auspices" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="auspices">auspices</a> of various national and international organizations, many such standards have been established by manufacturers and users of computers and peripherals.</p><!--[P53]--><p>Buses can be loosely classified as serial or parallel. <span id="ref829683"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/parallel-bus" class="md-crosslink">Parallel buses</a> have a relatively large number of wires bundled together that enable data to be transferred in parallel. This increases the throughput, or rate of data transfer, between the peripheral and computer. SCSI buses are parallel buses. Examples of <span id="ref829684"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/serial-bus" class="md-crosslink">serial buses</a> include the <span id="ref829538"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/USB" class="md-crosslink">universal serial bus</a> (USB). USB has an interesting feature in that the bus carries not only data to and from the peripheral but also electrical power. Examples of other peripheral <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/integration" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="integration">integration</a> schemes include integrated drive <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/electronics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">electronics</a> (IDE) and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhanced" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="enhanced">enhanced</a> integrated drive electronics (EIDE). Predating USB, these two schemes were designed initially to support greater flexibility in adapting <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hard-disk" class="md-crosslink autoxref">hard disk</a> drives to a variety of different computer makers.</p><!--[P54]--></section><span class="md-signature"><a href="/contributor/William-Morton-Pottenger/4753">William Morton Pottenger</a></span><span class="md-signature"><a href="/editor/The-Editors-of-Encyclopaedia-Britannica/4419">The Editors of Encyclopaedia Britannica</a></span></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235926" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Microprocessor <span id="ref829542"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/integrated-circuit" class="md-crosslink">integrated circuits</a></h2><p>Before integrated circuits (ICs) were invented, computers used circuits of individual <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/transistor" class="md-crosslink">transistors</a> and other electrical components—<a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/resistor" class="md-crosslink">resistors</a>, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/capacitance" class="md-crosslink">capacitors</a>, and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/diode" class="md-crosslink">diodes</a>—soldered to a circuit board. In 1959 <span id="ref829685"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jack-Kilby" class="md-crosslink">Jack Kilby</a> at <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Texas-Instruments-Incorporated" class="md-crosslink">Texas Instruments Incorporated</a>, and <span id="ref829686"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Noyce" class="md-crosslink">Robert Noyce</a> at <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Fairchild-Semiconductor-Corporation" class="md-crosslink">Fairchild Semiconductor Corporation</a> filed patents for integrated circuits. Kilby found how to make all the circuit components out of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/germanium" class="md-crosslink">germanium</a>, the semiconductor material then commonly used for transistors. Noyce used <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/silicon" class="md-crosslink">silicon</a>, which is now almost universal, and found a way to build the interconnecting wires as well as the components on a single silicon chip, thus eliminating all soldered connections except for those joining the IC to other components. Brief discussions of IC circuit design, fabrication, and some design issues follow. For a more extensive discussion, <em>see</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/semiconductor-device" class="md-crosslink">semiconductor</a> and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/integrated-circuit" class="md-crosslink">integrated circuit</a>.</p><!--[P55]--><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235903" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Design</h2><p>Today IC design starts with a circuit description written in a hardware-specification language (like a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-programming-language" class="md-crosslink">programming language</a>) or specified graphically with a digital design program. Computer simulation programs then test the design before it is approved. Another program translates the basic circuit layout into a multilayer network of electronic elements and wires.</p><!--[P56]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235904" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Fabrication</h2><p>The IC itself is formed on a <span id="ref829688"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/silicon" class="md-crosslink">silicon</a> wafer cut from a cylinder of pure silicon—now commonly 200–300 mm (8–12 inches) in diameter. Since more chips can be cut from a larger wafer, the material unit cost of a chip goes down with increasing wafer size. A photographic image of each layer of the circuit design is made, and photolithography is used to expose a corresponding circuit of “resist” that has been put on the wafer. The unwanted resist is washed off and the exposed material then etched. This process is repeated to form various layers, with <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/silica" class="md-crosslink autoxref">silicon dioxide</a> (glass) used as electrical insulation between layers.</p><!--[P57]--><p>Between these production stages, the silicon is doped with carefully controlled amounts of impurities such as <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/arsenic" class="md-crosslink">arsenic</a> and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/boron-chemical-element" class="md-crosslink">boron</a>. These create an excess and a deficiency, respectively, of electrons, thus creating regions with extra available negative charges (<em>n</em>-type) and positive “holes” (<em>p</em>-type). These <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adjacent" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="adjacent">adjacent</a> doped regions form <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/p-n-junction" class="md-crosslink"><em>p</em>-<em>n</em> junction</a> transistors, with electrons (in the <em>n</em>-type regions) and holes (in the <em>p</em>-type regions) migrating through the silicon conducting electricity.</p><!--[P58]--><p>Layers of metal or conducting polycrystalline silicon are also placed on the chip to provide interconnections between its transistors. When the fabrication is complete, a final layer of insulating <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/glass" class="md-crosslink autoxref">glass</a> is added, and the wafer is sawed into individual chips. Each chip is tested, and those that pass are mounted in a protective package with external contacts.</p><!--[P59]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235905" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref829545"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/transistor" class="md-crosslink">Transistor</a> size</h2><p>The size of transistor elements continually decreases in order to pack more on a chip. In 2001 a transistor commonly had dimensions of 0.25 micron (or micrometre; 1 micron = 10<sup>−6</sup> metre), and 0.1 micron was projected for 2006. This latter size would allow 200 million transistors to be placed on a chip (rather than about 40 million in 2001). Because the wavelength of visible light is too great for adequate resolution at such a small scale, ultraviolet photolithography techniques are being developed. As sizes decrease further, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/electron-beam" class="md-crosslink autoxref">electron beam</a> or X-ray techniques will become necessary. Each such advance requires new fabrication plants, costing several billion dollars apiece.</p><!--[P60]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235906" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Power consumption</h2><p>The increasing speed and density of elements on chips have led to problems of power <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consumption" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="consumption">consumption</a> and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dissipation" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="dissipation">dissipation</a>. Central processing units now typically dissipate about 50 watts of power—as much heat per square inch as an electric stove element generates—and require “heat sinks” and cooling fans or even water cooling systems. As CPU speeds increase, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/cryogenics" class="md-crosslink">cryogenic</a> cooling systems may become necessary. Because storage battery technologies have not kept pace with power consumption in portable devices, there has been renewed interest in <span id="ref829546"></span>gallium arsenide (GaAs) chips. GaAs chips can run at higher speeds and consume less power than silicon chips. (GaAs chips are also more resistant to radiation, a factor in military and space applications.) Although GaAs chips have been used in supercomputers for their speed, the brittleness of GaAs has made it too costly for most ordinary applications. One promising idea is to bond a GaAs layer to a silicon substrate for easier handling. Nevertheless, GaAs is not yet in common use except in some high-frequency communication systems.</p><!--[P61]--></section></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235907" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Future CPU designs</h2><p>Since the early 1990s, researchers have discussed two speculative but intriguing new approaches to computation—<a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/quantum-computer" class="md-crosslink">quantum computing</a> and molecular (<a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/DNA" class="md-crosslink">DNA</a>) computing. Each offers the prospect of highly parallel computation and a way around the approaching physical constraints to Moore’s law.</p><!--[P62]--><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235908" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref829553"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/quantum-computer" class="md-crosslink">Quantum computing</a></h2><p>According to <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics" class="md-crosslink">quantum mechanics</a>, an electron has a binary (two-valued) property known as “spin.” This suggests another way of representing a bit of information. While single-particle information storage is attractive, it would be difficult to manipulate. The fundamental idea of quantum computing, however, depends on another feature of quantum mechanics: that atomic-scale particles are in a “superposition” of all their possible states until an observation, or <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/measurement" class="md-crosslink autoxref">measurement</a>, “collapses” their various possible states into one actual state. This means that if a system of particles—known as quantum bits, or <span id="ref829689"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/qubit" class="md-crosslink">qubits</a>—can be “entangled” together, all the possible combinations of their states can be simultaneously used to perform a computation, at least in theory.</p><!--[P63]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="video" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="194887"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="video"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/82/179682-049-0FBBE7E9.jpg" alt="quantum computer" data-width="426" data-height="240"></div></div><figcaption><strong class="md-assembly-title">quantum computer</strong><span class="md-assembly-caption">Learn about quantum computers.</span><cite>Contunico © ZDF Enterprises GmbH, Mainz</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML63]--><p>Indeed, while a few <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/algorithms" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="algorithms">algorithms</a> have been devised for quantum computing, building useful quantum computers has been more difficult. This is because the qubits must maintain their <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/coherence" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="coherence">coherence</a> (quantum entanglement) with one another while preventing decoherence (interaction with the external environment). As of 2000, the largest entangled system built contained only seven qubits.</p><!--[P64]--></section><span id="ref235895" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235909" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref829554"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/DNA-computing" class="md-crosslink">Molecular computing</a></h2><p>In 1994 <span id="ref829562"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leonard-M-Adleman" class="md-crosslink">Leonard Adleman</a>, a mathematician at the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Southern-California" class="md-crosslink">University of Southern California</a>, demonstrated the first DNA computer by solving a simple example of what is known as the <span id="ref829556"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/traveling-salesman-problem" class="md-crosslink">traveling salesman problem</a>. A traveling salesman problem—or, more generally, certain types of network problems in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/graph-theory" class="md-crosslink">graph theory</a>—asks for a route (or the shortest route) that begins at a certain city, or “node,” and travels to each of the other nodes exactly once. Digital computers, and sufficiently persistent humans, can solve for small networks by simply listing all the possible routes and comparing them, but as the number of nodes increases, the number of possible routes grows exponentially and soon (beyond about 50 nodes) overwhelms the fastest supercomputer. While digital computers are generally constrained to performing calculations serially, Adleman realized that he could take advantage of DNA molecules to perform a “massively parallel” calculation. He began by selecting different <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/nucleotide" class="md-crosslink">nucleotide</a> sequences to represent each city and every direct route between two cities. He then made trillions of copies of each of these nucleotide strands and mixed them in a test tube. In less than a second he had the answer, <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/albeit" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="albeit">albeit</a> along with some hundred trillion spurious answers. Using basic recombinant DNA laboratory techniques, Adleman then took one week to isolate the answer—culling first molecules that did not start and end with the proper cities (nucleotide sequences), then those that did not contain the proper number of cities, and finally those that did not contain each city exactly once.</p><!--[P65]--><p>Although Adleman’s network contained only seven nodes—an extremely trivial problem for digital computers—it was the first demonstration of the feasibility of DNA computing. Since then Erik Winfree, a computer scientist at the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/California-Institute-of-Technology" class="md-crosslink">California Institute of Technology</a>, has demonstrated that nonbiologic DNA variants (such as branched DNA) can be adapted to store and process information. DNA and quantum computing remain intriguing possibilities that, even if they prove impractical, may lead to further advances in the hardware of future computers.</p><!--[P66]--></section></section></section><section id="ref235910" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref829563"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/operating-system" class="md-crosslink">Operating systems</a></h2><span id="ref235910" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235911" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Role of operating systems</h2><p>Operating systems manage a computer’s resources—memory, peripheral devices, and even CPU access—and provide a battery of services to the user’s programs. UNIX, first developed for minicomputers and now widely used on both PCs and mainframes, is one example; Linux (a version of UNIX), <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Microsoft-Corporation" class="md-crosslink">Microsoft Corporation</a>’s Windows XP, and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Apple-Inc" class="md-crosslink">Apple Computer</a>’s <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/operating-system" class="md-crosslink autoxref">OS</a> X are others.</p><!--[P67]--><p>One may think of an operating system as a set of concentric shells. At the centre is the bare processor, surrounded by layers of operating system routines to manage input/output (I/O), memory access, multiple processes, and communication among processes. User programs are located in the outermost layers. Each layer insulates its inner layer from direct access, while providing services to its outer layer. This architecture frees outer layers from having to know all the details of lower-level operations, while protecting inner layers and their essential services from interference.</p><!--[P68]--><p>Early computers had no operating system. A user loaded a program from paper tape by employing switches to specify its memory address, to start loading, and to run the program. When the program finished, the computer halted. The programmer had to have knowledge of every computer detail, such as how much memory it had and the characteristics of I/O devices used by the program.</p><!--[P69]--><p>It was quickly realized that this was an inefficient use of resources, particularly as the CPU was largely idle while waiting for relatively slow I/O devices to finish tasks such as reading and writing data. If instead several programs could be loaded at once and coordinated to interleave their steps of computation and I/O, more work could be done. The earliest operating systems were small supervisor programs that did just that: they coordinated several programs, accepting commands from the operator, and provided them all with basic I/O operations. These were known as multiprogrammed systems.</p><!--[P70]--><p>A multiprogrammed system must schedule its programs according to some priority rule, such as “shortest jobs first.” It must protect them from mutual interference to prevent an addressing error in a program from corrupting the data or code of another. It must ensure noninterference during I/O so that output from several programs does not get commingled or input misdirected. It might also have to record the CPU time of each job for billing purposes.</p><!--[P71]--></section><span id="ref235910" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235912" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Modern types of operating systems</h2><span id="ref235910" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235913" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref829565"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/time-sharing" class="md-crosslink">Multiuser systems</a></h2><p>An <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/plug-in" class="md-crosslink autoxref">extension</a> of multiprogramming systems was developed in the 1960s, known variously as multiuser or time-sharing systems. (For a history of this development, <em>see</em> the section <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer/History-of-computing#ref216067" class="md-crosslink">Time-sharing from Project MAC to UNIX</a>.) Time-sharing allows many people to interact with a computer at once, each getting a small portion of the CPU’s time. If the CPU is fast enough, it will appear to be dedicated to each user, particularly as a computer can perform many functions while waiting for each user to finish typing the latest commands.</p><!--[P72]--><p>Multiuser operating systems employ a technique known as <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/multiprocessing" class="md-crosslink autoxref">multiprocessing</a>, or <span id="ref829567"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/multitasking" class="md-crosslink">multitasking</a> (as do most single-user systems today), in which even a single program may consist of many separate computational activities, called processes. The system must keep track of active and queued processes, when each process must access secondary memory to retrieve and store its code and data, and the allocation of other resources, such as peripheral devices.</p><!--[P73]--><p>Since main memory was very limited, early operating systems had to be as small as possible to leave room for other programs. To overcome some of this limitation, operating systems use <span id="ref829570"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/virtual-memory" class="md-crosslink">virtual memory</a>, one of many computing techniques developed during the late 1950s under the direction of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Tom-Kilburn" class="md-crosslink">Tom Kilburn</a> at the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Manchester" class="md-crosslink autoxref">University of Manchester</a>, England. Virtual memory gives each process a large address space (memory that it may use), often much larger than the actual main memory. This address space resides in secondary memory (such as tape or disks), from which portions are copied into main memory as needed, updated as necessary, and returned when a process is no longer active. Even with virtual memory, however, some “kernel” of the operating system has to remain in main memory. Early UNIX kernels occupied tens of kilobytes; today they occupy more than a megabyte, and PC operating systems are comparable, largely because of the declining cost of main memory.</p><!--[P74]--><p>Operating systems have to maintain virtual memory tables to keep track of where each process’s address space resides, and modern CPUs provide special registers to make this more efficient. Indeed, much of an operating system consists of tables: tables of processes, of files and their locations (directories), of resources used by each process, and so on. There are also tables of user accounts and passwords that help control access to the user’s files and protect them against accidental or <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/malicious" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="malicious">malicious</a> interference.</p><!--[P75]--></section><span id="ref235910" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235914" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Thin systems</h2><p>While minimizing the memory requirements of operating systems for standard computers has been important, it has been absolutely essential for small, inexpensive, specialized devices such as personal digital assistants (PDAs), “smart” cellular telephones, portable devices for listening to compressed music files, and Internet kiosks. Such devices must be highly reliable, fast, and secure against break-ins or corruption—a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/cell-phone" class="md-crosslink">cellular telephone</a> that “freezes” in the middle of calls would not be tolerated. One might argue that these traits should characterize any operating system, but PC users seem to have become quite tolerant of frequent operating system failures that require restarts.</p><!--[P76]--></section><span id="ref235910" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235915" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Reactive systems</h2><p>Still more limited are embedded, or real-time, systems. These are small systems that run the control processors embedded in machinery from factory production lines to home appliances. They interact with their <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="environment">environment</a>, taking in data from sensors and making appropriate responses. Embedded systems are known as “hard” real-time systems if they must guarantee schedules that handle all events even in a worst case and “soft” if missed deadlines are not fatal. An aircraft control system is a <span id="ref829690"></span>hard real-time system, as a single flight error might be fatal. An airline reservation system, on the other hand, is a <span id="ref829691"></span>soft real-time system, since a missed booking is rarely catastrophic.</p><!--[P77]--><p>Many of the features of modern CPUs and operating systems are inappropriate for hard real-time systems. For example, pipelines and superscalar multiple execution units give high performance at the expense of occasional delays when a branch prediction fails and a pipeline is filled with unneeded instructions. Likewise, virtual memory and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/caches" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="caches">caches</a> give good memory-access times on the average, but sometimes they are slow. Such variability is <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inimical" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="inimical">inimical</a> to meeting demanding real-time schedules, and so embedded processors and their operating systems must generally be relatively simple.</p><!--[P78]--></section></section><span id="ref235910" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235916" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Operating system design approaches</h2><p>Operating systems may be <span id="ref829693"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/proprietary-system" class="md-crosslink">proprietary</a> or open. Mainframe systems have largely been <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/proprietary" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="proprietary">proprietary</a>, supplied by the computer manufacturer. In the PC domain, Microsoft offers its proprietary Windows systems, Apple has supplied <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Mac-OS" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Mac OS</a> for its line of Macintosh computers, and there are few other choices. The best-known <span id="ref829692"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/open-source" class="md-crosslink">open system</a> has been <span id="ref829694"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/UNIX" class="md-crosslink">UNIX</a>, originally developed by <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bell-Laboratories" class="md-crosslink">Bell Laboratories</a> and supplied freely to universities. In its <span id="ref829695"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Linux" class="md-crosslink">Linux</a> variant it is available for a wide range of PCs, workstations, and, most recently, IBM mainframes.</p><!--[P79]--><p>Open-source software is copyrighted, but its author grants free use, often including the right to modify it provided that use of the new version is not restricted. Linux is protected by the Free Software Foundation’s “GNU General Public License,” like all the other software in the extensive <span id="ref829696"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/GNU-Project" class="md-crosslink">GNU project</a>, and this protection permits users to modify Linux and even to sell copies, provided that this right of free use is preserved in the copies.</p><!--[P80]--><p>One consequence of the right of free use is that numerous authors have contributed to the GNU-Linux work, adding many valuable components to the basic system. Although quality control is managed voluntarily and some have predicted that Linux would not survive heavy commercial use, it has been remarkably successful and seems well on its way to becoming the version of UNIX on mainframes and on PCs used as Internet servers.</p><!--[P81]--><p>There are other variants of the UNIX system; some are proprietary, though most are now freely used, at least noncommercially. They all provide some type of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/graphical-user-interface" class="md-crosslink autoxref">graphical user interface</a>. Although Mac OS has been proprietary, its current version, Mac OS X, is built on UNIX.</p><!--[P82]--><p>Proprietary systems such as <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Windows-OS" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Microsoft’s Windows</a> 98, 2000, and XP provide highly integrated systems. All operating systems provide file directory services, for example, but a Microsoft system might use the same window display for a directory as for a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/World-Wide-Web" class="md-crosslink">World Wide Web</a> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/browser" class="md-crosslink autoxref">browser</a>. Such an integrated approach makes it more difficult for nonproprietary software to use Windows capabilities, a feature that has been an issue in antitrust lawsuits against Microsoft.</p><!--[P83]--></section></section><section id="ref235917" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref829571"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-network" class="md-crosslink">Networking</a></h2><p>Computer communication may occur through wires, optical fibres, or radio transmissions. Wired networks may use shielded <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/coaxial-cable" class="md-crosslink autoxref">coaxial cable</a>, similar to the wire connecting a television to a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/videocassette-recorder" class="md-crosslink autoxref">videocassette recorder</a> or an antenna. They can also use simpler unshielded wiring with modular connectors similar to telephone wires. Optical fibres can carry more signals than wires; they are often used for linking buildings on a college campus or corporate site and increasingly for longer distances as telephone companies update their networks. <span id="ref829697"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/microwave-radio" class="md-crosslink">Microwave radio</a> also carries <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-network" class="md-crosslink autoxref">computer network</a> signals, generally as part of long-distance telephone systems. Low-power microwave radio is becoming common for wireless networks within a building.</p><!--[P84]--><span id="ref235917" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235918" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829572"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/local-area-network" class="md-crosslink">Local area networks</a></h2><p><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/local-area-network" class="md-crosslink">Local area networks</a> (LANs) connect computers within a building or small group of buildings. A LAN may be configured as (1) a bus, a main channel to which nodes or secondary channels are connected in a branching structure, (2) a ring, in which each computer is connected to two neighbouring computers to form a closed circuit, or (3) a star, in which each computer is linked directly to a central computer and only indirectly to one another. Each of these has advantages, though the bus configuration has become the most common.</p><!--[P85]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="68195"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/37/24337-004-B57997F0.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/37/24337-004-B57997F0.jpg" alt="Local area networks (LANs)Simple bus networks, such as Ethernet, are common for home and small office configurations. The most common ring network is IBM's Token Ring, which employs a “token” that is passed around the network to control which location has sending privileges. Star networks are common in larger commercial networks since a malfunction at any node generally does not disrupt the entire network." data-width="753" data-height="300"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">Local area networks (LANs)Simple bus networks, such as Ethernet, are common for home and small office configurations. The most common ring network is IBM's Token Ring, which employs a “token” that is passed around the network to control which location has sending privileges. Star networks are common in larger commercial networks since a malfunction at any node generally does not disrupt the entire network.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML85]--><p>Even if only two computers are connected, they must follow rules, or <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/protocols" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="protocols">protocols</a>, to communicate. For example, one might signal “ready to send” and wait for the other to signal “ready to receive.” When many computers share a network, the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/protocol" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="protocol">protocol</a> might include a rule “talk only when it is your turn” or “do not talk when anyone else is talking.” Protocols must also be designed to handle network errors.</p><!--[P86]--><p>The most common LAN design since the mid-1970s has been the bus-connected <span id="ref829573"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Ethernet" class="md-crosslink">Ethernet</a>, originally developed at <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Xerox-PARC" class="md-crosslink">Xerox PARC</a>. Every computer or other device on an Ethernet has a unique 48-bit address. Any computer that wants to transmit listens for a carrier signal that indicates that a transmission is under way. If it detects none, it starts transmitting, sending the address of the recipient at the start of its transmission. Every system on the network receives each message but ignores those not addressed to it. While a system is transmitting, it also listens, and if it detects a simultaneous transmission, it stops, waits for a random time, and retries. The random time delay before retrying reduces the probability that they will collide again. This scheme is known as <span id="ref829574"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/carrier-sense-multiple-access-with-collision-detection" class="md-crosslink">carrier sense multiple access with collision detection</a> (CSMA/CD). It works very well until a network is moderately heavily loaded, and then it degrades as collisions become more frequent.</p><!--[P87]--><p>The first Ethernet had a capacity of about 2 megabits per second, and today 10- and 100-megabit-per-second Ethernet is common, with gigabit-per-second Ethernet also in use. Ethernet transceivers (transmitter-receivers) for PCs are inexpensive and easily installed.</p><!--[P88]--><p>A recent standard for wireless Ethernet, known as <span id="ref829576"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Wi-Fi" class="md-crosslink">Wi-Fi</a>, is becoming common for small office and home networks. Using frequencies from 2.4 to 5 gigahertz (GHz), such networks can transfer data at rates up to 600 megabits per second. Early in 2002 another Ethernet-like standard was released. Known as <span id="ref829578"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/HomePlug" class="md-crosslink">HomePlug</a>, the first version could transmit data at about 8 megabits per second through a building’s existing electrical power <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infrastructure" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="infrastructure">infrastructure</a>. A later version could achieve rates of 1 gigabit per second.</p><!--[P89]--></section><span id="ref235917" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235919" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829579"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/wide-area-network" class="md-crosslink">Wide area networks</a></h2><p>Wide area networks (WANs) span cities, countries, and the globe, generally using telephone lines and satellite links. The Internet connects multiple WANs; as its name suggests, it is a network of networks. Its success stems from early support by the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/US-Department-of-Defense" class="md-crosslink">U.S. Department of Defense</a>, which developed its <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/precursor" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="precursor">precursor</a>, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/ARPANET" class="md-crosslink">ARPANET</a>, to let researchers communicate readily and share computer resources. Its success is also due to its flexible communication technique. The emergence of the Internet in the 1990s as not only a communication medium but also one of the principal focuses of computer use may be the most significant development in computing in the past several decades. For more on the history and technical details of Internet communication protocols, <em>see</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet" class="md-crosslink">Internet</a>.</p><!--[P90]--></section></section><section id="ref235920" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Computer <span id="ref829581"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/software" class="md-crosslink">software</a></h2><p><em>Software</em> denotes programs that run on computers. <span id="ref829580"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Wilder-Tukey" class="md-crosslink">John Tukey</a>, a statistician at <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Princeton-University" class="md-crosslink">Princeton University</a> and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bell-Laboratories" class="md-crosslink">Bell Laboratories</a>, is generally credited with introducing the term in 1958 (as well as coining the word <em>bit</em> for binary digit). Initially software referred primarily to what is now called <span id="ref829582"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/system-software" class="md-crosslink">system software</a>—an operating system and the utility programs that come with it, such as those to compile (translate) programs into machine code and load them for execution. This software came with a computer when it was bought or leased. In 1969 IBM decided to “unbundle” its software and sell it separately, and software soon became a major income source for manufacturers as well as for dedicated software firms.</p><!--[P91]--><span id="ref235920" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235921" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Business and personal software</h2><p>Business software generally must handle large amounts of data but relatively little computation, although that has changed somewhat in recent years. Office software typically includes <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/word-processing" class="md-crosslink">word processors</a>, spreadsheets, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/database" class="md-crosslink">database</a> programs, and tools for designing public presentations.</p><!--[P92]--><p>A <span id="ref829584"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/spreadsheet" class="md-crosslink">spreadsheet</a> is a type of accounting program. Unlike specialized accounting programs (e.g., payroll and office records), an important function of spreadsheets is their ability to explore “What if?” scenarios. A spreadsheet not only holds tables of data but also defines relationships among their rows and columns. For example, if the profit on a product is defined in terms of various costs—materials, manufacturing, and shipping—it is easy to ask “What if we use cheaper materials that require more manufacturing expense?”</p><!--[P93]--><p>A database is an organized collection of data, or <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/archives" class="md-crosslink autoxref">records</a>. Databases organize information to answer questions such as “What companies in the Southwest bought more than 100 of our products last year?” or “Which products made by Acme Manufacturing are in low supply?” Such software is often integrated so that a database report or spreadsheet table can be added to a document composed with a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/word-processor" class="md-crosslink autoxref">word processor</a>, frequently with illustrative graphs. Today even the most trivial data can effortlessly be glorified by presenting it in a polychromatic bar chart with three-dimensional shading.</p><!--[P94]--></section><span id="ref235920" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235922" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Scientific and engineering software</h2><p>Scientific software is typically used to solve <span id="ref829585"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/differential-equation" class="md-crosslink">differential equations</a>. (Differential equations are used to describe continuous actions or processes that depend on some other factors.) Although some differential equations have relatively simple mathematical solutions, exact solutions of many differential equations are very difficult to obtain. Computers, however, can be used to obtain useful approximate solutions, particularly when a problem is split into simpler spatial or temporal parts. Nevertheless, large-scale problems often require parallel computation on supercomputers or clusters of small computers that share the work.</p><!--[P95]--><p>There are numerous standard libraries of equation-solving software—some commercial, some distributed by national organizations in several countries. Another kind of software package does symbolic mathematics, obtaining exact solutions by algebraic manipulations. Two of the most widely used symbolic packages are Mathematica and Maple.</p><!--[P96]--><p>Scientific <span id="ref829586"></span>visualization software couples high-performance graphics with the output of equation solvers to yield vivid displays of models of physical systems. As with spreadsheets, visualization software lets an experimenter vary initial conditions or <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/parameters" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="parameters">parameters</a>. Observing the effect of such changes can help in improving models, as well as in understanding the original system.</p><!--[P97]--><p>Visualization is an essential feature of <span id="ref829587"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-aided-engineering" class="md-crosslink">computer-aided engineering</a> (CAE) and <span id="ref829589"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/computer-aided-design" class="md-crosslink">computer-aided design</a> (CAD). An engineer can design a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/bridge-electrical-instrument" class="md-crosslink autoxref">bridge</a>, use modeling software to display it, and study it under different loads. CAE software can translate drawings into the precise specification of the parts of a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/mechanical-system" class="md-crosslink autoxref">mechanical system</a>. Computer chips themselves are designed with CAD programs that let an engineer write a specification for part of a chip, simulate its behaviour in detail, test it thoroughly, and then generate the layouts for the photolithographic process that puts the circuit on the silicon.</p><!--[P98]--><p>Astronomical sky surveys, weather forecasting, and medical imaging—such as <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/magnetic-resonance-imaging" class="md-crosslink autoxref">magnetic resonance imaging</a>, CAT scans, and DNA analyses—create very large collections of data. Scientific computation today uses the same kinds of powerful statistical and pattern-analysis techniques as many business applications.</p><!--[P99]--></section><span id="ref235920" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235923" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829590"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet" class="md-crosslink">Internet</a> and collaborative software</h2><p>Among the most commonly used personal Internet software are “browsers” for displaying information located on the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/World-Wide-Web" class="md-crosslink">World Wide Web</a>, newsreaders for reading “newsgroups” located on USENET, file-sharing programs for downloading files, and communication software for <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/e-mail" class="md-crosslink">e-mail</a>, as well as “instant messaging” and “chat room” programs that allow people to carry on conversations in real time. All of these applications are used for both personal and business activities.</p><!--[P100]--><p>Other common Internet software includes Web search engines and “<span id="ref829594"></span>Web-crawling” programs that <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/traverse" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="traverse">traverse</a> the Web to gather and classify information. Web-crawling programs are a kind of <span id="ref829592"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/agent" class="md-crosslink">agent</a> software, a term for programs that carry out routine tasks for a user. They stem from <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/artificial-intelligence" class="md-crosslink autoxref">artificial intelligence</a> research and carry out some of the tasks of librarians, but they are at a severe disadvantage. Although Web pages may have “content-tag” index terms, not all do, nor are there yet accepted standards for their use. Web search engines must use <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/heuristic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="heuristic">heuristic</a> methods to determine the quality of Web page information as well as its content. Many details are proprietary, but they may use techniques such as finding “hubs” and “authorities” (pages with many links to and from other Web sites). Such strategies can be very effective, though the need for a Web version of card catalogs has not vanished.</p><!--[P101]--><p>A different kind of Internet use depends on the vast number of computers connected to the Internet that are idle much of the time. Rather than run a “screen-saver” program, these computers can run software that lets them <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/collaborate" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="collaborate">collaborate</a> in the analysis of some difficult problem. Two examples are the SETI@home project, which distributes portions of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/radio-telescope" class="md-crosslink autoxref">radio telescope</a> data for analysis that might help in the search for <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/extraterrestrial-intelligence" class="md-crosslink autoxref">extraterrestrial intelligence</a> (<span id="ref829596"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/event/SETI" class="md-crosslink">SETI</a>), and the “Great Internet Mersenne Prime Search” (GIMPS), which parcels out tasks to test for large prime numbers.</p><!--[P102]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="99759"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/37/99737-004-4AEC842E.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/37/99737-004-4AEC842E.jpg" alt="Screen shot of the SETI@home page." data-width="550" data-height="412"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">Screen shot of the SETI@home page.</span><cite>Seti@home/University of California at Berkeley</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML102]--><p>The Internet has also become a business <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/tool" class="md-crosslink autoxref">tool</a>, and the ability to collect and store immense amounts of information has given rise to <span id="ref829597"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/data-warehousing" class="md-crosslink">data warehousing</a> and <span id="ref829599"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/data-mining" class="md-crosslink">data mining</a>. The former is a term for unstructured collections of data and the latter a term for its analysis. Data mining uses statistics and other mathematical tools to find patterns of information. For more information concerning business on the Internet, <em>see</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/e-commerce" class="md-crosslink">e-commerce</a>.</p><!--[P103]--></section><span id="ref235920" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref235924" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref829600"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/electronic-game" class="md-crosslink">Games</a> and entertainment</h2><p>Computer games are nearly as old as digital computers and have steadily developed in sophistication. Chinook, a recent <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/checkers" class="md-crosslink">checkers</a> (draughts) program, is widely believed to be better than any human player, and the IBM <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Deep-Blue" class="md-crosslink">Deep Blue</a> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/chess/The-time-element-and-competition#ref80452" class="md-crosslink">chess</a> program beat world champion <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Garry-Kasparov" class="md-crosslink">Garry Kasparov</a> in 1996. These programs have demonstrated the power of modern computers, as well as the strength of good <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/heuristics" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="heuristics">heuristics</a> for strategy. On the other hand, such brute-force search heuristics have failed to produce a <span id="ref829602"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/go-game" class="md-crosslink">go</a>-playing program that can defeat even moderately skilled players because there are too many possible moves in this Japanese game for simple quantification.</p><!--[P104]--><p>After board games, the earliest computer games were text-based adventures—in which players explored virtual worlds, sought treasure, and fought enemies by reading and typing simple commands. Such games resembled military simulation programs first used in the early 1950s. Contemporary games, however, depend on high-performance <span id="ref940329"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/computer-graphics" class="md-crosslink">computer graphics</a>. Played on arcade machines, special game computers for home use, or PCs, they use the same capabilities as simulation and visualization programs. A related area is computer-generated (CG) <a href="https://www.britannica.com/art/animation" class="md-crosslink">animation</a> for films and video.</p><!--[P105]--></section></section><span
