id="article-meta_1-0" class="comp article-meta mntl-block" data-tracking-container="true">				<div id="byline_1-0" class="comp byline right-rail__offset"><span class="byline-name">by<ahref="https://www.thoughtco.com/kimberly-powell-1420408">Kimberly Powell</a><!-- end: --></span></div><!-- end: comp byline right-rail__offset --><div id="updated-label_1-0" class="comp updated-label article-updated-label">Updated March 17, 2017</div><!-- end: comp updated-label article-updated-label -->		</div><!-- end: comp article-meta mntl-block --></div><div id="flex_1-0" class="comp flex article-content"><p>English surnames as we know them today -- family names passed down intact from father to son to grandson -- weren&#39;t widely used until after the Norman conquest of 1066. Prior to that time there just weren&#39;t enough people to really make it necessary to use anything other than a single name. As the country&#39;s population grew, however, people began tacking on descriptions such as &#34;John the Baker&#34; or &#34;Thomas, son of Richard&#34; to distinguish between men (and women) of the same name.</p><div id="billboard1-sticky_1-0" class="comp billboard1-sticky billboard-sticky scads-to-load right-rail__item" data-height="1050"><div class="spacer"><div id="billboard1_1-0" class="comp billboard1 mntl-gpt-adunit gpt billboard "><div id="billboard" class="wrapper"></div></div><!-- end: comp billboard1 mntl-gpt-adunit gpt billboard --></div></div><!-- end: comp billboard1-sticky billboard-sticky scads-to-load right-rail__item --><p class="html-slice"> These descriptive names eventually became associated with a family, inherited, or passed down, from one generation to the next. This was the origin of many of our current surnames.</p><p>While they came into use in the eleventh century, hereditary surnames were not commonplace in England prior to the era of the sixteenth century Reformation. It is conjectured that the introduction of parish registers in 1538 was a great influence in this, as a person entered under one surname at baptism would not be likely to be married under another name, and buried under a third. Some areas of England came later to <a data-inlink="P9xEyTkVFeT1uqHow-LtuQ&#61;&#61;" href="https://www.thoughtco.com/surname-meanings-and-origins-s2-1422408" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="1">the use of surnames</a>, however. It was not until the late seventeenth century that many families in Yorkshire and Halifax took permanent surnames.</p><p>Surnames in England generally developed from four major sources:</p><h3>Patronymic &amp; Matronymic Surnames</h3><p>These are surnames derived from baptismal or Christian names to indicate family relationship or descent—<em>patronymic</em> derived from the father&#39;s given name and <em>matronymic</em>, meaning derived from the mother&#39;s name.</p><div id="native-placeholder_1-0" class="comp native-placeholder mntl-block"></div><!-- end: comp native-placeholder mntl-block --><p class="html-slice"> Some baptismal or given names have become surnames without any change in form (a son took his father&#39;s given name as his surname). Others added an ending such as <em>-s</em> (more common in the South and West of England) or <em>-son</em> (preferred in the northern half of England) to his father&#39;s name. The latter <em>-son</em> suffix was also sometimes added to the mother&#39;s name.</p><div id="billboard2-sticky_1-0" class="comp billboard2-sticky billboard-sticky scads-to-load right-rail__item" data-height="600" data-parent><div class="spacer"><div id="billboard2-dynamic_1-0" class="comp billboard2-dynamic mntl-gpt-dynamic-adunit mntl-gpt-adunit gpt billboard dynamic"><div id="billboard2" class="wrapper" data-type="billboard" data-pos="btf1" data-priority="3" data-sizes="[[300, 250], [300, 600], [300, 601], [160, 600], [300, 251], [2, 1], 'fluid']" data-rtb="true" data-targeting="null"></div></div><!-- end: comp billboard2-dynamic mntl-gpt-dynamic-adunit mntl-gpt-adunit gpt billboard dynamic --></div></div><!-- end: comp billboard2-sticky billboard-sticky scads-to-load right-rail__item --><p class="html-slice"> English surnames ending in <em>-ing</em> (from the British <em>engi</em>, &#34;to bring forth,&#34; and <em>-kin</em> generally indicate a patronymic or family name as well.<br/><strong>Examples: </strong><a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/wilson-name-meaning-and-origin-1422646" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="2">Wilson</a> (son of Will), <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/rogers-name-meaning-and-origin-1422605" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="3">Rogers</a> (son of Roger), Benson (son of Ben), Madison (son/daughter of Maud), Marriott (son/daughter of Mary), Hilliard (son/daughter of Hildegard).</p><h3>Occupational Surnames</h3><p>Many English surnames developed from a person&#39;s job, trade or position in society. Three common English surnames—<a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/smith-name-meaning-and-origin-1422624" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="4">Smith</a>, <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/wright-last-name-meaning-and-origin-1422648" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="5">Wright</a> and <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/taylor-name-meaning-and-origin-1422630" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="6">Taylor</a>–are excellent examples of this. A name ending in <em>-man</em> or <em>-er</em> usually implies such a trade name, as in Chapman (shopkeeper), Barker (tanner) and Fiddler. On occasion a rare occupational name can provide a clue to the family&#39;s origin. For example, Dymond (dairymen) are commonly from Devon, and Arkwright (maker of arks or chests) are generally from Lancashire.</p><h3>Descriptive Surnames </h3><p>Based on a unique quality or physical characteristic of the individual, descriptive surnames often developed from nicknames or pet names. Most refer to an individual&#39;s appearance - size, color, complexion, or physical shape (<a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/little-surname-meaning-and-origin-4076696" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="7">Little</a>, <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/white-name-meaning-and-origin-1422643" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="8">White</a>, Armstrong). A descriptive surname may also refer to an individual&#39;s personal or moral characteristics, such as Goodchild, Puttock (greedy) or Wise.</p><h3>Geographical or Local Surnames </h3><p>These are names derived from the location of the homestead from which the first bearer and his family lived, and are generally the most common origin of English surnames. They were first introduced into England by the Normans, many of whom were known by the name of their personal estate. Thus, many English surnames derive from the name of an actual town, county, or estate where an individual lived, worked, or owned land. County names in Great Britain, such as Cheshire, Kent and Devon have been commonly adopted as surnames. A second class of local surnames derived from cities and towns, such as Hertford, Carlisle and Oxford. Other local surnames derive from descriptive landscape features such as hills, woods, and streams which describe the original bearer&#39;s residence.</p><p class="html-slice"> This is the origin of surnames such as <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/hill-last-name-meaning-and-origin-1422528" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="9">Hill</a>, <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/bush-surname-meaning-and-origin-3862445" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="10">Bush</a>, <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/ford-surname-meaning-and-origin-4068476" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="11">Ford</a>, Sykes (marshy stream) and Atwood (near a wood). Surnames which begin with the prefix <em>At-</em> can especially be attributed as a name with local origins. <em>By-</em> was also sometimes used as a prefix for local names.<br/> </p><p><strong>TOP
