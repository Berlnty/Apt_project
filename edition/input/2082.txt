research.<p>A US study showed women of reproductive age are far better at identifying  odours than men after repeated exposure to the source of the smell.<p>Researchers found female subjects whose odour sensitivity was tested many times, were able to detect the cherry-almond smell of benzaldehyde and a few other odours at progressively much lower concentrations.<p>Male subjects taking part in similar tests never improved their ability to identify odours with experience.<p></font><table bgcolor="#FFFFCC" class="floatingtable" hspace="2" vspace="2" cellspacing="0" width="150" border="0" second test alongside six new subjects.<p>They used benzaldehyde again and a second substance, isoamyl acetate, which smells like bananas.<p>Sensitivity to benzaldehyde increased substantially for both the experienced women and the new "naive" group, but not for men.<p>However this was not repeated with isoamyl acetate.<p>Scientists at Monell Chemical Senses Center concluded the effect of repeated testing of odours was replicable but odour specific.<p>The tests were then carried out in young girls and post menopausal women to assess whether hormones played a part.<p>An equal number of
