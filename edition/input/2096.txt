<tr>      <td align="center">		<p align="left"><font face="Arial" size="2"><span lang="en-us">&nbsp;</span>Similar appearance of      children<br>        <span lang="en-us">&nbsp;</span>from the same      region of Asia<br>		<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;due to a shared		<a href="glossary.htm#gene_pool">gene pool</a></span></font></td>    </tr>    <tr>      <td align="center" height="5"></td>    </tr>  </table>	<p><font face="Arial" size="1"><br>	</font><font size="3" face="Arial">With the  exception of <a href="glossary.htm#monozygotic_twins">monozygotic twins</a>, every one of  us is genetically different from every other human who ever lived.&nbsp; Each of us is  unique in terms of the combination of tens of thousands of genetically determined  characteristics that we possess.&nbsp; However,<span lang="en-us"> we clearly 	have some traits in common with other people.&nbsp; Most of us have readily 	identifiable male or female sexual characteristics which we share with 	others of our gender.&nbsp; P</span>eople who are closely related to each  other usually have <span lang="en-us">even greater </span>similar<span lang="en-us">ity 	in</span> appearance because much of their genetic makeup is  shared.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; Unrelated p</span>eople   whose ancestors came from the same part of the world often are generally similar in terms of  such body features as skin and hair color, facial characteristics, body shape, and  stature. &nbsp;Not surprisingly, these traits have a strong genetic  component<span lang="en-us"> as well</span>.&nbsp;  However, they can be affected by environmental influences.&nbsp; For instance, skin color  often can be darkened, or tanned, seasonally by prolonged exposure to <a href="glossary.htm#ultraviolet_radiation">ultraviolet radiation</a> from the sun.&nbsp;   Likewise, stature can be affected by  nutrition.&nbsp; When young children do   not receive sufficient calories in their diet, especially protein, their   growth is likely to be stunted<span lang="en-us">--t</span>hey will not reach their full   genetically programmed height.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">Humans like to classify and 	use identity labels for people and things with which we come in contact.&nbsp; 	It satisfies our apparent need for a sense of order.&nbsp; In addition to 	gender and age, m</span>ost of us readily <span lang="en-us">classify</span> each other into 	<span lang="en-us">distinct </span>categories on the basis of what we consider to be  races.&nbsp; In North America, people usually think in terms of  Black, White, Asian, Hispanic or Latino, and <span lang="en-us">Indian or	</span>Native American.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; These</span> are all 	archaic concepts of physical types that have little biological reality<span lang="en-us">.</span>&nbsp;	<span lang="en-us">Academics</span> may use more  sophisticated <span lang="en-us">sounding </span>terms for these perceived biological groupings, such as Negroid <a href="sounds/negroid.mp3">  <img src="/web/20121105101522im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/vary/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>, Caucasoid <a href="sounds/caucasoid.mp3">  <img src="/web/20121105101522im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/vary/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a><span lang="en-us"> or</span> Caucasian <a href="sounds/caucasian.mp3">  <img src="/web/20121105101522im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/vary/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>, and Mongoloid <a href="sounds/mongoloid.mp3">  <img src="/web/20121105101522im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/vary/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>. &nbsp; Nevertheless, they   are<span lang="en-us"> still</span> bad science.&nbsp; <span lang="en-us">H</span>owever,	<span lang="en-us">they are important to contemporary life in North America because they </span>reflect<span lang="en-us">	</span>culturally <span lang="en-us">defined</span> <span lang="en-us">	differences in our society</span>.&nbsp;   They  are<span lang="en-us"> essentially</span> <span lang="en-us">labels</span> 	<span lang="en-us">of ethnicity </span> 	that are used for categorizing and discriminating.</font></p>	<p><font size="3" face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">We now know that clearly distinct human 	biological races do not now exist.&nbsp; 	This</span> does not  mean that our species is lacking <a href="glossary.htm#anatomy">anatomical</a> and <a href="glossary.htm#physiology">physiological</a> variation<span lang="en-us"> 	between populations</span>.&nbsp; Rather, the true nature  of that variation is far more complex.&nbsp; It does not   correspond to commonly believed simple racial lines.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">The physical  traits that we think of as clustering together among particular peoples often have much  broader distributions. &nbsp;They continue well outside of the geographic areas in which a  &quot;race&quot; is stereotypically supposed to exist.&nbsp; For instance, dark brown skin  is usually thought of as the key trait in distinguishing  <a href="glossary.htm#sub-Saharan_Africa">sub-Saharan Africans</a> from people   elsewhere in the world.&nbsp; However, dark   brown skin is also found in <span lang="en-us">parts of </span>southern Asia, Australia, New Guinea and on<span lang="en-us"> 	the</span> nearby  islands of <a href="glossary.htm#Melanesia">Melanesia</a>.</font></p>  <div align="center"><center>    <table border="0" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: collapse" Their skin coloration is largely due to <a href="glossary.htm#natural_selection">natural selection</a> rather than recent shared  descent.&nbsp; The environmental factors that led to dark brown skin among Africans   apparently led to  it elsewhere as well.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">Genetically inherited traits  often have a <b>clinal distribution</b>.&nbsp; That is to say there  is a continuous, progressive gradation moving  from one geographic region to another.&nbsp; The 	<a href="glossary.htm#frequency">frequency</a> of  yellow-brown hair among  Australian Aborigines illustrates this trend (as shown by the map<span lang="en-us"> 	on the left</span> below).&nbsp; This  trait generally becomes more common with distance from the coast.&nbsp; Such patterns can  result when selective pressures differ from one region to another   and when people mate mostly with their immediate neighbors.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;	</span>Selective pressures<span lang="en-us"> favoring or discriminating against a trait</span> may come from several sources.&nbsp; There may be 	natural selection resulting from environmental constraints.&nbsp; At the 	same time, there also may be patterns of culturally defined discriminatory 	mate selection that vary from region to region.</font></p>  <div align="center"><center>    <table border="0" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111">    <tr>      <td align="center"><font size="3" face="Arial">      <img src="/web/20121105101522im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/vary/images/map_of_Australian_Abo_hair_color.gif" alt="map of Australia showing the progressive distribution of yellow-brown hair color among Australian Aborigines--the highest frequenies of this color hair are in west central Australia" width="422" height="245"></font></td>      <td width="10" align="center" rowspan="2">&nbsp;</td>      <td align="center"><font size="5" face="Times New Roman"><strong>      <img src="/web/20121105101522im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/vary/images/map_of_red_hair_in_England.gif" alt="map of most of Britain showing the frequency of people with red hair in England and Scotland--the highest frequencies of red hair are in Wales and southern Scotland" hspace="0" width="279" height="243"></strong></font></td>    </tr>    <tr>      <td align="center"><font face="Arial" size="2">Clinal distribution of hair 		color among Australian Aborigines</font></td>      <td align="center"><font size="2" face="Arial">Discontinuous distribution 		of red hair in </font><span lang="en-us">      <font size="2" face="Arial">Britain</font></span></td>    </tr>  </table>  </center></div></blockquote><blockquote>  <p align="left"><font size="3" face="Arial">Sometimes, the  distribution of genetically inherited traits does not follow a pattern of gradual change  from one geographic region to another but has a <font color="#333333"><b>discontinuous distribution</b></font>.&nbsp;  The frequency of red hair in Britain illustrates this sort of pattern.&nbsp; Note in the  map on the right<span lang="en-us"> above</span> that there are several relatively isolated pockets where there is a high  frequency of people with red hair.&nbsp;
