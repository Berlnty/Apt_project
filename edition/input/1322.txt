class="topic-identifier">mathematics</div><div class="md-byline">	<div class="written-by">			<span>Written By: </span>			<ul>				<li><a href="/contributor/JL-Heilbron/4434">J.L. Heilbron</a></li>				</ul>			</div>	<div class="last-updated hidden-xs">			<a data-type="accordion-link" href="#accordion-article-history" rel="nofollow">See Article History</a>			</div>	</div><div class="grid">				<div class="grid-sm">					<article id="article-content" class="content"><section id="ref1" data-level="1"><p><strong><span id="ref726375"></span>Geometry</strong>,  the branch of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">mathematics</a> concerned with the shape of individual objects, spatial relationships among various objects, and the properties of surrounding <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/space-physics-and-metaphysics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">space</a>. It is one of the oldest branches of mathematics, having arisen in response to such practical problems as those found in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/surveying" class="md-crosslink autoxref">surveying</a>, and its name is derived from Greek words meaning “Earth measurement.” Eventually it was realized that geometry need not be limited to the study of flat surfaces (plane geometry) and rigid three-dimensional objects (solid geometry) but that even the most abstract thoughts and images might be represented and developed in geometric terms.</p><!--[P1]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="video" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="208727"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="video"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/76/186376-049-E6B35938.jpg" alt="deformation; geometry" data-width="426" data-height="240"></div></div><figcaption><strong class="md-assembly-title">deformation; geometry</strong><span class="md-assembly-caption">Exploring how civil and environmental engineers use geometry to study processes of deformation in projects of various scales.</span><cite>© Massachusetts Institute of Technology</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML1]--><p>This article begins with a brief guidepost to the major branches of geometry and then proceeds to an extensive historical treatment. For information on specific branches of geometry, <em>see</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Euclidean-geometry" class="md-crosslink">Euclidean geometry</a>, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/analytic-geometry" class="md-crosslink">analytic geometry</a>, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/projective-geometry" class="md-crosslink">projective geometry</a>, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/differential-geometry" class="md-crosslink">differential geometry</a>, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/non-Euclidean-geometry" class="md-crosslink">non-Euclidean geometries</a>, and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/topology" class="md-crosslink">topology</a>.</p><!--[P2]--></section><div class="major-reference-upsell">			<iframe src="/GPTIframe/ajax/sync/EB_HOUSE_DESKTOP" scrolling="no" width="640" height="106"></iframe>		</div><section id="ref217464" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">Major branches of geometry</h2><section id="ref217465" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref726376"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Euclidean-geometry" class="md-crosslink">Euclidean geometry</a></h2><p>In several ancient <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="cultures">cultures</a> there developed a form of geometry suited to the relationships between lengths, areas, and volumes of physical objects. This geometry was codified in Euclid’s <em>Elements</em> about 300 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span> on the basis of 10 axioms, or postulates, from which several hundred theorems were proved by deductive logic. The <em>Elements</em> epitomized the axiomatic-deductive method for many centuries.</p><!--[P3]--></section><section id="ref217466" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref726377"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/analytic-geometry" class="md-crosslink">Analytic geometry</a></h2><p><a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Analytic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="Analytic">Analytic</a> geometry was initiated by the French mathematician <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rene-Descartes" class="md-crosslink">René Descartes</a> (1596–1650), who introduced rectangular <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/coordinate-system" class="md-crosslink autoxref">coordinates</a> to locate points and to enable lines and curves to be represented with algebraic equations. Algebraic geometry is a modern extension of the subject to multidimensional and non-Euclidean spaces.</p><!--[P4]--></section><section id="ref217467" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref726378"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/projective-geometry" class="md-crosslink">Projective geometry</a></h2><p>Projective geometry originated with the French mathematician <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Girard-Desargues" class="md-crosslink">Girard Desargues</a> (1591–1661) to deal with those properties of geometric figures that are not altered by projecting their image, or “shadow,” onto another <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/surface-geometry" class="md-crosslink autoxref">surface</a>.</p><!--[P5]--></section><section id="ref217468" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref726379"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/differential-geometry" class="md-crosslink">Differential geometry</a></h2><p>The German mathematician <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Carl-Friedrich-Gauss" class="md-crosslink">Carl Friedrich Gauss</a> (1777–1855), in connection with practical problems of surveying and geodesy, initiated the field of differential geometry. Using <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/differential-calculus" class="md-crosslink autoxref">differential calculus</a>, he characterized the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intrinsic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="intrinsic">intrinsic</a> properties of curves and surfaces. For instance, he showed that the intrinsic <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/curvature" class="md-crosslink autoxref">curvature</a> of a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/cylinder-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">cylinder</a> is the same as that of a plane, as can be seen by cutting a cylinder along its axis and flattening, but not the same as that of a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/sphere" class="md-crosslink autoxref">sphere</a>, which cannot be flattened without distortion.</p><!--[P6]--></section><section id="ref217469" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2"><span id="ref726380"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/non-Euclidean-geometry" class="md-crosslink">Non-Euclidean geometries</a></h2><p>Beginning in the 19th century, various mathematicians substituted <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternatives" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="alternatives">alternatives</a> to Euclid’s <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/parallel-postulate" class="md-crosslink autoxref">parallel postulate</a>, which, in its modern form, reads, “given a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/line-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">line</a> and a point not on the line, it is possible to draw exactly one line through the given point parallel to the line.” They hoped to show that the alternatives were logically impossible. Instead, they discovered that consistent non-Euclidean geometries exist.</p><!--[P7]--></section><section id="ref217470" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Topology</h2><p>Topology, the youngest and most sophisticated branch of geometry, focuses on the properties of geometric objects that remain unchanged upon continuous deformation—shrinking, stretching, and folding, but not tearing. The continuous development of topology dates from 1911, when the Dutch mathematician <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Luitzen-Egbertus-Jan-Brouwer" class="md-crosslink">L.E.J. Brouwer</a> (1881–1966) introduced methods generally applicable to the topic.</p><!--[P8]--></section></section><!--[H3]--><section id="ref217471" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">History of geometry</h2><p>The earliest known unambiguous examples of written records—dating from Egypt and Mesopotamia about 3100 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>—demonstrate that ancient peoples had already begun to devise mathematical rules and techniques useful for surveying land areas, constructing buildings, and measuring storage containers. Beginning about the 6th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>, the Greeks gathered and extended this practical knowledge and from it generalized the abstract subject now known as geometry, from the combination of the Greek words <em>geo</em> (“Earth”) and <em>metron</em> (“measure”) for the measurement of the Earth.</p><!--[P9]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57444"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/61/67361-004-46CBD13A.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/61/67361-004-46CBD13A.jpg" alt="Mathematicians of the Greco-Roman worldThis map spans a millennium of prominent Greco-Roman mathematicians, from Thales of Miletus (c. 600 bc) to Hypatia of Alexandria (c. ad 400). Their names—located on the map under their cities of birth—can be clicked to access their biographies.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML9]--><p>In addition to describing some of the achievements of the ancient Greeks, notably Euclid’s logical development of geometry in the <em>Elements</em>, this article examines some applications of geometry to <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomy" class="md-crosslink autoxref">astronomy</a>, cartography, and painting from classical Greece through <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="medieval">medieval</a> Islam and Renaissance Europe. It concludes with a brief discussion of extensions to non-Euclidean and multidimensional geometries in the modern age.</p><!--[P10]--><section id="ref217472" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Ancient geometry: practical and empirical</h2><p>The origin of geometry lies in the concerns of everyday life. The traditional account, preserved in Herodotus’s <em>History</em> (5th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>), credits the Egyptians with inventing surveying in order to reestablish property values after the annual flood of the Nile. Similarly, eagerness to know the volumes of solid figures derived from the need to evaluate tribute, store oil and grain, and build dams and pyramids. Even the three <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/abstruse" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="abstruse">abstruse</a> geometrical problems of ancient times—to double a cube, trisect an angle, and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/square-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">square</a> a circle, all of which will be discussed later—probably arose from practical matters, from religious ritual, timekeeping, and construction, respectively, in pre-Greek societies of the Mediterranean. And the main subject of later Greek geometry, the theory of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/conic-section" class="md-crosslink">conic sections</a>, owed its general importance, and perhaps also its origin, to its application to optics and astronomy.</p><!--[P11]--><p>While many ancient individuals, known and unknown, contributed to the subject, none equaled the impact of Euclid and his <em><span id="ref726398"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Elements-by-Euclid" class="md-crosslink">Elements</a></em> of geometry, a book now 2,300 years old and the object of as much painful and painstaking study as the Bible. Much less is known about <span id="ref726397"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Euclid-Greek-mathematician" class="md-crosslink">Euclid</a>, however, than about Moses. In fact, the only thing known with a fair degree of confidence is that Euclid taught at the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Library-of-Alexandria" class="md-crosslink">Library of Alexandria</a> during the reign of Ptolemy I (323–285/283 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>). Euclid wrote not only on geometry but also on astronomy and optics and perhaps also on mechanics and music. Only the <em>Elements</em>, which was extensively copied and translated, has survived intact.</p><!--[P12]--><p>Euclid’s <em>Elements</em> was so complete and clearly written that it literally obliterated the work of his predecessors. What is known about Greek geometry before him comes primarily from bits quoted by Plato and Aristotle and by later mathematicians and commentators. Among other <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/precious" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="precious">precious</a> items they preserved are some results and the general approach of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pythagoras" class="md-crosslink">Pythagoras</a> (<em>c.</em> 580–<em>c.</em> 500 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>) and his followers. The <span id="ref726399"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Pythagoreanism" class="md-crosslink">Pythagoreans</a> convinced themselves that all things are, or owe their relationships to, numbers. The doctrine gave mathematics supreme importance in the investigation and understanding of the world. Plato developed a similar view, and philosophers influenced by Pythagoras or Plato often wrote ecstatically about geometry as the key to the interpretation of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/universe" class="md-crosslink autoxref">universe</a>. Thus ancient geometry gained an association with the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/sublime" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="sublime">sublime</a> to complement its earthy origins and its reputation as the exemplar of precise reasoning.</p><!--[P13]--><span id="ref217472" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217473" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Finding the <span id="ref726435"></span>right angle</h2><p>Ancient builders and surveyors needed to be able to construct right angles in the field on demand. The method employed by the Egyptians earned them the name “rope pullers” in Greece, apparently because they employed a <span id="ref726436"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/rope" class="md-crosslink">rope</a> for laying out their construction guidelines. One way that they could have employed a rope to construct right triangles was to mark a looped rope with knots so that, when held at the knots and pulled tight, the rope must form a right triangle. The simplest way to perform the trick is to take a rope that is 12 units long, make a knot 3 units from one end and another 5 units from the other end, and then knot the ends together to form a loop, as shown in the animation. However, the Egyptian scribes have not left us instructions about these procedures, much less any hint that they knew how to generalize them to obtain the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Euclids-Windmill-1688351" class="md-crosslink">Pythagorean theorem</a>: the square on the line opposite the right angle equals the sum of the squares on the other two sides. Similarly, the Vedic scriptures of ancient India contain sections called <em>sulvasutra</em>s, or “rules of the rope,” for the exact positioning of sacrificial altars. The required right angles were made by ropes marked to give the triads (3, 4, 5) and (5, 12, 13).</p><!--[P14]--><p>In Babylonian clay tablets (<em>c.</em> 1700–1500 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>) modern historians have discovered problems whose solutions indicate that the Pythagorean theorem and some special triads were known more than a thousand years before Euclid. A right triangle made at random, however, is very unlikely to have all its sides measurable by the same unit—that is, every side a whole-number multiple of some common unit of measurement. This fact, which came as a shock when discovered by the Pythagoreans, gave rise to the concept and theory of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Incommensurables-1688515" class="md-crosslink">incommensurability</a>.</p><!--[P15]--></section><span id="ref217472" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217474" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Locating the inaccessible</h2><p>By ancient tradition, <span id="ref726455"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thales-of-Miletus" class="md-crosslink">Thales of Miletus</a>, who lived before Pythagoras in the 6th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>, invented a way to measure inaccessible heights, such as the Egyptian pyramids. Although none of his writings survives, Thales may well have known about a Babylonian observation that for similar triangles (triangles having the same shape but not necessarily the same size) the length of each corresponding side is increased (or decreased) by the same multiple. A determination of the height of a tower using similar triangles is demonstrated in the figure. The ancient Chinese arrived at measures of inaccessible heights and distances by another route, using “complementary” rectangles, as seen in the next <span data-assembly-id="57039" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>, which can be shown to give results equivalent to those of the Greek method involving triangles.</p><!--[P16]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57039"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/41/66641-004-0A9B7581.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/41/66641-004-0A9B7581.jpg" alt="A comparison of a Chinese and a Greek geometric theoremThe figure illustrates the equivalence of the Chinese complementary rectangles theorem and the Greek similar triangles theorem." data-width="360" data-height="386"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">A comparison of a Chinese and a Greek geometric theoremThe figure illustrates the equivalence of the Chinese complementary rectangles theorem and the Greek similar triangles theorem.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML16]--></section><span id="ref217472" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217475" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Estimating the wealth</h2><p>A Babylonian cuneiform tablet written some 3,500 years ago treats problems about dams, wells, water clocks, and excavations. It also has an exercise on circular enclosures with an implied value of π = 3. The contractor for King Solomon’s swimming pool, who made a pond 10 cubits across and 30 cubits around (1 Kings 7:23), used the same value. However, the Hebrews should have taken their π from the Egyptians before crossing the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Red-Sea" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Red Sea</a>, for the <span id="ref726456"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rhind-papyrus" class="md-crosslink">Rhind papyrus</a> (<em>c.</em> 2000 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>; our principal source for ancient Egyptian mathematics) implies π = 3.1605.</p><!--[P17]--><p>Knowledge of the area of a circle was of practical value to the officials who kept track of the pharaoh’s tribute as well as to the builders of altars and swimming pools. <span id="ref726457"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ahmes" class="md-crosslink">Ahmes</a>, the scribe who copied and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/annotated" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="annotated">annotated</a> the Rhind papyrus (<em>c.</em> 1650 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>), has much to say about cylindrical granaries and pyramids, whole and truncated. He could calculate their volumes, and, as appears from his taking the Egyptian <em>seked</em>, the horizontal distance associated with a vertical rise of one cubit, as the defining quantity for the pyramid’s slope, he knew something about similar triangles.</p><!--[P18]--></section></section><section id="ref217476" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Ancient geometry: abstract and applied</h2><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217477" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">The three classical problems</h2><p>In addition to proving mathematical theorems, ancient mathematicians constructed various geometrical objects. Euclid arbitrarily restricted the tools of construction to a straightedge (an unmarked ruler) and a compass. The restriction made three problems of particular interest (to double a cube, to trisect an arbitrary angle, and to square a circle) very difficult—in fact, impossible. Various methods of construction using other means were devised in the classical period, and efforts, always unsuccessful, using straightedge and compass persisted for the next 2,000 years. In 1837 the French mathematician Pierre Laurent Wantzel proved that doubling the cube and trisecting the angle are impossible, and in 1880 the German mathematician <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ferdinand-von-Lindemann" class="md-crosslink">Ferdinand von Lindemann</a> showed that squaring the circle is impossible, as a consequence of his <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/proof-logic" class="md-crosslink autoxref">proof</a> that <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/pi-mathematics" class="md-crosslink">π is a transcendental number</a>.</p><!--[P19]--><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217478" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Doubling the <span id="ref726458"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/cube-mathematics" class="md-crosslink">cube</a></h2><p>The Vedic scriptures made the cube the most advisable form of altar for anyone who wanted to supplicate in the same place twice. The rules of ritual required that the altar for the second plea have the same shape but twice the volume of the first. If the sides of the original and derived altars are <em>a</em> and <em>b</em>, respectively, then <em>b</em><sup>3</sup> = 2<em>a</em><sup>3</sup>. The problem came to the Greeks together with its ceremonial content. An oracle disclosed that the citizens of Delos could free themselves of a plague merely by replacing an existing altar by one twice its size. The Delians applied to Plato. He replied that the oracle did not mean that the gods wanted a larger altar but that they had intended “to shame the Greeks for their neglect of mathematics and their <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/contempt" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="contempt">contempt</a> for geometry.” With this blend of Vedic practice, Greek <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/myth" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="myth">myth</a>, and academic manipulation, the problem of the duplication of the cube took a leading place in the formation of Greek geometry.</p><!--[P20]--><p><span id="ref726475"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hippocrates-of-Chios" class="md-crosslink">Hippocrates of Chios</a>, who wrote an early <em>Elements</em> about 450 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>, took the first steps in cracking the altar problem. He reduced the duplication to finding two mean proportionals between 1 and 2, that is, to finding lines <em>x</em> and <em>y</em> in the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/ratio" class="md-crosslink autoxref">ratio</a> 1:<em>x</em> = <em>x</em>:<em>y</em> = <em>y</em>:2. After the intervention of the Delian oracle, several geometers around Plato’s Academy found complicated ways of generating mean proportionals.</p><!--[P21]--><p>A few generations later, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Eratosthenes" class="md-crosslink">Eratosthenes of Cyrene</a> (<em>c.</em> 276–<em>c.</em> 194 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>) devised a simple instrument with moving parts that could produce approximate mean proportionals.</p><!--[P22]--></section><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217479" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Trisecting the <span id="ref726495"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/angle-mathematics" class="md-crosslink">angle</a></h2><p>The Egyptians told time at night by the rising of 12 asterisms (constellations), each requiring on average two hours to rise. In order to obtain more convenient intervals, the Egyptians subdivided each of their asterisms into three parts, or decans. That presented the problem of trisection. It is not known whether the second celebrated problem of <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/archaic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="archaic">archaic</a> Greek geometry, the trisection of any given angle, arose from the difficulty of the decan, but it is likely that it came from some problem in angular measure.</p><!--[P23]--><p>Several geometers of Plato’s time tried their hands at trisection. Although no one succeeded in finding a solution with straightedge and compass, they did succeed with a mechanical device and by a trick. The mechanical device, perhaps never built, creates what the ancient geometers called a <span id="ref726496"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/quadratrix-of-Hippias" class="md-crosslink">quadratrix</a>. Invented by a geometer known as <span id="ref726497"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Hippias-of-Elis" class="md-crosslink">Hippias of Elis</a> (flourished 5th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>), the quadratrix is a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/curve" class="md-crosslink autoxref">curve</a> traced by the point of intersection between two moving lines, one rotating uniformly through a right angle, the other gliding uniformly parallel to itself. (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Trisecting-the-Angle-The-Quadratrix-of-Hippias-1688521" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: Trisecting the Angle: The Quadratrix of Hippias</a>.)</p><!--[P24]--><p>The trick for trisection is an application of what the Greeks called <em><span id="ref726498"></span>neusis</em>, a maneuvering of a measured length into a special position to complete a geometrical figure. A late version of its use, ascribed to <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Archimedes" class="md-crosslink">Archimedes</a> (<em>c.</em> 285–212/211 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>), exemplifies the method of angle trisection. (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Trisecting-the-Angle-Archimedes-Method-724632" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: Trisecting the Angle: Archimedes’ Method</a>.)</p><!--[P25]--></section><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217480" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Squaring the circle</h2><p>The pre-Euclidean Greek geometers transformed the practical problem of determining the area of a <span id="ref726499"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/circle-mathematics" class="md-crosslink">circle</a> into a tool of discovery. Three approaches can be distinguished: Hippocrates’ dodge of substituting one problem for another; the application of a mechanical instrument, as in Hippias’s device for trisecting the angle; and the technique that proved the most fruitful, the closer and closer approximation to an unknown magnitude difficult to study (e.g., the area of a circle) by a series of known magnitudes easier to study (e.g., areas of polygons)—a technique known in modern times as the “<span id="ref726538"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/method-of-exhaustion" class="md-crosslink">method of exhaustion</a>” and attributed by its greatest practitioner, Archimedes, to Plato’s student <span id="ref726541"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Eudoxus-of-Cnidus" class="md-crosslink">Eudoxus of Cnidus</a> (<em>c.</em> 408–<em>c.</em> 355 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>).</p><!--[P26]--><p>While not able to square the circle, Hippocrates did demonstrate the quadratures of lunes; that is, he showed that the area between two intersecting circular arcs could be expressed exactly as a rectilinear area and so raised the expectation that the circle itself could be treated similarly. (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Quadrature-of-the-Lune-1688524" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: Quadrature of the Lune</a>.) A contemporary of Hippias’s discovered that the quadratrix could be used to almost rectify circles. These were the substitution and mechanical approaches.</p><!--[P27]--><p>The method of exhaustion as developed by Eudoxus approximates a curve or surface by using polygons with calculable perimeters and areas. As the number of sides of a regular <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/polygon-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">polygon</a> inscribed in a circle increases indefinitely, its perimeter and area “exhaust,” or take up, the circumference and area of the circle to within any assignable <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/error-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">error</a> of length or area, however small. In Archimedes’ usage, the method of exhaustion produced upper and lower bounds for the value of π, the ratio of any circle’s circumference to its diameter. This he accomplished by inscribing a polygon within a circle, and circumscribing a polygon around it as well, thereby bounding the circle’s circumference between the polygons’ calculable perimeters. He used polygons with 96 sides and thus bound π between 3<span class="md-fraction md-fraction-oblique"><span><sup>10</sup>/<sub>71</sub></span></span> and 3<span class="md-fraction md-fraction-oblique"><span><sup>1</sup>/<sub>7</sub></span></span>. (<em>See</em> animation.)</p><!--[P28]--></section></section><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217481" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Idealization and proof</h2><p>The last great Platonist and Euclidean commentator of antiquity, <span id="ref726555"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Proclus" class="md-crosslink">Proclus</a> (<em>c.</em> 410–485 <span class="text-smallcaps">ce</span>), attributed to the inexhaustible Thales the discovery of the far-from-obvious proposition that even apparently obvious propositions need proof. Proclus referred especially to the theorem, known in the Middle Ages as the Bridge of Asses, that in an isosceles triangle the angles opposite the equal sides are equal. The theorem may have earned its nickname from the Euclidean figure or from the commonsense notion that only an ass would require proof of so obvious a statement. (<em>See</em> <span id="ref726556"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/The-Bridge-of-Asses" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: The Bridge of Asses</a>.)</p><!--[P29]--><p>The ancient Greek geometers soon followed Thales over the Bridge of Asses. In the 5th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span> the philosopher-mathematician <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Democritus" class="md-crosslink">Democritus</a> (<em>c.</em> 460–<em>c.</em> 370 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>) declared that his geometry excelled all the knowledge of the Egyptian rope pullers because he could prove what he claimed. By the time of Plato, geometers customarily proved their propositions. Their compulsion and the multiplication of theorems it produced fit perfectly with the endless questioning of Socrates and the uncompromising logic of Aristotle. Perhaps the origin, and certainly the exercise, of the peculiarly Greek method of mathematical proof should be sought in the same social setting that gave rise to the practice of philosophy—that is, the Greek polis. There citizens learned the skills of a governing class, and the wealthier among them enjoyed the leisure to engage their minds as they pleased, however useless the result, while slaves attended to the necessities of life. Greek society could support the transformation of geometry from a practical art to a deductive science. Despite its rigour, however, Greek geometry does not satisfy the demands of the modern systematist. Euclid himself sometimes appeals to <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/inferences" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="inferences">inferences</a> drawn from an intuitive grasp of concepts such as point and line or inside and outside, uses superposition, and so on. It took more than 2,000 years to purge the <em>Elements</em> of what pure deductivists deemed imperfections.</p><!--[P30]--></section><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217482" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">The Euclidean synthesis</h2><p>Euclid, in keeping with the self-conscious logic of Aristotle, began the first of his 13 books of the <em>Elements</em> with sets of definitions (“a line is breadthless length”), common notions (“the whole is greater than the part”), and axioms, or postulates (“all right angles are equal”). Of this preliminary matter, the fifth and last postulate, which states a sufficient condition that two straight lines meet if sufficiently extended, has received by far the greatest attention. In effect it defines parallelism. Many later geometers tried to prove the fifth postulate using other parts of the <em>Elements</em>. Euclid saw farther, for <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/coherent" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="coherent">coherent</a> geometries (known as <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/non-Euclidean-geometry" class="md-crosslink">non-Euclidean geometries</a>) can be produced by replacing the fifth postulate with other postulates that contradict Euclid’s choice.</p><!--[P31]--><p>The first six books contain most of what Euclid delivers about plane geometry. Book I presents many propositions doubtless discovered by his predecessors, from Thales’ equality of the angles opposite the equal sides of an isosceles triangle to the Pythagorean theorem, with which the book effectively ends. (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Euclids-Windmill-1688351" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: Euclid’s Windmill</a>.)</p><!--[P32]--><p>Book VI applies the theory of proportion from Book V to similar figures and presents the geometrical solution to quadratic equations. As usual, some of it is older than Euclid. Books VII–X, which concern various sorts of numbers, especially primes, and various sorts of ratios, are seldom studied now, despite the importance of the masterful Book X, with its elaborate classification of incommensurable magnitudes, to the later development of Greek geometry. (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Incommensurables-1688515" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: Incommensurables</a>.)</p><!--[P33]--><p>Books XI–XIII deal with solids: XI contains theorems about the intersection of planes and of lines and planes and theorems about the volumes of parallelepipeds (solids with parallel parallelograms as opposite faces); XII applies the method of exhaustion introduced by Eudoxus to the volumes of solid figures, including the sphere; XIII, a three-dimensional <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogue" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="analogue">analogue</a> to Book IV, describes the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Platonic-solid" class="md-crosslink">Platonic solids</a>. Among the jewels in Book XII is a proof of the recipe used by the Egyptians for the volume of a pyramid.</p><!--[P34]--></section><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217483" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Gnomonics and the cone</h2><p>During its daily course above the horizon the Sun appears to describe a circular arc. Supplying in his mind’s eye the missing portion of the daily circle, the Greek astronomer could imagine that his real eye was at the apex of a <span id="ref726595"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/cone-mathematics" class="md-crosslink">cone</a>, the surface of which was defined by the Sun’s rays at different times of the day and the base of which was defined by the Sun’s apparent diurnal course. Our astronomer, using the pointer of a sundial, known as a gnomon, as his eye, would generate a second, shadow cone spreading downward. The intersection of this second cone with a horizontal surface, such as the face of a sundial, would give the trace of the Sun’s image (or shadow) during the day as a plane section of a cone. (The possible intersections of a plane with a cone, known as the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/conic-section" class="md-crosslink">conic sections</a>, are the circle, ellipse, point, straight line, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/parabola" class="md-crosslink autoxref">parabola</a>, and hyperbola.)</p><!--[P35]--><p>However, the doxographers ascribe the discovery of conic sections to a student of Eudoxus’s, <span id="ref726596"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Menaechmus" class="md-crosslink">Menaechmus</a> (mid-4th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>), who used them to solve the problem of duplicating the cube. His restricted approach to conics—he worked with only right circular cones and made his sections at right angles to one of the straight lines composing their surfaces—was standard down to Archimedes’ era. Euclid adopted Menaechmus’s approach in his lost book on conics, and Archimedes followed suit. Doubtless, however, both knew that all the conics can be obtained from the same right cone by allowing the section at any angle.</p><!--[P36]--><p>The reason that Euclid’s <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/treatise" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="treatise">treatise</a> on conics perished is that <span id="ref726615"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Apollonius-of-Perga" class="md-crosslink">Apollonius of Perga</a> (<em>c.</em> 262–<em>c.</em> 190 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>) did to it what Euclid had done to the geometry of Plato’s time. Apollonius reproduced known results much more generally and discovered many new properties of the figures. He first proved that all conics are sections of any circular cone, right or oblique. Apollonius introduced the terms <em>ellipse</em>, <em>hyperbola</em>, and <em>parabola</em> for curves produced by intersecting a circular cone with a plane at an angle less than, greater than, and equal to, respectively, the opening angle of the cone.</p><!--[P37]--></section><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217484" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Astronomy and trigonometry</h2><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217485" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Calculation</h2><p>In an inspired use of their geometry, the Greeks did what no earlier people seems to have done: they geometrized the heavens by supposing that the Sun, Moon, and planets move around a stationary Earth on a rotating circle or <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/set-mathematics-and-logic" class="md-crosslink autoxref">set</a> of circles, and they calculated the speed of rotation of these supposititious circles from observed motions. Thus they assigned to the Sun a circle <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/eccentric" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="eccentric">eccentric</a> to the Earth to account for the unequal lengths of the seasons.</p><!--[P38]--><p><span id="ref726635"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ptolemy" class="md-crosslink">Ptolemy</a> (flourished 127–145 <span class="text-smallcaps">ce</span> in Alexandria, Egypt) worked out complete sets of circles for all the planets. In order to account for phenomena arising from the Earth’s motion around the Sun, the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Ptolemaic-system" class="md-crosslink">Ptolemaic system</a> included a secondary circle known as an epicycle, whose centre moved along the path of the primary orbital circle, known as the deferent. Ptolemy’s <em>Great Compilation</em>, or <em><span id="ref726676"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Almagest" class="md-crosslink">Almagest</a></em> after its Arabic translation, was to astronomy what Euclid’s <em>Elements</em> was to geometry. Contrary to the <em>Elements</em>, however, the <em>Almagest</em> <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deploys" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="deploys">deploys</a> geometry for the purpose of calculation. Among the items Ptolemy calculated was a table of chords, which correspond to the trigonometric sine <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/function-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">function</a> later introduced by Indian and Islamic mathematicians. The table of chords assisted the calculation of distances from angular measurements as a modern astronomer might do with the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/law-of-sines" class="md-crosslink autoxref">law of sines</a>.</p><!--[P39]--></section><span id="ref217476" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217486" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Epistemology</h2><p>The application of geometry to astronomy reframed the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/perennial" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="perennial">perennial</a> Greek pursuit of the nature of truth. If a mathematical description fit the facts, as did Ptolemy’s explanation of the unequal lengths of the seasons by the eccentricity of the Sun’s orbit, should the description be taken as true of nature? The answer, with increasing emphasis, was “no.” Astronomers remarked that the eccentric orbit representing the Sun’s annual motion could be replaced by a pair of circles, a deferent centred on the Earth and an epicycle the centre of which moved along the circumference of the deferent. That gave two observationally equivalent solar theories based on two quite different mechanisms. Geometry was too <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/prolific" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="prolific">prolific</a> of alternatives to disclose the true principles of nature. The Greeks, who had raised a sublime science from a pile of practical recipes, discovered that in reversing the process, in reapplying their mathematics to the world, they had no securer claims to truth than the Egyptian rope pullers.</p><!--[P40]--></section></section></section><section id="ref217487" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Ancient geometry: cosmological and metaphysical</h2><span id="ref217487" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217488" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Pythagorean numbers and <span id="ref726767"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Platonic-solid" class="md-crosslink">Platonic solids</a></h2><p>The Pythagoreans used geometrical figures to illustrate their slogan that all is number—thus their “triangular numbers” (<span class="md-fraction md-fraction-oblique"><span><sup><em>n</em>(<em>n</em>−1)</sup>/<sub>2</sub></span></span>), “square numbers” (<em>n</em><sup>2</sup>), and “altar numbers” (<em>n</em><sup>3</sup>), some of which are shown in the <span data-assembly-id="57046" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>. Since the ancients recognized four or five elements at most, Plato sought a small set of uniquely defined geometrical objects to serve as elementary <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituents" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="constituents">constituents</a>. It guided <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johannes-Kepler" class="md-crosslink">Johannes Kepler</a> (1571–1630) to his discovery of the <span id="ref726759"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Keplers-laws-of-planetary-motion" class="md-crosslink">laws of planetary motion</a>. Kepler <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/deployed" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="deployed">deployed</a> the five regular <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/Platonic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="Platonic">Platonic</a> solids not as indicators of the nature and number of the elements but as a model of the structure of the heavens. In 1596 he published <em>Prodromus Dissertationum Mathematicarum Continens Mysterium Cosmographicum</em> (“Cosmographic Mystery”), in which each of the known six planets revolved around the Sun on spheres separated by the five Platonic solids, as shown in the photograph. Although <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Tycho-Brahe-Danish-astronomer" class="md-crosslink">Tycho Brahe</a> (1546–1601), the world’s greatest observational astronomer before the invention of the telescope, rejected the Copernican model of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-system" class="md-crosslink autoxref">solar system</a>, he invited Kepler to assist him at his new observatory outside of Prague. In trying to resolve discrepancies between his original theory and Brahe’s observations, Kepler made the capital discovery that the planets move in ellipses around the Sun as a focus.</p><!--[P42]--></section><span id="ref217487" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217489" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Measuring the Earth and heavens</h2><p>Geometry offered Greek cosmologists not only a way to speculate about the structure of the universe but also the means to measure it. South of Alexandria and roughly on the same meridian of longitude is the village of Syene (modern Aswān), where the Sun stands directly overhead at noon on a midsummer day. At the same moment at Alexandria, the Sun’s rays make an angle α with the tip of a vertical rod, as shown in the <span data-assembly-id="57047" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>. Since the Sun’s rays fall almost parallel on the <span id="ref726775"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth" class="md-crosslink">Earth</a>, the angle subtended by the arc <em>l</em> (representing the distance between Alexandria and Syene) at the centre of the Earth also equals α; thus the ratio of the Earth’s circumference, <em>C</em>, to the distance, <em>l</em>, must equal the ratio of 360° to the angle α—in symbols, <em>C</em>:<em>l</em> = 360°:α. <span id="ref726815"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Eratosthenes" class="md-crosslink">Eratosthenes</a> made the measurements, obtaining a value of about 5,000 stadia for <em>l</em>, which gave a value for the Earth’s circumference of about 250,000 stadia. Because the accepted length of the Greek stadium varied locally, we cannot accurately determine Eratosthenes’ margin of error. However, if we credit the ancient historian Plutarch’s guess at Eratosthenes’ unit of length, we obtain a value for the Earth’s circumference of about 46,250 km—remarkably close to the modern value (about 15 percent too large), considering the difficulty in accurately measuring <em>l</em> and α. (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Measuring-the-Earth-Classical-and-Arabic-1673315" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: Measuring the Earth, Classical and Arabic</a>.)</p><!--[P43]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57047"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/49/66649-004-0C10672E.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/49/66649-004-0C10672E.jpg" alt="Eratosthenes' measurement of the EarthEratosthenes knew that on midsummer day the Sun is directly overhead at Syene, as indicated in the figure by the solar rays illuminating a deep well. Using the Moon as a ruler and noting that the apparent sizes of the Sun and the Moon are about equal, he calculated values for his treatise “On the Sizes and Distances of the Sun and Moon.” The great difficulty of making the observations resulted in an underestimation of the solar distance about 20-fold—he obtained a solar distance, σ, roughly 1,200 times the Earth’s radius, <em>r</em>. Possibly Aristarchus’ inquiry into the relative sizes of the Sun, Moon, and Earth led him to propound the first heliocentric (“Sun-centred”) model of the universe.</p><!--[P44]--><p>Aristarchus’ value for the solar distance was confirmed by an astonishing coincidence. Ptolemy equated the maximum distance of the Moon in its eccentric orbit with the closest approach of Mercury riding on its epicycle; the farthest distance of Mercury with the closest of Venus; and the farthest of Venus with the closest of the Sun. Thus he could compute the solar distance in terms of the lunar distance and thence the terrestrial radius. His answer agreed with that of Aristarchus. The Ptolemaic <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conception" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="conception">conception</a> of the order and machinery of the planets, the most powerful application of Greek geometry to the physical world, thus <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/corroborated" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="corroborated">corroborated</a> the result of direct measurement and established the dimensions of the cosmos for over a thousand years. As the ancient philosophers said, there is no truth in astronomy.</p><!--[P45]--></section></section><section id="ref217490" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">The post-classical period</h2><span id="ref217490" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217491" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Passage through Islam</h2><p>Two centuries after they broke out of their desert around Mecca, the followers of Muhammad occupied the lands from Persia to Spain and settled down to master the arts and sciences of the peoples they had conquered. They admired especially the works of the Greek mathematicians and physicians and the philosophy of Aristotle. By the late 9th century they were already able to add to the geometry of Euclid, Archimedes, and Apollonius. In the 10th century they went beyond Ptolemy. Stimulated by the problem of finding the effective orientation for prayer (the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/qiblah" class="md-crosslink"><em>qiblah</em></a>, or direction from the place of worship to Mecca), Islamic geometers and astronomers developed the stereographic <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/projection-geometry" class="md-crosslink autoxref">projection</a> (invented to project the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/celestial-sphere" class="md-crosslink autoxref">celestial sphere</a> onto a two-dimensional map or instrument) as well as plane and spherical <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/trigonometry" class="md-crosslink autoxref">trigonometry</a>. Here they incorporated elements derived from India as well as from Greece. Their achievements in geometry and geometrical astronomy materialized in instruments for drawing conic sections and, above all, in the beautiful brass <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/astrolabe-instrument" class="md-crosslink">astrolabes</a> with which they reduced to the turn of a dial the toil of calculating astronomical quantities.</p><!--[P46]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57443"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/66/67366-004-DC133B15.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/66/67366-004-DC133B15.jpg" alt="Mathematicians of the Islamic worldThis map spans more than 600 years of prominent Islamic mathematicians, from al-Khwārizmī (c. ad 800) to al-Kāshī (c. ad 1400). Their names—located on the map under their cities of birth—can be clicked to access their biographies.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML46]--><p><span id="ref726875"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thabit-ibn-Qurrah" class="md-crosslink">Thābit ibn Qurrah</a> (836–901) had precisely the attributes required to bring the geometry of the Arabs up to the mark set by the Greeks. As a member of a religious sect close but hostile to both Jews and Christians, he knew Syriac and Greek as well as Arabic; as a money changer, he knew how to calculate; as both, he recommended himself to the Banū Mūsā, a set of mathematician brothers descended from a robber who had diversified into astrology. The Banū Mūsā directed a House of Wisdom in Baghdad sponsored by the caliph. There they presided over translations of the Greek classics. Thābit became an ornament of the House of Wisdom. He translated Archimedes and Apollonius, some of whose books now are known only in his versions. In a notable addition to Euclid, he tried valiantly to prove the parallel postulate (discussed later in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry#ref217502" class="md-crosslink">Non-Euclidean geometries</a>).</p><!--[P47]--><p>Among the pieces of Greek geometrical astronomy that the Arabs made their own was the planispheric <span id="ref726876"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/astrolabe-instrument" class="md-crosslink">astrolabe</a>, which incorporated one of the methods of projecting the celestial sphere onto a two-dimensional surface invented in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Greece" class="md-crosslink autoxref">ancient Greece</a>. One of the desirable mathematical features of this method (the stereographic projection) is that it converts circles into circles or straight lines, a property proved in the first pages of Apollonius’s <em>Conics</em>. As Ptolemy showed in his <em>Planisphaerium</em>, the fact that the stereographic projection maps circles into circles or straight lines makes the astrolabe a very convenient instrument for reckoning time and representing the motions of celestial bodies. The earliest known Arabic astrolabes and manuals for their construction date from the 9th century. The <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Islamic world</a> improved the astrolabe as an aid for determining the time for prayers, for finding the direction to Mecca, and for astrological divination.</p><!--[P48]--></section><span id="ref217490" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217492" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Europe rediscovers the classics</h2><p>Contacts among Christians, Jews, and Arabs in Catalonia brought knowledge of the astrolabe to the West before the year 1000. During the 12th century many manuals for its use and construction were translated into Latin along with geometrical works by the Banū Mūsā, Thābit, and others. Some of the achievements of the Arab geometers were rediscovered in the West after wide and close study of Euclid’s <em>Elements</em>, which was translated repeatedly from the Arabic and once from the Greek in the 12th and 13th centuries. The <em>Elements</em> (Venice, 1482) was one of the first technical books ever printed. Archimedes also came West in the 12th century, in Latin translations from Greek and Arabic sources. Apollonius arrived only by bits and pieces. Ptolemy’s <em>Almagest</em> appeared in Latin manuscript in 1175. Not until the humanists of the Renaissance turned their classical learning to mathematics, however, did the Greeks come out in standard printed editions in both Latin and Greek.</p><!--[P49]--><p>These texts affected their Latin readers with the strength of revelation. Europeans discovered the notion of proof, the power of generalization, and the superhuman cleverness of the Greeks; they hurried to master techniques that would enable them to improve their calendars and horoscopes, fashion better instruments, and raise Christian mathematicians to the level of the infidels. It took more than two centuries for the Europeans to make their unexpected heritage their own. By the 15th century, however, they were prepared to go beyond their sources. The most novel developments occurred where creativity was strongest, in the art of the Italian Renaissance.</p><!--[P50]--></section><span id="ref217490" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217493" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref726895"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/art/linear-perspective" class="md-crosslink">Linear perspective</a></h2><p>The theory of linear perspective, the brainchild of the Florentine architect-engineers <span id="ref726935"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Filippo-Brunelleschi" class="md-crosslink">Filippo Brunelleschi</a> (1377–1446) and <span id="ref726915"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leon-Battista-Alberti" class="md-crosslink">Leon Battista Alberti</a> (1404–72) and their followers, was to help remake geometry during the 17th century. The scheme of Brunelleschi and Alberti, as given without proofs in Alberti’s <em>De pictura</em> (1435; <em>On Painting</em>), exploits the pyramid of rays that, according to what they had learned from the Westernized versions of the optics of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ibn-al-Haytham" class="md-crosslink">Ibn Al-Haytham</a> (<em>c.</em> 965–1040), proceeds from the object to the painter’s eye. Imagine, as Alberti directed, that the painter studies a scene through a window, using only one eye and not moving his head; he cannot know whether he looks at an external scene or at a glass painted to present to his eye the same visual pyramid. Supposing this decorated window to be the canvas, Alberti interpreted the painting-to-be as the projection of the scene in life onto a vertical plane cutting the visual pyramid. A distinctive feature of his system was the “point at infinity” at which parallel lines in the painting appear to converge, as shown in the photograph.</p><!--[P51]--><p>Alberti’s procedure, as developed by <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Piero-della-Francesca" class="md-crosslink">Piero della Francesca</a> (<em>c.</em> 1410–92) and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albrecht-Durer-German-artist" class="md-crosslink">Albrecht Dürer</a> (1471–1528), was used by many artists who wished to render perspective persuasively. At the same time, cartographers tried various projections of the sphere to accommodate the record of geographical discoveries that began in the mid-15th century with Portuguese exploration of the west coast of Africa. Coincidentally with these explorations, mapmakers recovered Ptolemy’s <em>Geography</em>, in which he had recorded by latitude (sometimes near enough) and longitude (usually far off) the principal places known to him and indicated how they could be projected onto a map (<em>see</em> photograph).</p><!--[P52]--><p>The discoveries that enlarged the known Earth did not fit easily on Ptolemy’s projections. Cartographers therefore adopted the stereographic projection that had served astronomers. Several projected the Northern Hemisphere onto the Equator just as in the standard astrolabe, but the most widely used aspect, popularized in the world maps made by <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gerardus-Mercator" class="md-crosslink">Gerardus Mercator</a>’s son for later editions of his father’s atlas (beginning in 1595), projected points on the Earth onto a cylinder tangent to the Earth at the Equator. After cutting the cylinder along a vertical line and flattening the resulting rectangle, the result was the now-familiar <span id="ref726955"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Mercator-projection" class="md-crosslink">Mercator</a> map shown in the photograph.</p><!--[P53]--><p>The intense cultivation of methods of projection by artists, architects, and cartographers during the Renaissance eventually provoked mathematicians into considering the properties of linear perspective in general. The most profound of these generalists was a sometime architect named <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Girard-Desargues" class="md-crosslink">Girard Desargues</a> (1591–1661).</p><!--[P54]--></section></section><section id="ref217494" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Transformation</h2><span id="ref217494" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217495" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">French circles</h2><p>Desargues was a member of intersecting circles of 17th-century French mathematicians worthy of Plato’s Academy of the 4th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span> or Baghdad’s House of Wisdom of the 9th century <span class="text-smallcaps">ce</span>. They included <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rene-Descartes" class="md-crosslink">René Descartes</a> (1596–1650) and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pierre-de-Fermat" class="md-crosslink">Pierre de Fermat</a> (1601–65), inventors of analytic geometry; <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gilles-Personne-de-Roberval" class="md-crosslink">Gilles Personne de Roberval</a> (1602–75), a pioneer in the development of the calculus; and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Blaise-Pascal" class="md-crosslink">Blaise Pascal</a> (1623–62), a contributor to the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/calculus-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">calculus</a> and an exponent of the principles set forth by Desargues.</p><!--[P55]--><span id="ref217494" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217496" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Projective geometry</h2><p>Two main directions can be distinguished in Desargues’s work. Like Renaissance artists, <span id="ref726995"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Girard-Desargues" class="md-crosslink">Desargues</a> freely admitted the point at <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/infinity-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">infinity</a> into his demonstrations and showed that every set of parallel lines in a scene (apart from those parallel to the sides of the canvas) should project as converging bundles at some point on the “line at infinity” (the horizon). With the addition of points at infinity to the Euclidean plane, Desargues could frame all his propositions about straight lines without excepting parallel ones—which, like the others, now met one another, although not before “infinity.” A farther-reaching matter arising from artistic perspective was the relation between projections of the same object from different points of view and different positions of the canvas. Desargues observed that neither size nor shape is generally preserved in projections, but collinearity is, and he provided an example, possibly useful to artists, in images of triangles seen from different points of view. The statement that accompanied this example became known as <span id="ref726996"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Desarguess-theorem" class="md-crosslink">Desargues’s theorem</a>.</p><!--[P56]--><p>Desargues’s second direction was to “simplify” Apollonius’s work on <span id="ref726997"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/conic-section" class="md-crosslink">conic sections</a>. Despite his generality of approach, Apollonius needed to prove all his theorems for each type of conic separately. Desargues saw that he could prove them all at once and, moreover, by treating a cylinder as a cone with vertex at infinity, demonstrate useful <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analogies" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="analogies">analogies</a> between cylinders and cones. Following his lead, Pascal made his surprising discovery that the intersections of the three pairs of opposite sides of a hexagon inscribed in a conic lie on a straight line. (<em>See</em> <span data-assembly-id="57051" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>.) In 1685, in his <em>Sectiones Conicæ</em>, Philippe de la Hire (1640–1718), a Parisian painter turned mathematician, proved several hundred propositions in Apollonius’s <em>Conics</em> by Desargues’s efficient methods.</p><!--[P57]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57051"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/53/66653-004-0B8D7195.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/53/66653-004-0B8D7195.jpg" alt="Pascal's hexagonBlaise Pascal proved that for any hexagon inscribed in any conic section (ellipse, parabola, hyperbola) the three pairs of opposite sides when extended intersect in points that lie on a straight line. Descartes esteemed these <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implements" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="implements">implements</a> and the constructions they effected as (to quote from a letter of 1619) “no less certain and geometrical than the ordinary ones with which circles are drawn.” By the use of apt instruments, he would bring ancient mathematics to perfection: “scarcely anything will remain to be discovered in geometry.”</p><!--[P58]--><p>What Descartes had in mind was the use of compasses with sliding members to generate curves. To classify and study such curves, Descartes took his lead from the relations Apollonius had used to classify conic sections, which contain the squares, but no higher powers, of the variables. To describe the more complicated curves produced by his instruments or defined as the loci of points satisfying involved <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/criteria" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="criteria">criteria</a>, Descartes had to include cubes and higher powers of the variables. He thus overcame what he called the deceptive character of the terms <em>square</em>, <em>rectangle</em>, and <em>cube</em> as used by the ancients and came to identify geometric curves as depictions of relationships defined algebraically. By reducing relations difficult to state and prove geometrically to algebraic relations between coordinates (usually rectangular) of points on curves, Descartes brought about the union of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/algebra" class="md-crosslink autoxref">algebra</a> and geometry that gave birth to the calculus.</p><!--[P59]--></section></section><span id="ref217494" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217498" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Geometrical calculus</h2><p>The familiar use of infinity, which underlay much of perspective theory and projective geometry, also leavened the tedious Archimedean method of exhaustion. Not surprisingly, a practical man, the Flemish engineer <span id="ref727055"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Simon-Stevin" class="md-crosslink">Simon Stevin</a> (1548–1620), who wrote on perspective and cartography among many other topics of applied mathematics, gave the first effective impulse toward redefining the object of Archimedean <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/analysis-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">analysis</a>. Instead of confining the circle between an inscribed and a circumscribed polygon, the new view regarded the circle as identical to the polygons, and the polygons to one another, when the number of their sides becomes infinitely great.</p><!--[P60]--><p>This revitalized approach to exhaustion received a preliminary systematization in the <em>Geometria Indivisibilibus Continuorum Nova Quadam Ratione Promota</em> (1635; “A Method for the Determination of a New Geometry of Continuous Indivisibles”) by the Italian mathematician <span id="ref727016"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bonaventura-Cavalieri" class="md-crosslink">Bonaventura (Francesco) Cavalieri</a> (1598–1647). Cavalieri, perhaps influenced by Kepler’s method of determining volumes in <em>Nova Steriometria Doliorum</em> (1615; “New Stereometry of Wine Barrels”), regarded lines as made up of an <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infinite" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="infinite">infinite</a> number of dimensionless points, areas as made up of lines of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/infinitesimal" class="md-crosslink autoxref">infinitesimal</a> thickness, and volumes as made up of planes of infinitesimal depth in order to obtain algebraic ways of summing the elements into which he divided his figures. Cavalieri’s method may be stated as follows: if two figures (solids) of equal height are cut by parallel lines (planes) such that each pair of lengths (areas) matches, then the two figures (solids) have the same area (volume). (<em>See</em> <span data-assembly-id="57052" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>.) Although not up to the rigorous standards of today and criticized by “classicist” contemporaries (who were unaware that Archimedes himself had explored similar techniques), Cavalieri’s <span id="ref727035"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/method-of-indivisibles" class="md-crosslink">method of indivisibles</a> became a standard tool for solving volumes until the introduction of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/integral-calculus" class="md-crosslink autoxref">integral calculus</a> near the end of the 17th century.</p><!--[P61]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57052"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/54/66654-004-99D4F14F.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/54/66654-004-99D4F14F.jpg" alt="Cavalieri's principleBonaventura Cavalieri observed that figures (solids) of equal height and in which all corresponding cross sections match in length (area) are of equal area (volume). Let the sides sought for the rectangle be denoted by <em>a</em> and <em>b</em>. Increase one side and diminish the other by a small amount ε; the resultant area is then given by (<em>a</em> + ε)(<em>b</em> − ε). Fermat observed what Kepler had perceived earlier in investigating the most useful shapes for wine casks, that near its maximum (or minimum) a quantity scarcely changes as the variables on which it depends alter slightly. On this principle, Fermat equated the areas <em>a</em><em>b</em> and (<em>a</em> + ε)(<em>b</em> − ε) to obtain the stationary values: <em>a</em><em>b</em> = <em>a</em><em>b</em> − ε<em>a</em> + ε<em>b</em> − ε<sup>2</sup>. By canceling the common term <em>a</em><em>b</em>, dividing by ε, and then setting ε at zero, Fermat had his well-known answer, <em>a</em> = <em>b</em>. The figure with maximum area is a square. To obtain the tangent to a curve by this method, Fermat began with a secant through two points a short distance apart and let the distance vanish (<em>see</em> <span data-assembly-id="57048" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>).</p><!--[P62]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57048"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/50/66650-004-4FF38AA7.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/50/66650-004-4FF38AA7.jpg" alt="Fermat's tangent methodPierre de Fermat anticipated the calculus with his approach to finding the tangent line to a given curve. Kepler not only replaced the many circles of the old planetary system with a few ellipses, he also substituted a complicated rule of motion (his “second law”) for the relatively simple Ptolemaic rule that all motions must be <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/compounded" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="compounded">compounded</a> of rotations performed at <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/constant" class="md-crosslink autoxref">constant</a> velocity. <span id="ref727095"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Keplers-second-law-of-planetary-motion" class="md-crosslink">Kepler’s second law</a> states that a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/planet" class="md-crosslink autoxref">planet</a> moves in its ellipse so that the line between it and the Sun placed at a focus sweeps out equal areas in equal times. His astronomy thus made pressing and practical the otherwise merely difficult problem of the quadrature of conics and the associated theory of indivisibles.</p><!--[P63]--><p>With the methods of Apollonius and a few infinitesimals, an inspired geometer showed that the laws regarding both area and ellipse can be derived from the suppositions that bodies free from all forces either rest or travel uniformly in straight lines and that each planet constantly falls toward the Sun with an acceleration that depends only on the distance between their centres. The inspired geometer was <span id="ref727117"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Isaac-Newton" class="md-crosslink">Isaac Newton</a> (1642 [Old Style]–1727), who made planetary <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dynamics" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="dynamics">dynamics</a> a matter entirely of geometry by replacing the planetary orbit by a succession of infinitesimal chords, planetary acceleration by a series of centripetal jerks, and, in keeping with Kepler’s second law, time by an area.</p><!--[P64]--><p>Besides the problem of planetary motion, questions in optics pushed 17th-century natural philosophers and mathematicians to the study of conic sections. As Archimedes is supposed to have shown (or shone) in his destruction of a Roman fleet by reflected sunlight, a parabolic mirror brings all rays parallel to its axis to a common focus. The story of Archimedes provoked many later geometers, including Newton, to emulation. Eventually they created instruments powerful enough to melt iron.</p><!--[P65]--><p>The figuring of telescope lenses likewise strengthened interest in conics after <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Galileo-Galilei" class="md-crosslink">Galileo Galilei</a>’s revolutionary improvements to the astronomical telescope in 1609. Descartes emphasized the desirability of lenses with hyperbolic surfaces, which focus bundles of parallel rays to a point (spherical lenses of wide apertures give a blurry image), and he invented a machine to cut them—which, however, proved more ingenious than useful.</p><!--[P66]--><p>A final example of early modern applications of geometry to the physical world is the old problem of the size of the <span id="ref727115"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth" class="md-crosslink">Earth</a>. (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Measuring-the-Earth-Modernized-1673316" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: Measuring the Earth, Modernized</a>.) On the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypothesis" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="hypothesis">hypothesis</a> that the Earth cooled from a spinning liquid blob, Newton calculated that it is an oblate spheroid (obtained by rotating an ellipse around its minor axis), not a sphere, and he gave the excess of its equatorial over its polar diameter. During the 18th century many geodesists tried to find the eccentricity of the terrestrial ellipse. At first it appeared that all the measurements might be compatible with a Newtonian Earth. By the end of the century, however, geodesists had uncovered by geometry that the Earth does not, in fact, have a regular geometrical shape.</p><!--[P67]--></section></section><section id="ref217500" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Relaxation and rigour</h2><p>The dominance of analysis (algebra and the calculus) during the 18th century produced a reaction in favour of geometry early in the 19th century. Fundamental new branches of the subject resulted that deepened, generalized, and violated principles of ancient geometry. The cultivators of these new fields, such as <span id="ref727119"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Victor-Poncelet" class="md-crosslink">Jean-Victor Poncelet</a> (1788–1867) and his self-taught <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disciple" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="disciple">disciple</a> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jakob-Steiner" class="md-crosslink">Jakob Steiner</a> (1796–1863), vehemently urged the claims of geometry over analysis. The early 19th-century revival of pure geometry produced the discovery that Euclid had devoted his efforts to only one of several <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="comprehensive">comprehensive</a> geometries, the others of which can be created by replacing Euclid’s fifth postulate with another about parallels.</p><!--[P68]--><span id="ref217500" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217501" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Projection again</h2><p>Poncelet, who was an officer in the French corps of engineers, learned scraps of Desargues’s work from his teacher <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gaspard-Monge-comte-de-Peluse" class="md-crosslink">Gaspard Monge</a> (1746–1818), who developed his own method of projection for drawings of buildings and machines. Poncelet relied on this information to keep himself alive. Taken captive during Napoleon’s invasion of Russia in 1812, he passed his time by rehearsing in his head the things he had learned from Monge. The result was <span id="ref727118"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/projective-geometry" class="md-crosslink">projective geometry</a>.</p><!--[P69]--><p>Poncelet employed three basic tools. One he took from Desargues: the demonstration of difficult theorems about a complicated figure by working out equivalent simpler theorems on an elementary figure interchangeable with the original figure by projection. The second tool, <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/continuity" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="continuity">continuity</a>, allows the geometer to claim certain things as true for one figure that are true of another equally general figure provided that the figures can be derived from one another by a certain process of continual change. Poncelet and his defender <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michel-Chasles" class="md-crosslink">Michel Chasles</a> (1793–1880) extended the principle of continuity into the domain of the imagination by considering constructs such as the common chord in two circles that do not intersect.</p><!--[P70]--><p>Poncelet’s third tool was the “principle of <span id="ref727295"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/duality" class="md-crosslink">duality</a>,” which interchanges various concepts such as points with lines, or lines with planes, so as to generate new theorems from old theorems. <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Desarguess-theorem" class="md-crosslink">Desargues’s theorem</a> allows their interchange. So, as Steiner showed, does Pascal’s theorem that the three points of intersection of the opposite sides of a hexagon inscribed in a conic lie on a line; thus, the lines joining the opposite vertices of a hexagon circumscribed about a conic meet in a point. (<em>See</em> <span data-assembly-id="57051" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>.)</p><!--[P71]--><p>Poncelet’s followers realized that they were hampering themselves, and disguising the true fundamentality of projective geometry, by retaining the concept of length and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/congruence" class="md-crosslink autoxref">congruence</a> in their formulations, since projections do not usually preserve them. Similarly, parallelism had to go. Efforts were well under way by the middle of the 19th century, by <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Karl-Georg-Christian-von-Staudt" class="md-crosslink">Karl George Christian von Staudt</a> (1798–1867) among others, to purge projective geometry of the last superfluous relics from its Euclidean past.</p><!--[P72]--></section><span id="ref217500" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217502" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Non-Euclidean geometries</h2><p>The Enlightenment was not so preoccupied with analysis as to completely ignore the problem of Euclid’s fifth postulate. In 1733 <span id="ref727315"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Girolamo-Saccheri" class="md-crosslink">Girolamo Saccheri</a> (1667–1733), a Jesuit professor of mathematics at the University of Pavia, Italy, substantially advanced the age-old discussion by setting forth the alternatives in great clarity and detail before declaring that he had “cleared Euclid of every defect” (<em>Euclides ab Omni Naevo Vindicatus</em>, 1733). Euclid’s fifth postulate runs: “If a straight line falling on two straight lines makes the interior angles on the same side less than two right angles, the straight lines, if produced indefinitely, will meet on that side on which are the angles less than two right angles.” Saccheri took up the quadrilateral of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Omar-Khayyam-Persian-poet-and-astronomer" class="md-crosslink">Omar Khayyam</a> (1048–1131), who started with two parallel lines <em>A</em><em>B</em> and <em>D</em><em>C</em>, formed the sides by drawing lines <em>A</em><em>D</em> and <em>B</em><em>C</em> perpendicular to <em>A</em><em>B</em>, and then considered three <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hypotheses" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="hypotheses">hypotheses</a> for the internal angles at <em>C</em> and <em>D</em>: to be right, <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/obtuse" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="obtuse">obtuse</a>, or <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/acute" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="acute">acute</a> (<em>see</em> <span data-assembly-id="57049" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>). The first possibility gives Euclidean geometry. Saccheri devoted himself to proving that the obtuse and the acute alternatives both end in contradictions, which would thereby eliminate the need for an explicit parallel postulate.</p><!--[P73]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57049"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/51/66651-004-D22D2B83.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/51/66651-004-D22D2B83.jpg" alt="Quadrilateral of Omar KhayyamOmar Khayyam constructed the quadrilateral shown in the figure in an effort to prove that Euclid's fifth postulate, concerning parallel lines, is superfluous. He then destroyed the obtuse hypothesis by an argument that depended upon allowing lines to increase in length indefinitely. If this is disallowed, the hypothesis of the obtuse angle produces a system equivalent to standard spherical geometry, the geometry of figures drawn on the surface of a sphere.</p><!--[P74]--><p>As for the acute angle, Saccheri could defeat it only by appealing to an arbitrary hypothesis about the behaviour of lines at infinity. One of his followers, the Swiss-German polymath <span id="ref727316"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johann-Heinrich-Lambert" class="md-crosslink">Johann Heinrich Lambert</a> (1728–77), observed that, based on the acute hypothesis, the area of a triangle is the negative of that of a spherical triangle. Since the latter is proportional to the square of the radius, <em>r</em>, the former appeared to Lambert to be the area of an imaginary sphere with radius <em>i</em><em>r</em>, where <em>i</em> = <span class="md-root"><span class="ada-visuallyhidden">Square root of</span><span class="root-symbol">√</span><span class="root-content">−1</span></span>.</p><!--[P75]--><p>Although both Saccheri and Lambert aimed to establish the hypothesis of the right angle, their arguments seemed rather to indicate the unimpeachability of the alternatives. Several mathematicians at the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Gottingen" class="md-crosslink autoxref">University of Göttingen</a>, notably the great <span id="ref727435"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Carl-Friedrich-Gauss" class="md-crosslink">Carl Friedrich Gauss</a> (1777–1855), then took up the problem. Gauss was probably the first to perceive that a consistent geometry could be built up independent of Euclid’s fifth postulate, and he derived many relevant propositions, which, however, he <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/promulgated" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="promulgated">promulgated</a> only in his teaching and correspondence. The earliest published non-Euclidean geometric systems were the independent work of two young men from the East who had nothing to lose by their boldness. Both can be considered Gauss’s <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disciples" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="disciples">disciples</a> once removed: the Russian <span id="ref727317"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nikolay-Ivanovich-Lobachevsky" class="md-crosslink">Nikolay Ivanovich Lobachevsky</a> (1792–1856), who learned his mathematics from a close friend of Gauss’s at the University of Kazan, where Lobachevsky later became a professor; and <span id="ref727318"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Janos-Bolyai" class="md-crosslink">János Bolyai</a> (1802–60), an officer in the Austro-Hungarian army whose father also was a friend of Gauss’s. Both Lobachevsky and Bolyai had worked out their novel geometries by 1826.</p><!--[P76]--><p>Lobachevsky and Bolyai reasoned about the hypothesis of the acute angle in the manner of Saccheri and Lambert and recovered their results about the areas of triangles. They advanced beyond Saccheri and Lambert by deriving an imaginary trigonometry to go with their imaginary geometry. Just as Desargues’s projective geometry was neglected for many years, so the work of Bolyai and Lobachevsky made little impression on mathematicians for a generation and more. It was largely the posthumous publication in 1855 of Gauss’s ideas about <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/non-Euclidean-geometry" class="md-crosslink autoxref">non-Euclidean geometry</a> that gave the new approaches the cachet to attract the attention of later mathematicians.</p><!--[P77]--></section><span id="ref217500" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217503" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">A grand synthesis</h2><p>Another of the profound impulses Gauss gave geometry concerned the general description of surfaces. Typically—with the notable exception of the geometry of the sphere—mathematicians had treated surfaces as structures in three-dimensional <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/Euclidean-space" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Euclidean space</a>. However, as these surfaces occupy only two dimensions, only two variables are needed to describe them. This prompted the thought that two-dimensional surfaces could be considered as “spaces” with their own geometries, not just as Euclidean structures in ordinary space. For example, the shortest distance, or path, between two points on the surface of a sphere is the lesser arc of the great circle joining them, whereas, considered as points in three-dimensional space, the shortest distance between them is an ordinary straight line.</p><!--[P78]--><p>The shortest path between two points on a surface lying wholly within that surface is called a geodesic, which reflects the origin of the concept in geodesy, in which Gauss took an active interest. His <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/initiative" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="initiative">initiative</a> in the study of surfaces as spaces and geodesics as their “lines” was pursued by his student and, briefly, his successor at Göttingen, <span id="ref727495"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Bernhard-Riemann" class="md-crosslink">Bernhard Riemann</a> (1826–66). Riemann began with an abstract space of <em>n</em> dimensions. That was in the 1850s, when mathematicians and mathematical physicists were beginning to use <em>n</em>-dimensional Euclidean space to describe the motions of systems of particles in the then-new <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/kinetic-theory-of-gases" class="md-crosslink">kinetic theory of gases</a>. Riemann worked in a quasi-Euclidean space—“quasi” because he used the calculus to generalize the Pythagorean theorem to supply sufficient flexibility to provide for geodesics on any surface.</p><!--[P79]--><p>When this very general <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/differential-geometry" class="md-crosslink">differential geometry</a> came down to two-dimensional surfaces of constant curvature, it revealed excellent models for non-Euclidean geometries. Riemann himself pointed out that, merely by calling the geodesics of a sphere “straight lines,” the maligned hypothesis of the obtuse angle produces the geometry appropriate to the sphere’s surface. Similarly, as shown by <span id="ref727515"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Eugenio-Beltrami" class="md-crosslink">Eugenio Beltrami</a> (1835–1900), who ended his teaching career in Saccheri’s old post at Pavia, the geometry defined in the plane by the hypothesis of the acute angle fits perfectly a surface of revolution of constant negative curvature now called a pseudosphere (<em>see</em> <span data-assembly-id="57050" class="md-link media-overlay-link">figure</span>)—again, provided that its geodesics are accepted as the straight lines of the geometry.</p><!--[P80]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="57050"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/52/66652-004-DC031B61.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/52/66652-004-DC031B61.jpg" alt="The pseudosphereThe pseudosphere has constant negative curvature; i.e., it maintains a constant concavity over its entire surface. Unable to be shown in its entirety in an illustration, the pseudosphere tapers to infinity in both directions away from the central disk. The pseudosphere was one of the first models for a non-Euclidean space." data-width="188" data-height="226"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">The pseudosphereThe pseudosphere has constant negative curvature; i.e., it maintains a constant concavity over its entire surface. Unable to be shown in its entirety in an illustration, the pseudosphere tapers to infinity in both directions away from the central disk. The pseudosphere was one of the first models for a non-Euclidean space.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML80]--><p>Since the hypothesis of the obtuse angle correctly characterizes Euclidean geometry applied to the surface of a sphere, the non-Euclidean geometry based on it must be exactly as consistent as Euclidean geometry. The case of the acute angle treated by Lobachevsky and Bolyai required a sharper tool. Beltrami found it in a projection into a disc in the Euclidean plane of the points of a non-Euclidean space, in which each geodesic from the non-Euclidean space corresponds to a chord of the disc. Geometry built on the hypothesis of the acute angle has the same consistency as Euclidean geometry.</p><!--[P81]--><p>The key role of Euclidean geometry in proofs of the consistency of non-Euclidean geometries exposed the <em>Elements</em> to ever-deeper scrutiny. The old blemishes—particularly appeals to <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intuition" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="intuition">intuition</a> and diagrams for the meaning of concepts like “inside” and “between” and the use of questionable procedures like superposition to prove congruency—became intolerable to mathematicians who laboured to clarify the foundations of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/arithmetic" class="md-crosslink autoxref">arithmetic</a> and the calculus as well as the interrelations of the new geometries. The German mathematician <span id="ref727535"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Moritz-Pasch" class="md-crosslink">Moritz Pasch</a> (1843–1930), in his <em><span id="ref727555"></span>Vorlesungen über neuere Geometrie</em> (1882; “Lectures on the New Geometry”), identified what was wanting: undefined concepts, axioms about those concepts, and more rigorous logic based on those axioms. The choice of undefined concepts and axioms is free, apart from the constraint of consistency. Mathematicians following Pasch’s path introduced various elements and axioms and developed their geometries with greater or lesser elegance and trouble. The most successful of these systematizers was the Göttingen professor <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/David-Hilbert" class="md-crosslink">David Hilbert</a> (1862–1943), whose <em>The Foundations of Geometry</em> (1899) greatly influenced efforts to axiomatize all of mathematics. (<em>See</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Teaching-the-Elements-733774" class="md-crosslink">Sidebar: Teaching the <em>Elements</em></a>.)</p><!--[P82]--></section><span id="ref217500" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref217504" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">The real world</h2><p>Euclid’s <em>Elements</em> had claimed the excellence of being a true account of space. Within this interpretation, Euclid’s fifth postulate was an <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/empirical" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="empirical">empirical</a> finding; non-Euclidean geometries did not apply to the real world. Bolyai apparently could not free himself from the persuasion that Euclidean geometry represented reality. Lobachevsky observed that, if there were a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/star-astronomy" class="md-crosslink autoxref">star</a> so distant that its parallax was not observable from the Earth’s orbit, his geometry would be indistinguishable from Euclid’s at the point where the parallax vanished. By his calculation, based on stellar parallaxes then just detected, his geometry could be physically meaningful only in gargantuan triangles spanning interstellar space.</p><!--[P83]--><p>In fact, non-Euclidean geometries apply to the cosmos more locally than Lobachevsky imagined. In 1916 <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-Einstein" class="md-crosslink">Albert Einstein</a> (1879–1955) published “The Foundation of the General <span id="ref727575"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/general-relativity" class="md-crosslink">Theory</a> of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/relativity" class="md-crosslink">Relativity</a>,” which replaced Newton’s description of gravitation as a force that attracts distant masses to each other through Euclidean space with a principle of least effort, or shortest (temporal) path, for motion along the geodesics of a curved space. Einstein not only explained how gravitating bodies give this surface its properties—that is, mass determines how the differential distances, or curvatures, in Riemann’s geometry differ from those in Euclidean space—but also successfully predicted the deflection of light, which has no mass, in the vicinity of a star or other massive body. This was an extravagant piece of geometrizing—the replacement of gravitational force by the curvature of a surface. But it was not all. In relativity theory time is considered to be a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/dimension-geometry" class="md-crosslink autoxref">dimension</a> along with the three dimensions of space. On the closed four-dimensional world thus formed, the history of the universe stands revealed as describable by motion within a vast congeries of geodesics in a non-Euclidean universe.</p><!--[P84]--></section></section></section><span class="md-signature"><a href="/contributor/JL-Heilbron/4434">J.L.
