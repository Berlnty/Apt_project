Now, another salvo has been launched, in the form of a new report from environmental activist organization Friends of the Earth International and the Center for Food Safety, a Washington (D.C.) advocacy group. Called <cite>Who Benefits from GM Crops?</cite>, the study examines the emergence of &quot;superweeds&quot; that have developed a resistance to conventional herbicides such as Monsanto&apos;s (MON) Roundup. The culprits, says the report, are plants like corn, soybeans, and cotton that have been genetically modified to survive Roundup. Farmers can spray their fields and the weeds will die but the crops will thrive.</p>      <p>As more acres of &quot;Roundup Ready&quot; crops are planted, the use of the pesticide has increased. The increased application has led some weeds to develop a resistance to glyphosate, the generic term for the chemical in Roundup. And, in turn, farmers have had to apply stronger doses of pesticide to kill the superweeds.</p>      <p>According to the report, the amount of weed-killing herbicides used by farmers has exploded, rising fifteenfold since biotech crops were first planted. The report lists eight weeds in the U.S.&#x2014;among them horseweed, common waterhemp, and hairy fleabane&#x2014;that have developed resistance to glyphosate, the most commonly applied pesticide. The next generations of biotech seeds include some that have been modified to withstand stronger doses of herbicides, while another strategy has been to develop tolerances to different herbicides and to combine multiple types of resistance in the same seed. &quot;It&apos;s a chemical arms race against these weeds,&quot; says Bill Freese, a policy analyst at the Center for Food Safety and a co-author of the report.</p>      <h3>Monsanto Profit Forecast Up</h3>              <div id="box-MyWrqfC" class="page-ad" data-position="mobile-box">            <script class="params" Its Roundup business was thought to be an albatross, as the pesticide came off patent in 2000 and revenue quickly plunged. Chief Executive Hugh Grant hastened the company&apos;s shift away from reliance on Roundup sales to an emphasis on GMO (genetically modified organism) seeds&#x2014;in particular, commodity crops such as corn and soy, which are the grist for animal feed, food processing, and biofuels. In the company&apos;s first fiscal quarter, sales of Roundup and other chemicals jumped 47%.</p>                                                      <p>The company expects up to $1.4 billion in gross profit for the year from its chemicals business, Begemann said, which would be a 10% increase from 2007.
