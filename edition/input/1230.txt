class="md-byline">	<div class="written-by">			<span>Written By: </span>			<ul>				<li><a href="/contributor/Vladimir-Slamecka/3442">Vladimir Slamecka</a></li>				</ul>			</div>	<div class="last-updated hidden-xs">			<a data-type="accordion-link" href="#accordion-article-history" rel="nofollow">See Article History</a>			</div>	</div><div class="grid">				<div class="grid-sm">					<article id="article-content" class="content"><section id="ref1" data-level="1"><p><strong><span id="ref377121"></span>Information processing </strong>,  the acquisition, recording, organization, retrieval, display, and dissemination of <span id="ref730235"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/information-science" class="md-crosslink">information</a>. In recent years, the term has often been applied to computer-based operations specifically.</p><!--[P1]--><p>In popular usage, the term <em>information</em> refers to facts and opinions provided and received during the course of daily life: one obtains information directly from other living beings, from mass media, from electronic data banks, and from all sorts of observable phenomena in the surrounding <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="environment">environment</a>. A person using such facts and opinions generates more information, some of which is communicated to others during discourse, by instructions, in letters and documents, and through other media. Information organized according to some logical relationships is referred to as a body of knowledge, to be acquired by systematic exposure or study. Application of knowledge (or skills) yields expertise, and additional <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analytic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="analytic">analytic</a> or experiential insights are said to <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitute" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="constitute">constitute</a> instances of wisdom. Use of the term <em>information</em> is not restricted exclusively to its communication via natural language. Information is also registered and communicated through art and by facial expressions and gestures or by such other physical responses as shivering. Moreover, every living entity is endowed with information in the form of a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/genetic-code" class="md-crosslink autoxref">genetic code</a>. These information phenomena permeate the physical and mental world, and their variety is such that it has defied so far all attempts at a unified definition of information.</p><!--[P2]--><p>Interest in information phenomena increased dramatically in the 20th century, and today they are the objects of study in a number of <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disciplines" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="disciplines">disciplines</a>, including philosophy, physics, biology, linguistics, information and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/computer-science" class="md-crosslink autoxref">computer science</a>, electronic and communications <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/engineering" class="md-crosslink autoxref">engineering</a>, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/management-science" class="md-crosslink autoxref">management science</a>, and the social sciences. On the commercial side, the information <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/service-industry" class="md-crosslink autoxref">service industry</a> has become one of the newer industries worldwide. Almost all other industries—manufacturing and service—are increasingly concerned with information and its handling. The different, though often overlapping, viewpoints and phenomena of these fields lead to different (and sometimes conflicting) concepts and “definitions” of information.</p><!--[P3]--><p>This article touches on such concepts as they relate to information processing. In treating the basic elements of information processing, it distinguishes between information in <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/analog" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="analog">analog</a> and digital form, and it describes its acquisition, recording, organization, retrieval, display, and techniques of dissemination. A separate article, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/information-system" class="md-crosslink">information system</a>, covers methods for organizational control and dissemination of information.</p><!--[P4]--></section><div class="major-reference-upsell">			<iframe src="/GPTIframe/ajax/sync/EB_HOUSE_DESKTOP" scrolling="no" width="640" height="106"></iframe>		</div><section id="ref61649" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">General considerations</h2><section id="ref61650" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Basic concepts</h2><p>Interest in how information is <span id="ref212006"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/communication" class="md-crosslink">communicated</a> and how its carriers convey meaning has occupied, since the time of pre-Socratic philosophers, the field of inquiry called <span id="ref212007"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/semiotics" class="md-crosslink">semiotics</a>, the study of <span id="ref212008"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/sign-communications" class="md-crosslink">signs</a> and sign phenomena. Signs are the irreducible elements of communication and the carriers of meaning. The American philosopher, mathematician, and physicist <span id="ref212009"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Sanders-Peirce" class="md-crosslink">Charles S. Peirce</a> is credited with having pointed out the three dimensions of signs, which are concerned with, respectively, the body or medium of the sign, the object that the sign designates, and the interpretant or interpretation of the sign. Peirce recognized that the fundamental relations of information are essentially triadic; in contrast, all relations of the physical sciences are reducible to dyadic (binary) relations. Another American philosopher, <span id="ref212010"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-William-Morris" class="md-crosslink">Charles W. Morris</a>, designated these three sign dimensions syntactic, semantic, and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pragmatic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="pragmatic">pragmatic</a>, the names by which they are known today.</p><!--[P5]--><p>Information processes are executed by information processors. For a given information processor, whether physical or biological, a <span id="ref212011"></span>token is an object, devoid of meaning, that the processor recognizes as being totally different from other tokens. A group of such unique tokens recognized by a processor <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="constitutes">constitutes</a> its basic “alphabet”; for example, the dot, dash, and space constitute the basic token alphabet of a Morse-code processor. Objects that carry meaning are represented by patterns of tokens called <span id="ref212012"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/symbol" class="md-crosslink">symbols</a>. The latter combine to form symbolic expressions that constitute inputs to or outputs from information processes and are stored in the processor memory.</p><!--[P6]--><p><span id="ref212013"></span>Information processors are components of an information system, which is a class of constructs. An abstract model of an information system features four basic elements: processor, memory, receptor, and effector (<span data-assembly-id="2009" class="md-link media-overlay-link">Figure 1</span>). The processor has several functions: (1) to carry out elementary information processes on symbolic expressions, (2) to store temporarily in the processor’s <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/short-term-memory" class="md-crosslink autoxref">short-term memory</a> the input and output expressions on which these processes operate and that they generate, (3) to schedule execution of these processes, and (4) to change this sequence of operations in accordance with the contents of the short-term memory. The <span id="ref212014"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-memory" class="md-crosslink">memory</a> stores symbolic expressions, including those that represent composite information processes, called programs. The two other components, the <span id="ref212015"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/receptor-information-processing" class="md-crosslink">receptor</a> and the <span id="ref212016"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/effector-information-processing" class="md-crosslink">effector</a>, are input and output mechanisms whose functions are, respectively, to receive symbolic expressions or stimuli from the external environment for manipulation by the processor and to emit the processed structures back to the environment.</p><!--[P7]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="2009"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/89/1689-004-E4A85B38.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/89/1689-004-E4A85B38.jpg" alt="Structure of an information system." data-width="443" data-height="165"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">Structure of an information system.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML7]--><p>The <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/power-physics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">power</a> of this abstract model of an information-processing system is provided by the ability of its component processors to carry out a small number of elementary information processes: reading; comparing; creating, modifying, and naming; copying; storing; and writing. The model, which is representative of a broad variety of such systems, has been found useful to explicate man-made information systems <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implemented" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="implemented">implemented</a> on sequential information processors.</p><!--[P8]--><p>Because it has been recognized that in nature information processes are not strictly sequential, increasing attention has been focused since 1980 on the study of the human brain as an information processor of the parallel type. The <span id="ref632822"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/cognitive-science" class="md-crosslink">cognitive sciences</a>, the interdisciplinary field that focuses on the study of the human mind, have contributed to the development of <span id="ref212019"></span>neurocomputers, a new class of parallel, distributed-information processors that mimic the functioning of the human <span id="ref212020"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/brain" class="md-crosslink">brain</a>, including its capabilities for self-organization and learning. So-called <span id="ref212021"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/neural-network" class="md-crosslink">neural networks</a>, which are mathematical models inspired by the neural circuit network of the human brain, are increasingly finding applications in areas such as pattern recognition, control of industrial processes, and finance, as well as in many research disciplines.</p><!--[P9]--></section><section id="ref61651" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Information as a resource and <span id="ref212022"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/commodity" class="md-crosslink">commodity</a></h2><p>In the late 20th century, information acquired two major utilitarian <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/connotations" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="connotations">connotations</a>. On the one hand, it is considered an economic resource, somewhat on par with other resources such as labour, material, and capital. This view stems from evidence that the possession, manipulation, and use of information can increase the cost-effectiveness of many physical and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cognitive" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="cognitive">cognitive</a> processes. The rise in information-processing activities in industrial <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/manufacturing" class="md-crosslink autoxref">manufacturing</a> as well as in human <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/problem-solving" class="md-crosslink autoxref">problem solving</a> has been remarkable. Analysis of one of the three traditional divisions of the economy, the <span id="ref212023"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/service-industry" class="md-crosslink">service sector</a>, shows a sharp increase in information-intensive activities since the beginning of the 20th century. By 1975 these activities accounted for half of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/labor-in-economics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">labour force</a> of the United States (see <span id="ref1210"></span>Table 1), giving rise to the so-called <span id="ref212024"></span>information society.</p><!--[P10]--><span class="md-raw-html"><div class="md-drag md-table-wrapper"><table class="md-pre-table"><thead><tr><th class="table-header">Table 1: Labour Distribution (%) in the United States, 1880-2000</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>                                       1880      1920      1955      1975      2000 (est.)  Agriculture and extractive              50        28        14         4            2 Manufacturing, commerce, industry       36        53        37        29           22 Information, knowledge, education        2         9        29        50           66 Other services                          12        10        20        17           10  Source: Adapted from Graham T.T. Molitor, "The Information Society: The Path to Post-Industrial Growth," Edward Cornish (ed.),<em>Communications Tomorrow, The Coming of the Information Society,</em>        reprinted by permission of the World Future Society, Bethesda, Md. </td></tr></tbody></table></div></span><p>As an individual and societal resource, information has some interesting characteristics that separate it from the traditional notions of economic resources. Unlike other resources, information is expansive, with limits apparently imposed only by time and human cognitive capabilities. Its expansiveness is attributable to the following: (1) it is naturally diffusive, (2) it reproduces rather than being consumed through use, and (3) it can be shared only, not exchanged in transactions. At the same time, information is compressible, both syntactically and semantically. Coupled with its ability to be substituted for other economic resources, its transportability at very high speeds, and its ability to impart advantages to the holder of information, these characteristics are at the base of such societal industries as research, education, publishing, marketing, and even politics. Societal concern with the husbanding of information resources has extended from the traditional domain of libraries and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/archives" class="md-crosslink autoxref">archives</a> to <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/encompass" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="encompass">encompass</a> organizational, institutional, and governmental information under the umbrella of information resource management.</p><!--[P11]--><p>The second perception of information is that it is an economic commodity, which helps to stimulate the worldwide growth of a new segment of national economies—the information service sector. Taking advantage of the properties of information and building on the perception of its individual and societal utility and value, this sector provides a broad range of information products and services. By 1992 the market share of the U.S. information service sector had grown to about $25 billion. This was equivalent to about one-seventh of the country’s computer market, which, in turn, represented roughly 40 percent of the global market in computers in that year. However, the probable convergence of computers and television (which constitutes a market share 100 times larger than computers) and its impact on information services, entertainment, and education are likely to restructure the respective market shares of the information <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/industry" class="md-crosslink autoxref">industry</a>.</p><!--[P12]--></section></section><!--[H3]--><section id="ref61652" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">Elements of information processing</h2><p>Humans receive information with their <span id="ref212025"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/senses" class="md-crosslink">senses</a>: sounds through hearing; images and text through sight; shape, temperature, and affection through touch; and odours through smell. To interpret the signals received from the senses, humans have developed and learned complex systems of languages consisting of “alphabets” of symbols and stimuli and the associated rules of usage. This has enabled them to recognize the objects they see, understand the messages they read or hear, and comprehend the signs received through the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/tactile" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="tactile">tactile</a> and olfactory senses.</p><!--[P13]--><p>The carriers of information-conveying signs received by the senses are energy phenomena—audio waves, light waves, and chemical and electrochemical stimuli. In engineering parlance, humans are receptors of analog signals; and, by a somewhat loose convention, the messages conveyed via these carriers are called analog-form information, or simply <span id="ref212026"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/analog-information" class="md-crosslink">analog information</a>. Until the development of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/digital-computer" class="md-crosslink autoxref">digital computer</a>, cognitive information was stored and processed only in analog form, basically through the technologies of printing, photography, and telephony.</p><!--[P14]--><p>Although humans are adept at processing information stored in their memories, analog information stored external to the mind is not processed easily. Modern information technology greatly <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/facilitates" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="facilitates">facilitates</a> the manipulation of externally stored information as a result of its representation as digital signals—i.e., as the presence or absence of energy (electricity, light, or magnetism). Information represented digitally in two-state, or <span id="ref212027"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/bit-communications" class="md-crosslink">binary</a>, form is often referred to as digital information. Modern information systems are characterized by extensive <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/metamorphoses" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="metamorphoses">metamorphoses</a> of analog and digital information. With respect to information storage and communication, the transition from analog to digital information is so <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/pervasive" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="pervasive">pervasive</a> as to bring a historic transformation of the manner in which humans create, access, and use information.</p><!--[P15]--><section id="ref61653" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Acquisition and recording of information in analog form</h2><p>The principal categories of information sources useful in modern information systems are text, video, and voice. One of the first ways in which prehistoric humans communicated was by <span id="ref212030"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/sound-physics" class="md-crosslink">sound</a>; sounds represented concepts such as pleasure, anger, and fear, as well as objects of the surrounding environment, including food and tools. Sounds assumed their meaning by convention—namely, by the use to which they were consistently put. Combining parts of sound allowed representation of more complex concepts and gradually led to the development of speech and eventually to spoken “natural” languages.</p><!--[P16]--><p>For information to be communicated broadly, it needs to be stored external to human memory; because accumulation of human experience, knowledge, and learning would be severely limited without such storage, the development of <span id="ref212031"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/writing" class="md-crosslink">writing</a> systems was made necessary.</p><!--[P17]--><p>Civilization can be traced to the time when humans began to associate abstract shapes with concepts and with the sounds of speech that represented them. Early recorded representations were those of visually perceived objects and events, as, for example, the animals and activities depicted in Paleolithic cave drawings. The evolution of writing systems proceeded through the early development of <span id="ref212032"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/pictography" class="md-crosslink">pictographic</a> languages, in which a symbol would represent an entire concept. Such symbols would go through many metamorphoses of shape in which the resemblance between each symbol and the object it stood for gradually disappeared, but its semantic meaning would become more precise. As the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/conceptual" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="conceptual">conceptual</a> world of humans became larger, the symbols, called <span id="ref212033"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/ideogram" class="md-crosslink">ideographs</a>, grew in number. Modern Chinese, a present-day result of this evolutionary direction of a pictographic writing system, has upwards of 50,000 ideographs.</p><!--[P18]--><p>At some point in the evolution of written languages, the method of representation shifted from the pictographic to the phonetic: speech sounds began to be represented by an <span id="ref212034"></span>alphabet of graphic symbols. Combinations of a relatively small set of such symbols could stand for more complex concepts as words, phrases, and sentences. The <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/invention-technology" class="md-crosslink autoxref">invention</a> of the written phonetic alphabet is thought to have taken place during the 2nd millennium <span class="text-smallcaps">bc</span>. The pragmatic advantages of alphabetic writing systems over the pictographic became apparent twice in the past millennium: after the invention of the movable-type <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/printing-press" class="md-crosslink autoxref">printing press</a> in the 15th century and again with the development of information processing by electronic means since the mid-1940s.</p><!--[P19]--><p>From the time early humans learned to represent concepts symbolically, they used whatever materials were readily available in nature for recording. The Sumerian <span id="ref212035"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/cuneiform" class="md-crosslink">cuneiform</a>, a wedge-shaped writing system, was impressed by a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/stylus-writing-implement" class="md-crosslink autoxref">stylus</a> into soft <span id="ref212036"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/clay-tablet" class="md-crosslink">clay tablets</a>, which were subsequently hardened by drying in the sun or the oven. The earliest <span id="ref212037"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chinese-writing" class="md-crosslink">Chinese writing</a>, dating to the 2nd millennium <span class="text-smallcaps">bc</span>, is preserved on animal bone and shell, while early writing in India was done on palm leaves and birch bark. Applications of technology yielded other materials for writing. The Chinese had recorded their pictographs on silk, using brushes made from animal hair, long before they invented paper. The <span id="ref212038"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Egyptian-language" class="md-crosslink">Egyptians</a> first wrote on cotton, but they began using <span id="ref212039"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/papyrus-writing-material" class="md-crosslink">papyrus</a> sheets and rolls made from the fibrous lining of the papyrus plant during the 4th millennium <span class="text-smallcaps">bc</span>. The reed brush and a palette of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/ink-writing-medium" class="md-crosslink autoxref">ink</a> were the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/implements" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="implements">implements</a> with which they wrote hieroglyphic script. Writing on <span id="ref212040"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/parchment" class="md-crosslink">parchment</a>, a material that was superior to papyrus and was made from the prepared skins of animals, became commonplace about 200 <span class="text-smallcaps">bc</span>, some 300 years after its first recorded use, and the quill <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/pen-writing-implement" class="md-crosslink autoxref">pen</a> replaced the reed brush. By the 4th century <span class="text-smallcaps">ad</span>, parchment came to be the principal writing material in Europe.</p><!--[P20]--><p><span id="ref212041"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/paper" class="md-crosslink">Paper</a> was invented in China at the beginning of the 2nd century <span class="text-smallcaps">ad</span>, and for some 600 years its use was confined to East Asia. In <span class="text-smallcaps">ad</span> 751 Arab and Chinese armies clashed at the Battle of Talas, near Samarkand; among the Chinese taken captive were some papermakers from whom the Arabs learned the techniques. From the 7th century on, paper became the dominant writing material of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Islamic-world" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Islamic world</a>. Papermaking finally reached Spain and Sicily in the 12th century, and it took another three centuries before it was practiced in Germany.</p><!--[P21]--><p>With the invention of <span id="ref212042"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/printing-publishing" class="md-crosslink">printing</a> from movable type, <span id="ref212043"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/typesetting" class="md-crosslink">typesetting</a> became the standard method of creating copy. Typesetting was an entirely manual operation until the adoption of a <span id="ref212044"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/typewriter" class="md-crosslink">typewriter-like</a> keyboard in the 19th century. In fact, it was the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/typewriter" class="md-crosslink autoxref">typewriter</a> that mechanized the process of recording original text. Although the typewriter was invented during the early 18th century in England, the first practical version, constructed by the American inventor <span id="ref212045"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Christopher-Latham-Sholes" class="md-crosslink">Christopher Latham Sholes</a>, did not appear until 1867. The mechanical typewriter finally found wide use after World War I. Today its electronic variant, the computer video terminal, is used pervasively to record original text.</p><!--[P22]--><p>Recording of original nontextual (image) information was a manual process until the development of <span id="ref212046"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology-of-photography" class="md-crosslink">photography</a> during the early decades of the 19th century; drawing and carving were the principal early means of recording graphics. Other techniques were developed alongside printing—for example, etching in stone and metal. The invention of film and the photographic process added a new dimension to information acquisition: for the first time, complex visual images of the real world could be captured accurately. Photography provided a method of storing information in less space and more accurately than was previously possible with narrative information.</p><!--[P23]--><p>During the 20th century, versatile electromagnetic media opened up new possibilities for capturing original analog information. <span id="ref730115"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/magnetic-tape" class="md-crosslink">Magnetic audio tape</a> is used to capture speech and music, and magnetic <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/videotape" class="md-crosslink autoxref">videotape</a> provides a low-cost medium for recording analog voice and video signals directly and simultaneously. Magnetic technology has other uses in the direct recording of analog information, including alphanumerics. Magnetic characters, bar codes, and special marks are printed on checks, labels, and forms for subsequent sensing by magnetic or optical readers and conversion to digital form. Banks, educational institutions, and the retail industry rely heavily on this technology. Nonetheless, paper and film continue to be the dominant media for direct storage of textual and visual information in analog form.</p><!--[P24]--></section><section id="ref61654" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Acquisition and recording of information in digital form</h2><p>The versatility of modern information systems stems from their ability to represent information electronically as digital signals and to manipulate it automatically at exceedingly high speeds. Information is stored in <span id="ref212048"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/bit-communications" class="md-crosslink">binary</a> devices, which are the basic components of <span id="ref730175"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/digital-information" class="md-crosslink">digital</a> technology. Because these devices exist only in one of two states, information is represented in them either as the absence or the presence of energy (electric pulse). The two states of binary devices are conveniently designated by the binary digits, or bits, zero (0) and one (1).</p><!--[P25]--><p>In this manner, alphabetic symbols of natural-language writing systems can be represented digitally as combinations of zeros (no pulse) and ones (pulse). Tables of equivalences of alphanumeric characters and strings of binary digits are called <span id="ref212049"></span>coding systems, the counterpart of writing systems. A combination of three binary digits can represent up to eight such characters; one <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprising" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="comprising">comprising</a> four digits, up to 16 characters; and so on. The choice of a particular coding system depends on the size of the character set to be represented. The widely used systems are the American Standard Code for Information Interchange (<span id="ref212050"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/ASCII" class="md-crosslink">ASCII</a>), a seven- or eight-bit code representing the English alphabet, numerals, and certain special characters of the standard computer keyboard; and the corresponding eight-bit <span id="ref212051"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/EBCDIC" class="md-crosslink">Extended Binary Coded Decimal Interchange Code</a> (EBCDIC), used for computers produced by IBM (International Business Machines Corp.) and most compatible systems. The digital representation of a character by eight bits is called a <span id="ref212052"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/byte" class="md-crosslink">byte</a>.</p><!--[P26]--><p>The seven-bit ASCII code is capable of representing up to 128 alphanumeric and special characters—sufficient to accommodate the writing systems of many phonetic scripts, including Latin and Cyrillic. Some alphabetic scripts require more than seven bits; for example, the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arabic-alphabet" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Arabic alphabet</a>, also used in the Urdu and Persian languages, has 28 consonantal characters (as well as a number of vowels and diacritical marks), but each of these may have four shapes, depending on its position in the word.</p><!--[P27]--><p>For digital representation of nonalphabetic writing systems, even the eight-bit code accommodating 256 characters is inadequate. Some writing systems that use Chinese characters, for example, have more than 50,000 ideographs (the minimal standard <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/font" class="md-crosslink autoxref">font</a> for the Hanzi system in Chinese and the kanji system in Japanese has about 7,000 ideographs). Digital representation of such scripts can be accomplished in three ways. One approach is to develop a phonetic character set; the Chinese Pinyin, the Korean Hangul, and the Japanese hiragana phonetic schemes all have alphabetic sets similar in number to the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Latin-alphabet" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Latin alphabet</a>. As the use of phonetic alphabets in Oriental <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cultures" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="cultures">cultures</a> is not yet widespread, they may be converted to ideographic by means of a dictionary lookup. A second technique is to decompose ideographs into a small number of elementary signs called strokes, the sum of which constitutes a shape-oriented, nonphonetic alphabet. The third approach is to use more than eight bits to encode the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/large-numbers-1765137" class="md-crosslink autoxref">large numbers</a> of ideographs; for instance, two bytes can represent uniquely more than 65,000 ideographs. Because the eight-bit ASCII code is inadequate for a number of writing systems, either because they are nonalphabetic or because their phonetic scripts possess large numbers of diacritical marks, the computer industry in 1991 began formulating a new international coding standard based on 16 bits.</p><!--[P28]--><span id="ref61654" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61655" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Recording media</h2><p><span id="ref212053"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/punched-card" class="md-crosslink">Punched cards</a> and perforated paper tape were once widely used to store data in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/art/binary-form" class="md-crosslink autoxref">binary form</a>. Today they have been supplanted by media based on electromagnetic and electro-optic technologies except in a few special applications</p><!--[P29]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="video" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="198702"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="video"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/37/179437-049-BEAA504D.jpg" alt="preserving digital information" data-width="426" data-height="240"></div></div><figcaption><strong class="md-assembly-title">preserving digital information</strong><span class="md-assembly-caption">The challenge of preserving digital information, 2009 film.</span><cite>Contunico © ZDF Enterprises GmbH, Mainz</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML29]--><p>Present-day storage media are of two types: random- and serial-, or sequential-, access. In <span id="ref212055"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/RAM-computing" class="md-crosslink">random-access</a> media (such as primary memory), the time required for accessing a given piece of data is independent of its location, while in <span id="ref212056"></span>serial-access media the access time depends on the data’s location and the position of the read-write head. The typical serial-access medium is magnetic tape. The storage density of magnetic tape has increased considerably over the years, mainly by increases in the number of tracks packed across the width of the tape.</p><!--[P30]--><p>While magnetic tape remains a popular choice in applications requiring low-cost <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/auxiliary" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="auxiliary">auxiliary</a> storage and data exchange, new tape variants began entering the market of the 1990s. Video recording tape has been adapted for digital storage, and <span id="ref212057"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/digital-audio-tape" class="md-crosslink">digital audio tape</a> (DAT) surpasses all tape storage devices in offering the highest areal data densities. DAT technology uses a helical-scan recording method in which both the tape and the recording head move simultaneously, which allows extremely high recording densities. Early four-millimetre DAT cassettes had a capacity of up to eight billion bytes (eight gigabytes).</p><!--[P31]--><p>Another type of magnetic storage medium, the <span id="ref212058"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/magnetic-disk" class="md-crosslink">magnetic disk</a>, provides rapid, random access to data. This device, developed in 1962, consists of either an aluminum or a plastic platen coated with a metallic material. Information is recorded on a disk by turning the charge of the read-write head on and off, which produces magnetic “dots” representing binary digits in circular tracks. A block of data on a given track can be accessed without having to pass over a large portion of its contents sequentially, as in the case of tape. Data-retrieval time is thus reduced dramatically. Hard disk drives built into personal computers and workstations have storage capacities of up to several gigabytes. Large computers using disk cartridges can provide virtually unlimited mass storage.</p><!--[P32]--><p>During the 1970s the <span id="ref212060"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/floppy-disk" class="md-crosslink">floppy disk</a>—a small, flexible disk—was introduced for use in personal computers and other <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/microcomputer" class="md-crosslink autoxref">microcomputer</a> systems. Compared with the storage capacity of the conventional <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hard-disk" class="md-crosslink autoxref">hard disk</a>, that of such a “soft” diskette is low—under three million characters. This medium is used primarily for loading and backing up personal computers.</p><!--[P33]--><p>An entirely different kind of recording and storage medium, the <span id="ref212061"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/optical-disc" class="md-crosslink">optical</a> disc, became available during the early 1980s. The optical disc makes use of <span id="ref212062"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser" class="md-crosslink">laser</a> technology: digital data are recorded by burning a series of microscopic holes, or pits, with a laser beam into thin metallic film on the surface of a 4<span class="md-fraction md-fraction-oblique"><span><sup>3</sup>/<sub>4</sub></span></span>-inch (12-centimetre) plastic disc. In this way, information from magnetic tape is encoded on a master disc; subsequently, the master is replicated by a process called stamping. In the read mode, low-intensity laser light is reflected off the disc surface and is “read” by light-sensitive diodes. The <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/radiant-energy" class="md-crosslink autoxref">radiant energy</a> received by the diodes varies according to the presence of the pits, and this input is digitized by the diode circuits. The digital signals are then converted to analog information on a video screen or in printout form.</p><!--[P34]--><p>Since the introduction of this technology, three main types of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/optical-storage" class="md-crosslink autoxref">optical storage</a> media have become available: (1) <span id="ref287297"></span>rewritable, (2) write-once read-many (<span id="ref287298"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/WORM-computer-science" class="md-crosslink">WORM</a>), and (3) compact disc read-only memory (<span id="ref287299"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/CD-ROM" class="md-crosslink">CD-ROM</a>). Rewritable discs are functionally equivalent to magnetic disks, although the former are slower. WORM discs are used as an archival storage medium to enter data once and retrieve it many times. CD-ROMs are the preferred medium for electronic distribution of digital libraries and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/software" class="md-crosslink autoxref">software</a>. To raise storage capacity, optical discs are arranged into “jukeboxes” holding as many as 10 million pages of text or more than one terabyte (one trillion bytes) of image data. The high storage capacities and random access of the magneto-optical, rewritable discs are particularly suited for storing multimedia information, in which text, image, and sound are combined.</p><!--[P35]--></section><span id="ref61654" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61656" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Recording techniques</h2><p>Digitally stored information is commonly referred to as <span id="ref287300"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/data-computing" class="md-crosslink">data</a>, and its analog counterpart is called <span id="ref287301"></span>source data. Vast quantities of nondocument analog data are collected, digitized, and compressed automatically by means of appropriate instruments in fields such as astronomy, environmental monitoring, scientific experimentation and modeling, and national security. The capture of information generated by humankind, in the form of packages of symbols called documents, is accomplished by manual and, increasingly, automatic techniques. Data are entered manually by striking the keys of a <span id="ref287302"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/keyboard-information-recording" class="md-crosslink">keyboard</a>, touching a computer screen, or writing by hand on a digital tablet or its variant, the so-called pen computer. Manual data entry, a slow and error-prone process, is <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/facilitated" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="facilitated">facilitated</a> to a degree by special computer programs that include editing software, with which to insert formatting commands, verify spelling, and make text changes, and document-formatting software, with which to arrange and rearrange text and graphics flexibly on the output page.</p><!--[P36]--><p>It is estimated that 5 percent of all documents in the United States exist in digitized form and that two-thirds of the paper documents cannot be digitized by keyboard transcription because they contain drawings or still images and because such transcription would be highly uneconomical. Such documents are digitized economically by a process called <span id="ref287303"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/document-imaging" class="md-crosslink">document imaging</a> (see <span data-assembly-id="2014" class="md-link media-overlay-link">Figure 2</span>).</p><!--[P37]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="2014"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/90/1690-004-2F789B5A.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/90/1690-004-2F789B5A.jpg" alt="Document imaging." data-width="518" data-height="296"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">Document imaging.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML37]--><p>Document imaging utilizes digital scanners to generate a digital representation of a document page. An <span id="ref287304"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/optical-scanner" class="md-crosslink">image scanner</a> divides the page into minute picture areas called <span id="ref287305"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/pixel" class="md-crosslink">pixels</a> and produces an array of binary digits, each representing the brightness of a pixel. The resulting stream of bits is <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhanced" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="enhanced">enhanced</a> and compressed (to as little as 10 percent of the original volume) by a device called an <span id="ref287306"></span>image controller and is stored on a magnetic or optical medium. A large storage capacity is required, because it takes about 45,000 bytes to store a typical compressed text page of 2,500 characters and as much as 1,000,000 bytes to store a page containing an image. Aside from document imaging applications, digital scanning is used for transmission of documents via facsimile, in satellite photography, and in other applications.</p><!--[P38]--><p>An image scanner digitizes an entire document page for storage and display as an image and does not recognize characters and words of text. The stored material therefore cannot be linguistically manipulated by text processing and other software techniques. When such manipulation is desired, a software program performs the <span id="ref287307"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/OCR" class="md-crosslink">optical character recognition</a> (OCR) function by converting each optically scanned character into an electric signal and comparing it with the internally stored representation of an alphabet of characters, so as to select from it the one that matches the scanned character most closely or to reject it as an unidentifiable token. The more sophisticated of present-day OCR programs distinguish shapes, sizes, and pitch of symbols—including handwriting—and learn from experience. A universal OCR <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/machine" class="md-crosslink autoxref">machine</a> is not available, however, for even a single alphabet.</p><!--[P39]--><p>Still <span id="ref287308"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/photography" class="md-crosslink">photographs</a> can be digitized by scanning or transferred from film to a compact digital disc holding more than 100 images. A recent development, the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/digital-camera" class="md-crosslink autoxref">digital camera</a>, makes it possible to bypass the film/paper step completely by capturing the image into the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/camera" class="md-crosslink autoxref">camera’s</a> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/RAM-computing" class="md-crosslink autoxref">random-access memory</a> or a special diskette and then transferring it to a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/personal-computer" class="md-crosslink autoxref">personal computer</a>. Since both technologies produce a graphics file, in either case the image is editable by means of suitable software.</p><!--[P40]--><p>The digital <span id="ref287309"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/sound-recording" class="md-crosslink">recording</a> of sound is important because speech is the most frequently used natural carrier of communicable information. Direct capture of sound into personal computers is accomplished by means of a <span id="ref287310"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/digital-signal-processor" class="md-crosslink">digital signal processor</a> (DSP) <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-chip" class="md-crosslink autoxref">chip</a>, a special-purpose device built into the computer to perform array-processing operations. Conversion of analog audio signals to digital recordings is a commonplace process that has been used for years by the telecommunications and entertainment industries. Although the resulting digital sound track can be edited, automatic speech recognition—analogous to the recognition of characters and words in text by means of optical character recognition—is still under development. When perfected, voice recognition is certain to have a tremendous impact on the way humans communicate with recorded information, with computers, and among themselves.</p><!--[P41]--><p>By the beginning of the 1990s, the technology to record (or convert), store in digital form, and edit all visually and aurally perceived signals—<span id="ref287311"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/electronic-publishing" class="md-crosslink">text</a>, graphics, still images, animation, motion video, and sound—had thus become available and affordable. These capabilities opened a way for a new kind of multimedia document that employs print, video, and sound to generate more powerful and colourful messages, communicate them securely at electronic speeds, and allow them to be modified almost at will. The traditional business letter, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/newspaper" class="md-crosslink autoxref">newspaper</a>, journal, and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/book-publication" class="md-crosslink autoxref">book</a> will no longer be the same.</p><!--[P42]--></section></section><section id="ref61657" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Inventory of recorded information</h2><p>The development of recording media and techniques enabled society to begin building a store of human knowledge. The idea of collecting and organizing written records is thought to have originated in <span id="ref287314"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Sumer" class="md-crosslink">Sumer</a> about 5,000 years ago; <span id="ref287315"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/ancient-Egypt" class="md-crosslink">Egyptian</a> writing was introduced soon after. Early collections of Sumerian and Egyptian writings, recorded in cuneiform on clay tablets and in hieroglyphic script on papyrus, contained <span id="ref287312"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/information-system" class="md-crosslink">information</a> about legal and economic transactions. In these and other early <span id="ref287313"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/document" class="md-crosslink">document</a> collections (e.g., those of <span id="ref287316"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/China" class="md-crosslink">China</a> produced during the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Shang-dynasty" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Shang dynasty</a> in the 2nd millennium <span class="text-smallcaps">bc</span> and <span id="ref287317"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Buddhism" class="md-crosslink">Buddhist</a> collections in India dating to the 5th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bc</span>), it is difficult to separate the concepts of the archive and the library.</p><!--[P43]--><p>From the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Middle-East" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Middle East</a> the concept of document collections penetrated the Greco-Roman world. Roman kings institutionalized the population and property census as early as the 6th century <span class="text-smallcaps">bc</span>. The great <span id="ref287318"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Library-of-Alexandria" class="md-crosslink">Library of Alexandria</a>, established in the 3rd century <span class="text-smallcaps">bc</span>, is best known as a large collection of papyri containing inventories of property, taxes, and other payments by citizens to their rulers and to each other. It is, in short, the ancient equivalent of today’s administrative information systems.</p><!--[P44]--><p>The scholarly splendour of the Islamic world from the 8th to the 13th century <span class="text-smallcaps">ad</span> can in large part be attributed to the maintenance of public and private book libraries. The <span id="ref287319"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Bayt-al-Hikmah" class="md-crosslink">Bayt al-Ḥikmah</a> (“House of Wisdom”), founded in <span class="text-smallcaps">ad</span> 830 in Baghdad, contained a public library with a large collection of materials on a wide range of subjects, and the 10th-century library of Caliph al-<span id="ref287320"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/al-Hakam-II" class="md-crosslink">Ḥakam</a> in Cordova, Spain, boasted more than 400,000 books.</p><!--[P45]--><span id="ref61657" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61658" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Primary and secondary literature</h2><p>The late but rapid development of European libraries from the 16th century on followed the invention of printing from movable type, which spurred the growth of the printing and publishing industries. Since the beginning of the 17th century, <span id="ref287321"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/art/literature" class="md-crosslink">literature</a> has become the principal medium for <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disseminating" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="disseminating">disseminating</a> knowledge. The phrase <span id="ref287322"></span>primary literature is used to designate original information in various printed formats: newspapers, monographs, conference proceedings, learned and trade journals, reports, patents, bulletins, and newsletters. The <span id="ref287323"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/scholarly-journal" class="md-crosslink">scholarly journal</a>, the classic medium of scientific communication, first appeared in 1665. Three hundred years later the number of periodical titles published in the world was estimated at more than 60,000, reflecting not only growth in the number of practitioners of science and expansion of its body of knowledge through specialization but also a maturing of the system of rewards that encourages scientists to publish.</p><!--[P46]--><p>The sheer quantity of printed information has for some time prevented any individual from fully absorbing even a minuscule fraction of it. Such devices as tables of contents, summaries, and indexes of various types, which aid in identifying and locating relevant information in primary literature, have been in use since the 16th century and led to the development of what is termed <span id="ref287324"></span>secondary literature during the 19th century. The purpose of secondary literature is to “filter” the primary information sources, usually by subject area, and provide the indicators to this literature in the form of reviews, <span id="ref287325"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/abstract-document" class="md-crosslink">abstracts</a>, and <span id="ref287326"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/index-information-retrieval" class="md-crosslink">indexes</a>. Over the past 100 years there has evolved a system of disciplinary, national, and international abstracting and indexing services that acts as a gateway to several attributes of primary literature: authors, subjects, publishers, dates (and languages) of publication, and citations. The professional activity associated with these access-facilitating tools is called documentation.</p><!--[P47]--><p>The quantity of printed materials also makes it impossible, as well as undesirable, for any institution to acquire and house more than a small portion of it. The husbanding of recorded information has become a matter of public policy, as many countries have established national <span id="ref287327"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/library" class="md-crosslink">libraries</a> and archives to direct the orderly acquisition of analog-form documents and records. Since these institutions alone are not able to keep up with the output of such documents and records, new forms of cooperative planning and sharing recorded materials are evolving—namely, public and private, national and regional library networks and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/consortia" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="consortia">consortia</a>.</p><!--[P48]--></section><span id="ref61657" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61659" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref212097"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/database" class="md-crosslink">Databases</a></h2><p>The emergence of <span id="ref730195"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/digital-information" class="md-crosslink">digital technology</a> in the mid-20th century has affected humankind’s inventory of recorded information dramatically. During the early 1960s <span id="ref287328"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer" class="md-crosslink">computers</a> were used to digitize text for the first time; the purpose was to reduce the cost and time required to publish two American abstracting journals, the <em>Index Medicus</em> of the National Library of Medicine and the <em>Scientific and Technical Aerospace Reports</em> of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/NASA" class="md-crosslink autoxref">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA). By the late 1960s such bodies of digitized alphanumeric information, known as <span id="ref212096"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/bibliography" class="md-crosslink">bibliographic</a> and numeric databases, <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constituted" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="constituted">constituted</a> a new type of information resource. This resource is husbanded outside the traditional repositories of information (libraries and archives) by <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/database" class="md-crosslink autoxref">database</a> “vendors.” Advances in computer storage, telecommunications, software for computer sharing, and automated techniques of text indexing and searching fueled the development of an on-line database service industry. Meanwhile, electronic applications to bibliographic control in libraries and archives have led to the development of computerized catalogs and of union catalogs in library networks. They also have resulted in the introduction of <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="comprehensive">comprehensive</a> automation programs in these institutions.</p><!--[P49]--><p>The explosive growth of communications networks after 1990, particularly in the scholarly world, has accelerated the establishment of the “virtual library.” At the leading edge of this development is public-domain information. Residing in thousands of databases distributed worldwide, a growing portion of this vast resource is now accessible almost instantaneously via the <span id="ref212098"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet" class="md-crosslink">Internet</a>, the web of computer networks linking the global <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/communities" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="communities">communities</a> of researchers and, increasingly, nonacademic organizations. Internet resources of electronic information include selected library catalogs, collected works of the literature, some abstracting journals, full-text electronic journals, encyclopaedias, scientific data from numerous disciplines, software archives, <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/demographic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="demographic">demographic</a> registers, daily news summaries, environmental reports, and prices in commodity markets, as well as hundreds of thousands of e-mail and bulletin-board messages.</p><!--[P50]--><p>The vast inventory of recorded information can be useful only if it is systematically organized and if mechanisms exist for locating in it items relevant to human needs. The main approaches for achieving such organization are reviewed in the following section, as are the tools used to retrieve desired information.</p><!--[P51]--></section></section><section id="ref61660" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Organization and retrieval of information</h2><p>In any collection, physical objects are related by order. The ordering may be random or according to some characteristic called a <span id="ref212100"></span>key. Such characteristics may be <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/intrinsic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="intrinsic">intrinsic</a> properties of the objects (e.g., size, weight, shape, or colour), or they may be assigned from some agreed-upon set, such as object class or date of purchase. The values of the key are arranged in a sorting sequence that is dependent on the type of key involved: alphanumeric key values are usually sorted in alphabetic sequence, while other types may be sorted on the basis of similarity in class, such as books on a particular subject or flora of the same genus.</p><!--[P52]--><p>In most cases, order is imposed on a set of information objects for two reasons: to create their inventory and to <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/facilitate" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="facilitate">facilitate</a> locating specific objects in the set. There also exist other, secondary objectives for selecting a particular ordering, as, for example, conservation of space or economy of effort in fetching objects. Unless the objects in a collection are replicated, any ordering scheme is one-dimensional and unable to meet all the functions of ordering with equal effectiveness. The main approach for overcoming some of the limitations of one-dimensional ordering of recorded information relies on extended description of its content and, for analog-form information, of some features of the physical items. This approach employs various tools of content analysis that subsequently facilitate accessing and searching recorded information.</p><!--[P53]--><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61661" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Description and <span id="ref212101"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/content-analysis" class="md-crosslink">content analysis</a> of analog-form records</h2><p>The collections of libraries and archives, the primary repositories of analog-form information, constitute one-dimensional ordering of physical materials in print (documents), in image form (maps and photographs), or in audio-video format (recordings and videotapes). To break away from the confines of one-dimensional ordering, librarianship has developed an extensive set of attributes in terms of which it describes each item in the collection. The rules for assigning these attributes are called <span id="ref212102"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/library-catalog" class="md-crosslink">cataloging</a> rules. <span id="ref212103"></span>Descriptive cataloging is the extraction of bibliographic elements (author names, title, publisher, date of publication, etc.) from each item; the assignment of subject categories or headings to such items is termed <span id="ref212104"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/subject-catalog" class="md-crosslink">subject cataloging</a>.</p><!--[P54]--><p>Conceptually, the library catalog is a table or matrix in which each row describes a discrete physical item and each column provides values of the assigned key. When such a catalog is represented digitally in a computer, any attribute can serve as the ordering key. By sorting the catalog on different keys, it is possible to produce a variety of indexes as well as subject bibliographies. More important, any of the attributes of a computerized catalog becomes a search key (access point) to the collection, surpassing the utility of the traditional card catalog.</p><!--[P55]--><p>The most useful access key to analog-form items is subject. The extensive lists of subject headings of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/library-classification" class="md-crosslink autoxref">library classification</a> schemes provide, however, only a gross access <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/tool" class="md-crosslink autoxref">tool</a> to the content of the items. A technique called <span id="ref212105"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/index-information-retrieval" class="md-crosslink">indexing</a> provides a refinement over library subject headings. It consists of extracting from the item or assigning to it subject and other “descriptors”—words or phrases denoting significant concepts (topics, names) that occur in or characterize the content of the record. Indexing frequently accompanies abstracting, a technique for condensing the full text of a document into a short summary that contains its main ideas (but invariably incurs an information loss and often introduces a bias). Computer-printed, indexed abstracting journals provide a means of keeping users informed of primary information sources.</p><!--[P56]--></section><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61662" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Description and content analysis of digital-form information</h2><p>The description of an electronic document generally follows the principles of bibliographic cataloging if the document is part of a database that is expected to be accessed directly and individually. When the database is an element of a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/universe" class="md-crosslink autoxref">universe</a> of globally distributed database servers that are searchable in parallel, the matter of document naming is considerably more challenging, because several complexities are introduced. The document description must include the name of the database server—i.e., its physical location. Because database servers may delete particular documents, the description must also contain a pointer to the document’s logical address (the generating organization). In contrast to their usefulness in the descriptive cataloging of analog documents, physical attributes such as format and size are highly variable in the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/milieu" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="milieu">milieu</a> of electronic documents and therefore are meaningless in a universal document-naming scheme. On the other hand, the data type of the document (text, sound, etc.) is critical to its transmission and use. Perhaps the most challenging design is the “living document”—a constantly changing pastiche consisting of sections electronically copied from different documents, interspersed with original narrative or graphics or voice comments contributed by persons in distant locations, whose different versions reside on different servers. Efforts are under way to standardize the naming of documents in the universe of electronic networks.</p><!--[P57]--><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61663" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref212107"></span>Machine indexing</h2><p>The subject analysis of electronic text is accomplished by means of machine indexing, using one of two approaches: the assignment of subject descriptors from an unlimited vocabulary (<span id="ref212108"></span>free indexing) or their assignment from a list of authorized descriptors (<span id="ref212109"></span>controlled indexing). A collection of authorized descriptors is called an authority list or, if it also displays various relationships among descriptors such as <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/hierarchy" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="hierarchy">hierarchy</a> or synonymy, a thesaurus. The result of the indexing process is a computer file known as an inverted index, which is an alphabetic listing of descriptors and the addresses of their occurrence in the document body.</p><!--[P58]--><p><span id="ref212110"></span>Full-text indexing, the use of every character string (word of a natural language) in the text as an index term, is an extreme case of free-text indexing: each word in the document (except function words such as articles and prepositions) becomes an access point to it. Used earlier for the generation of concordances in literary analysis and other computer applications in the humanities, full-text indexing placed great demands on computer storage because the resulting index is at least as large as the body of the text. With decreasing cost of mass storage, automatic full-text indexing capability has been incorporated routinely into state-of-the-art information-management software.</p><!--[P59]--><p>Text indexing may be supplemented by other syntactic techniques so as to increase its precision or robustness. One such method, the <span id="ref212111"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/SGML" class="md-crosslink">Standard Generalized Markup Language</a> (SGML), takes advantage of standard text markers used by editors to pinpoint the location and other characteristics of document elements (paragraphs and tables, for example). In indexing <span id="ref212112"></span>spatial data such as maps and astronomical images, the textual index specifies the search areas, each of which is further described by a set of coordinates defining a rectangle or irregular polygon. These digital spatial document attributes are then used to retrieve and display a specific point or a selected region of the document. There are other specialized techniques that may be employed to augment the indexing of specific document types, such as encyclopaedias, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/e-mail" class="md-crosslink autoxref">electronic mail</a>, catalogs, bulletin boards, tables, and maps.</p><!--[P60]--></section><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61664" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref212114"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/semantics" class="md-crosslink">Semantic</a> content analysis</h2><p>The analysis of digitally recorded <span id="ref212113"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/natural-language-processing-computer-science" class="md-crosslink">natural-language</a> information from the semantic viewpoint is a matter of considerable complexity, and it lies at the foundation of such <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/incipient" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="incipient">incipient</a> applications as automatic question answering from a database or retrieval by means of unrestricted natural-language queries. The general approach has been that of computational linguistics: to derive representations of the syntactic and semantic relations between the linguistic elements of sentences and larger parts of the document. Syntactic relations are described by <span id="ref212115"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/parsing" class="md-crosslink">parsing</a> (decomposing) the grammar of sentences (<span data-assembly-id="2010" class="md-link media-overlay-link">Figure 3</span>). For semantic representation, three related formalisms dominate. In a so-called <span id="ref212116"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/semantic-network" class="md-crosslink">semantic network</a>, conceptual entities such as objects, actions, or events are represented as a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/graph-mathematics" class="md-crosslink autoxref">graph</a> of linked nodes (<span data-assembly-id="2011" class="md-link media-overlay-link">Figure 4</span>). “Frames” represent, in a similar graph network, physical or abstract attributes of objects and in a sense define the objects. In “scripts,” events and actions rather than objects are defined in terms of their attributes.</p><!--[P61]--><ul class="assemblies medialist" data-type="other"><li><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="2010"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/91/1691-004-A0DB63F7.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/91/1691-004-A0DB63F7.jpg" alt="A parsing graph." data-width="497" data-height="281"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">A parsing graph.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></li><li><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="2011"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/92/1692-004-687FBF60.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/92/1692-004-687FBF60.jpg" alt="A semantic network representation." data-width="588" data-height="332"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">A semantic network representation.</span><cite>C.S. Shapiro, "Natural Language Processing,"; from A. Ralston and E.D. Reilly, Jr. (eds.), <em>Encyclopedia of Computer Science and Engineering</em>, Van Nostrand Reinhold, 1983</cite></figcaption></figure></li></ul><!--[ML61]--><p>Indexing and linguistic analyses of text generate a relatively gross measure of the semantic relationship, or subject similarity, of documents in a given collection. Subject similarity is, however, a pragmatic phenomenon that varies with the observer and the circumstances of an observation (purpose, time, and so forth). A technique experimented with briefly in the mid-1960s, which assigned to each document one or more “roles” (functions) and one or more “links” (pointers to other documents having the same or a similar role), showed potential for a pragmatic measure of similarity; its use, however, was too unwieldy for the computing environment of the day. Some 20 years later, a similar technique became popular under the name <span id="ref212117"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hypertext" class="md-crosslink">“hypertext.”</a> In this technique, documents that a person or a group of persons consider related (by concept, sequence, hierarchy, experience, motive, or other characteristics) are connected via <span id="ref212118"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hyperlink" class="md-crosslink">“hyperlinks,”</a> mimicking the way humans associate ideas. Objects so linked need not be only text; speech and music, graphics and images, and animation and video can all be interlinked into a <span id="ref212119"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hypermedia" class="md-crosslink">“hypermedia”</a> database. The objects are stored with their hyperlinks, and a user can easily navigate the network of associations by clicking with a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/mouse-computer-device" class="md-crosslink autoxref">mouse</a> on a series of entries on a computer screen. Another technique that elicits semantic relationships from a body of text is SGML.</p><!--[P62]--></section><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61665" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Image analysis</h2><p>The content analysis of images is accomplished by two primary methods: <span id="ref212120"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/image-processing" class="md-crosslink">image processing</a> and <span id="ref212121"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/pattern-recognition-computer-science" class="md-crosslink">pattern recognition</a>. Image processing is a set of computational techniques for analyzing, <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhancing" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="enhancing">enhancing</a>, compressing, and reconstructing images. Pattern recognition is an information-reduction process: the assignment of visual or logical patterns to classes based on the features of these patterns and their relationships. The stages in pattern recognition involve <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/measurement" class="md-crosslink autoxref">measurement</a> of the object to identify distinguishing attributes, extraction of features for the defining attributes, and assignment of the object to a class based on these features. Both image processing and pattern recognition have extensive applications in various areas, including astronomy, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/drug-chemical-agent" class="md-crosslink autoxref">medicine</a>, industrial robotics, and remote sensing by satellites.</p><!--[P63]--></section><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61666" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Speech analysis</h2><p>The immediate objective of content analysis of digital speech is the conversion of discrete sound elements into their alphanumeric equivalents. Once so represented, <span id="ref212123"></span>speech can be subjected to the same techniques of content analysis as natural-language text—i.e., indexing and linguistic analysis. Converting speech elements into their alphanumeric counterparts is an intriguing problem because the “shape” of speech sounds embodies a wide range of many acoustic characteristics and because the linguistic elements of speech are not clearly distinguishable from one another. The technique used in speech processing is to classify the spectral representations of sound and to match the resulting digital spectrographs against prestored “templates” so as to identify the alphanumeric equivalent of the sound. (The obverse of this technique, the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/digital-to-analog-conversion" class="md-crosslink autoxref">digital-to-analog conversion</a> of such templates into sound, is a relatively straightforward approach to generating <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/synthetic" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="synthetic">synthetic</a> speech.)</p><!--[P64]--><p>Speech processing is complex as well as expensive in terms of storage capacity and computational requirements. State-of-the-art <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/speech-recognition" class="md-crosslink autoxref">speech recognition</a> systems can identify limited vocabularies and parts of distinctly spoken speech and can be programmed to recognize tonal idiosyncracies of individual speakers. When more <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/robust" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="robust">robust</a> and reliable techniques become available and the process is made computationally tractable (as is expected with parallel computers), humans will be able to interact with computers via spoken commands and queries on a routine basis. In many situations this may make the keyboard obsolete as a data-entry device.</p><!--[P65]--></section></section><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61667" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Storage structures for digital-form information</h2><p>Digital information is <span id="ref730215"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/storage-computing" class="md-crosslink">stored</a> in complex patterns that make it <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/feasible" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="feasible">feasible</a> to address and operate on even the smallest element of symbolic expression, as well as on larger strings such as words or sentences and on images and sound.</p><!--[P66]--><p>From the viewpoint of digital information storage, it is useful to distinguish between <span id="ref212125"></span>“structured” data, such as inventories of objects that can be represented by short symbol strings and numbers, and <span id="ref212126"></span>“unstructured” data, such as the natural-language text of documents or pictorial images. The principal objective of all storage structures is to facilitate the processing of data elements on the basis of their relationships; the structures thus vary with the type of relationship they represent. The choice of a particular storage structure is governed by the relevance of the relationships it allows to be represented to the information-processing requirements of the task or system at hand.</p><!--[P67]--><p>In information systems whose store consists of unstructured databases of natural-language records, the objective is to retrieve records (or portions thereof) on the basis of the presence in the records of words or short phrases that constitute the query. Since there exists an index as a separate file that provides information about the locations of words and phrases in the database records, the relationships that are of interest (e.g., word adjacency) can be calculated from the index. Consequently, the database text itself can be stored as a simple ordered sequential file of records. The majority of the computations use the index, and they access the text file only to pull out the records or those portions that satisfy the result of the computations. The sequential file structure remains popular, with document-retrieval software intended for use with personal computers and CD-ROM databases.</p><!--[P68]--><p>When relationships between data elements need to be represented as part of the records so as to make more efficient the desired operations on these records, two types of “chained” <span id="ref212127"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/file-structure" class="md-crosslink">structures</a> are commonly used: hierarchical and network. In the <span id="ref212128"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hierarchical-file-structure" class="md-crosslink">hierarchical file structure</a>, records are arranged in a scheme resembling a family tree, with records related to one another from top to bottom. In the <span id="ref212129"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/network-file-structure" class="md-crosslink">network file structure</a>, records are arranged in groupings known as sets; these can be connected in any number of ways, giving rise to considerable flexibility. In both hierarchical and network structures, the relationships are shown by means of <span id="ref212130"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/pointer-computing" class="md-crosslink">“pointers”</a> (i.e., identifiers such as addresses or keys) that become part of the records.</p><!--[P69]--><p>Another type of database storage structure, the <span id="ref212131"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/relational-database" class="md-crosslink">relational structure</a>, has become increasingly popular since the late 1970s. Its major advantage over the hierarchical and network structures is the ability to handle unanticipated data relationships without pointers. Relational storage structures are two-dimensional tables consisting of rows and columns, much like the conceptual library catalog mentioned above. The elegance of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/relational-database" class="md-crosslink autoxref">relational model</a> lies in its conceptual simplicity, the availability of theoretical underpinnings (relational algebra), and the ability of its associated software to handle data relationships without the use of pointers. The relational model was initially used for databases containing highly structured information. In the 1990s it largely replaced the hierarchical and network models, and it also became the model of choice for large-scale information-management applications, both textual and multimedia.</p><!--[P70]--><p>The feasibility of storing large volumes of full text on an economical medium (the digital optical disc) has renewed interest in the study of storage structures that permit more powerful retrieval and processing techniques to operate on cognitive entities other than words, to facilitate more extensive semantic content and <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="context">context</a> analysis, and to organize text conceptually into logical units rather than those dictated by printing conventions.</p><!--[P71]--></section><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61668" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref212132"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/query-language" class="md-crosslink">Query languages</a></h2><p>The uses of databases are manifold. They provide a means of retrieving records or parts of records and performing various calculations before displaying the results. The interface by which such manipulations are specified is called the query language. Whereas early query languages were originally so complex that interacting with electronic databases could be done only by specially trained individuals, recent interfaces are more user-friendly, allowing casual users to access database information.</p><!--[P72]--><p>The main types of popular query modes are the <span id="ref212133"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/menu" class="md-crosslink">menu,</a> the “fill-in-the-blank” technique, and the structured query. Particularly suited for novices, the menu requires a person to choose from several <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/alternatives" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="alternatives">alternatives</a> displayed on the video terminal screen. The <span id="ref212134"></span>fill-in-the-blank technique is one in which the user is prompted to enter key words as search statements. The structured query approach is effective with relational databases. It has a formal, powerful <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/syntax" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="syntax">syntax</a> that is in fact a programming language, and it is able to accommodate logical operators. One implementation of this approach, the <span id="ref212135"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/SQL" class="md-crosslink">Structured Query Language</a> (SQL), has the form</p><!--[P73]--><p><em>select</em> [field Fa, Fb, . . ., Fn]</p><!--[P74]--><p><em>from</em> [database Da, Db, . . ., Dn]</p><!--[P75]--><p><em>where</em> [field Fa = abc] <em>and</em> [field Fb = def].</p><!--[P76]--><p>Structured query languages support database searching and other operations by using commands such as “find,” “delete,” “print,” “sum,” and so forth. The sentencelike structure of an SQL query resembles natural language except that its syntax is limited and fixed. Instead of using an SQL statement, it is possible to represent queries in tabular form. The technique, referred to as <span id="ref212136"></span>query-by-example (or QBE), displays an empty tabular form and expects the searcher to enter the search specifications into appropriate columns. The program then constructs an SQL-type query from the table and executes it.</p><!--[P77]--><p>The most flexible query language is of course natural language. The use of <span id="ref212137"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/natural-language-processing-computer-science" class="md-crosslink">natural-language</a> sentences in a constrained form to search databases is allowed by some commercial database management software. These programs parse the syntax of the query; recognize its action words and their synonyms; identify the names of files, records, and fields; and perform the logical operations required. Experimental systems that accept such natural-language queries in spoken voice have been developed; however, the ability to employ unrestricted natural language to query unstructured information will require further advances in machine understanding of natural language, particularly in techniques of representing the semantic and pragmatic context of ideas. The prospect of an intelligent conversation between humans and a large store of digitally encoded knowledge is not <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/imminent" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="imminent">imminent</a>.</p><!--[P78]--></section><span id="ref61660" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61669" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Information <span id="ref730216"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/searching" class="md-crosslink">searching</a> and retrieval</h2><p>State-of-the-art approaches to retrieving information employ two generic techniques: (1) matching words in the query against the database index (<span id="ref212138"></span>key-word searching) and (2) <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/traversing" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="traversing">traversing</a> the database with the aid of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hypertext" class="md-crosslink autoxref">hypertext</a> or hypermedia links.</p><!--[P79]--><p>Key-word searches can be made either more general or more narrow in scope by means of logical operators (e.g., disjunction and conjunction). Because of the semantic <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ambiguities" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="ambiguities">ambiguities</a> involved in free-text indexing, however, the precision of the key-word retrieval technique—that is, the percentage of relevant documents correctly retrieved from a collection—is far from ideal, and various modifications have been introduced to improve it. In one such enhancement, the search output is sorted by degree of relevance, based on a statistical match between the key words in the query and in the document; in another, the program automatically generates a new query using one or more documents considered relevant by the user. Key-word searching has been the dominant approach to text retrieval since the early 1960s; hypertext has so far been largely confined to personal or corporate information-retrieval applications.</p><!--[P80]--><p>The exponential growth of the use of <span id="ref730135"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-network" class="md-crosslink">computer networks</a> in the 1990s presages significant changes in systems and techniques of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/information-retrieval" class="md-crosslink autoxref">information retrieval</a>. In a wide-area information service, a number of which began operating at the beginning of the 1990s on the Internet <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-network" class="md-crosslink autoxref">computer network</a>, a user’s personal computer or terminal (called a <span id="ref212139"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/client" class="md-crosslink">client</a>) can search simultaneously a number of databases maintained on <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/heterogeneous" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="heterogeneous">heterogeneous</a> computers (called <span id="ref212140"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/server" class="md-crosslink">servers</a>). The latter are located at different geographic sites, and their databases contain different data types and often use incompatible data formats. The simultaneous, distributed search is possible because clients and servers agree on a standard document addressing scheme and adopt a common communications <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/protocol" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="protocol">protocol</a> that accommodates all the data types and formats used by the servers. Communication with other wide-area services using different <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/protocols" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="protocols">protocols</a> is accomplished by routing through so-called <span id="ref212141"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/gateway-computing" class="md-crosslink">gateways</a> capable of protocol translation. The architecture of a typical networked information system is illustrated in <span data-assembly-id="2015" class="md-link media-overlay-link">Figure 5</span>. Several representative clients are shown: a “dumb” terminal (i.e., one with no internal processor), a personal computer (PC), a Macintosh (Mac), and a NeXT machine. They have access to data on the servers sharing a common protocol as well as to data provided by services that require protocol conversion via the gateways. Network news is such a wide-area service, containing hundreds of news groups on a variety of subjects, by which users can read and post messages.</p><!--[P81]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="image" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="2015"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/93/1693-004-31D3F0C9.jpg" big-src="https://cdn.britannica.com/93/1693-004-31D3F0C9.jpg" alt="The architecture of a networked information system." data-width="630" data-height="370"></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">The architecture of a networked information system.</span><cite>Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc.</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML81]--><p>Evolving information-retrieval techniques, exemplified by an experimental interface to the NASA <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/space-shuttle" class="md-crosslink autoxref">space shuttle</a> reference manual, combine natural language, hyperlinks, and key-word searching. Other techniques, seeking higher levels of retrieval precision and effectiveness, are studied by researchers involved with <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/artificial-intelligence" class="md-crosslink autoxref">artificial intelligence</a> and neural networks. The next major milestone may be a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-program" class="md-crosslink autoxref">computer program</a> that <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/traverses" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="traverses">traverses</a> the seamless information universe of wide-area electronic networks and continuously filters its contents through profiles of organizational and personal interest: the information robot of the 21st century.</p><!--[P82]--></section></section><section id="ref61670" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Information <span id="ref212142"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/display-information-recording" class="md-crosslink">display</a></h2><p>For humans to perceive and understand information, it must be presented as print and image on paper; as print and image on film or on a video terminal; as sound via radio or telephony; as print, sound, and video in motion pictures, on television broadcasts, or at lectures and conferences; or in face-to-face encounters. Except for live encounters and audio information, such displays emanate increasingly from digitally stored data, with the output media being video, print, and sound.</p><!--[P83]--><span id="ref61670" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61671" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Video</h2><p>Possibly the most widely used <span id="ref212143"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/video-display-terminal" class="md-crosslink">video display</a> device, at least in the industrialized world, is the <span id="ref212144"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/television-technology" class="md-crosslink">television</a> set. Designed primarily for video and sound, its image resolution is inadequate for alphanumeric data except in relatively small amounts. Use of the television set in text-oriented information systems has been limited to menu-oriented applications such as videotex, in which information is selected from hierarchically arranged menus (with the aid of a numeric keyboard attachment) and displayed in fixed frames. The television, computer, and communications technologies are, however, converging in a high-resolution digital television set capable of receiving alphanumeric, video, and audio signals.</p><!--[P84]--><p>The computer video terminal is today’s <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ubiquitous" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="ubiquitous">ubiquitous</a> interface that transforms computer-stored data into analog form for human viewing. The two basic apparatuses used are the <span id="ref212145"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/cathode-ray-tube" class="md-crosslink">cathode-ray tube</a> (CRT) and the more recent <span id="ref212146"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/flat-panel-display" class="md-crosslink">flat-panel display</a>. In CRT displays an <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/electron-gun" class="md-crosslink autoxref">electron gun</a> emits beams of electrons on a phosphorus-coated surface; the beams are deflected, forming visible patterns representative of data. Flat-panel displays use one of four different media for visual representation of data: <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/liquid-crystal" class="md-crosslink autoxref">liquid crystal</a>, light-emitting diodes, plasma panels, and electroluminescence. Advanced video display systems enable the user to scroll, page, zoom (change the scale of the details of the display image for enhancement), divide the screen into multiple colours and windows (viewing areas), and in some cases even activate commands by touching the screen instead of using the keyboard. The information capacity of the terminal screen depends on its resolution, which ranges from low (character-addressable) to high (bit-addressable). High resolution is indispensable for the display of graphic and video data in state-of-the-art workstations, such as those used in engineering or information systems design.</p><!--[P85]--></section><span id="ref61670" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61672" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3"><span id="ref212147"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/printing-publishing" class="md-crosslink">Print</a></h2><p>Modern society continues to be dominated by printed information. The convenience and portability of print on paper make it difficult to imagine the paperless world that some have predicted. The generation of paper print has changed considerably, however. Although manual typesetting is still practiced for artwork, in special situations, and in some developing countries, electronic means of composing pages for subsequent reproduction by photoduplication and other methods has become commonplace.</p><!--[P86]--><p>Since the 1960s, volume <span id="ref212148"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/publishing" class="md-crosslink">publishing</a> has become an automated process using large computers and high-speed printers to transfer digitally stored data on paper. The appearance of microcomputer-based publishing systems has proved to be another significant advance. Economical enough to allow even small organizations to become in-house publishers, these so-called <span id="ref212149"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/desktop-publishing" class="md-crosslink">desktop publishing</a> systems are able to format text and graphics interactively on a high-resolution video screen with the aid of page-description command languages. Once a page has been formatted, the entire image is transferred to an electronic printing or photocomposition device.</p><!--[P87]--><span id="ref61670" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61673" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4"><span id="ref212150"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/computer-printer" class="md-crosslink">Printers</a></h2><p>Computer printers are commonly divided into two general classes according to the way they produce images on paper: <span id="ref212151"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/impact-printer" class="md-crosslink">impact</a> and nonimpact. In the first type, images are formed by the print mechanism making contact with the paper through an ink-coated ribbon. The mechanism consists either of print hammers shaped like characters or of a print head containing a row of pins that produce a pattern of dots in the form of characters or other images.</p><!--[P88]--><p>Most <span id="ref212152"></span>nonimpact printers form images from a matrix of dots, but they employ different techniques for transferring images to paper. The most popular type, the <span id="ref212153"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/laser-printer" class="md-crosslink">laser printer</a>, uses a beam of laser light and a system of optical components to etch images on a photoconductor drum from which they are carried via electrostatic photocopying to paper. <span id="ref212154"></span>Light-emitting diode (LED) printers resemble laser printers in operation but direct light from energized diodes rather than a laser onto a photoconductive surface. <span id="ref212155"></span>Ion-deposition printers make use of technology similar to that of photocopiers for producing electrostatic images. Another type of nonimpact printer, the <span id="ref212156"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/ink-jet-printer" class="md-crosslink">ink-jet printer</a>, sprays electrically charged drops of ink onto the print surface.</p><!--[P89]--></section><span id="ref61670" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61674" data-level="4"><h2 class="h4">Microfilm and microfiche</h2><p>Alphanumeric and image information can be transferred from digital computer storage directly to film. Reel <span id="ref212157"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/microfilm" class="md-crosslink">microfilm</a> and <span id="ref212158"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/microfiche" class="md-crosslink">microfiche</a> (a flat sheet of film containing multiple microimages reduced from the original) were popular methods of document storage and reproduction for several decades. During the 1990s they were largely replaced by optical disc technology (<em>see above</em> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/information-processing#ref61655" class="md-crosslink">Recording media</a>).</p><!--[P90]--><div class="assemblies"><figure data-type="video" class="md-assembly  media-overlay-link print-false" data-assembly-id="191521"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="video"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/700x450/35/179435-049-B6A60652.jpg" alt="microfilm" data-width="426" data-height="240"></div></div><figcaption><strong class="md-assembly-title">microfilm</strong><span class="md-assembly-caption">An overview of microfilm, including a discussion of the Barbarastollen archive bunker near Freiburg im Breisgau, Germany.</span><cite>Contunico © ZDF Enterprises GmbH, Mainz</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[ML90]--></section></section><span id="ref61670" data-level="2"></span><section id="ref61675" data-level="3"><h2 class="h3">Voice</h2><p>In synthetic <span id="ref212159"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/speech-synthesizer" class="md-crosslink">speech</a> generation, digitally prestored sound elements are converted to analog sound signals and combined to form words and sentences. Digital-to-analog converters are available as inexpensive boards for microcomputers or as software for larger machines. Human speech is the most effective natural form of communication, and so applications of this technology are becoming increasingly popular in situations where there are numerous requests for specific information (e.g., time, travel, and entertainment), where there is a need for repetitive instruction, in electronic <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/voice-mail" class="md-crosslink autoxref">voice mail</a> (the counterpart of electronic text mail), and in toys.</p><!--[P91]--></section></section><section id="ref218046" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Dissemination of information</h2><p>The process of recording information by handwriting was obviously laborious and required the dedication of the likes of Egyptian scribes or monks in monasteries around the world. It was only after mechanical means of reproducing writing were invented that information records could be duplicated more efficiently and economically.</p><!--[P92]--><p>The first practical method of reproducing writing mechanically was <span id="ref212161"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/block-printing" class="md-crosslink">block printing</a>; it was developed in <span id="ref212162"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/China" class="md-crosslink">China</a> during the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Tang-dynasty" class="md-crosslink autoxref">T’ang dynasty</a> (618–907). <span id="ref212163"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/ideogram" class="md-crosslink">Ideographic</a> text and illustrations were engraved in wooden blocks, inked, and copied on paper. Used to produce books as well as cards, charms, and calendars, block printing spread to Korea and Japan but apparently not to the Islamic or European Christian civilizations. European woodcuts and metal engravings date only to the 14th century.</p><!--[P93]--><p>Printing from movable type was also invented in China (in the mid-11th century <span class="text-smallcaps">ad</span>). There and in the bookmaking industry of <span id="ref212164"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Korea" class="md-crosslink">Korea</a>, where the method was applied more extensively during the 15th century, the ideographic type was made initially of baked clay and wood and later of metal. The large number of typefaces required for pictographic text <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/composition" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="composition">composition</a> continued to handicap printing in the Orient until the present time.</p><!--[P94]--><p>The invention of character-oriented printing from movable type (1440–50) is attributed to the German printer <span id="ref212165"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johannes-Gutenberg" class="md-crosslink">Johannes Gutenberg</a>. Within 30 years of his invention, the movable-type printing press was in use throughout Europe. Character-type pieces were metallic and apparently cast from metallic molds; paper and vellum (calfskin parchment) were used to carry the impressions. Gutenberg’s technique of assembling individual letters by hand was employed until 1886, when the German-born American printer <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ottmar-Mergenthaler" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Ottmar Mergenthaler</a> developed the <span id="ref212166"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Linotype" class="md-crosslink">Linotype</a>, a keyboard-driven device that cast lines of type automatically. Typesetting <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/speed-photography" class="md-crosslink autoxref">speed</a> was further enhanced by the <span id="ref212167"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Monotype-typesetting-machine" class="md-crosslink">Monotype</a> technique, in which a perforated paper ribbon, punched from a keyboard, was used to operate a type-casting machine. Mechanical methods of typesetting prevailed until the 1960s. Since that time they have been largely supplanted by the electronic and optical printing techniques described in the previous section.</p><!--[P95]--><p>Unlike the use of movable type for printing text, early <span id="ref212168"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/art/graphic-art" class="md-crosslink">graphics</a> were reproduced from wood relief engravings in which the nonprinting portions of the image were cut away. Musical scores, on the other hand, were reproduced from etched stone plates. At the end of the 18th century, the German printer <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alois-Senefelder" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Aloys Senefelder</a> developed <span id="ref212169"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/lithography" class="md-crosslink">lithography</a>, a planographic technique of transferring images from a specially prepared surface of stone. In <span id="ref212170"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/offset-printing" class="md-crosslink">offset lithography</a> the image is transferred from zinc or aluminum plates instead of stone, and in <span id="ref212171"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/photoengraving" class="md-crosslink">photoengraving</a> such plates are superimposed with film and then etched.</p><!--[P96]--><p>The first successful photographic process, the <span id="ref212172"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/daguerreotype" class="md-crosslink">daguerreotype</a>, was developed during the 1830s. The invention of <span id="ref212173"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology-of-photography" class="md-crosslink">photography</a>, aside from providing a new medium for capturing still images and later video in analog form, was significant for two other reasons. First, recorded information (textual and graphic) could be easily reproduced from film, and, second, the image could be enlarged or reduced. Document reproduction from film to film has been relatively unimportant, because both printing and photocopying (see above) are cheaper. The ability to reduce images, however, has led to the development of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/microform" class="md-crosslink autoxref">microform</a>, the most economical method of disseminating analog-form information.</p><!--[P97]--><p>Another technique of considerable commercial importance for the duplication of paper-based information is <span id="ref212174"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/photocopying-machine" class="md-crosslink">photocopying</a>, or dry photography. Printing is most economical when large numbers of copies are required, but photocopying provides a fast and efficient means of duplicating records in small quantities for personal or local use. Of the several technologies that are in use, the most popular process, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/xerography" class="md-crosslink autoxref">xerography</a>, is based on electrostatics.</p><!--[P98]--><p>While the volume of information issued in the form of printed matter continues unabated, the <span id="ref212175"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/electronic-publishing" class="md-crosslink">electronic publishing</a> industry has begun to <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/disseminate" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="disseminate">disseminate</a> information in digital form. The digital <span id="ref212176"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/optical-disc" class="md-crosslink">optical</a> disc (see above <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/information-processing#ref61655" class="md-crosslink">Recording media</a>) is developing as an increasingly popular means of issuing large bodies of archival information—for example, legislation, court and hospital records, encyclopaedias and other reference works, referral databases, and libraries of computer software. Full-text databases, each containing digital page images of the complete text of some 400 periodicals stored on <span id="ref212177"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/CD-ROM" class="md-crosslink">CD-ROM</a>, entered the market in 1990. The optical disc provides the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/mass-production" class="md-crosslink autoxref">mass production</a> technology for publication in machine-readable form. It offers the prospect of having large libraries of information available in virtually every school and at many professional workstations.</p><!--[P99]--><p>The coupling of computers and digital <span id="ref212178"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/telecommunication" class="md-crosslink">telecommunications</a> is also changing the modes of information dissemination. High-speed digital satellite communications facilitate electronic printing at remote sites; for example, the world’s major newspapers and magazines transmit electronic page copies to different geographic locations for local printing and distribution. Updates of catalogs, computer software, and archival databases are distributed via e-mail, a method of rapidly forwarding and storing bodies of digital information between remote computers.</p><!--[P100]--><p>Indeed, a large-scale transformation is taking place in modes of formal as well as informal communication. For more than three centuries, formal communication in the scientific <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/community" class="md-dictionary-link" data-term="community">community</a> has relied on the <span id="ref212180"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/scholarly-journal" class="md-crosslink">scholarly</a> and professional periodical, widely distributed to tens of thousands of libraries and to tens of millions of individual subscribers. In 1992 a major international publisher announced that its journals would gradually be available for computer storage in digital form; and in that same year the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/State-University-of-New-York" class="md-crosslink autoxref">State University of New York</a> at Buffalo began building a completely electronic, paperless library. The scholarly article, rather than the journal, is likely to become the basic unit of formal communication in scientific disciplines; digital copies of such an article will be transmitted electronically to subscribers or, more likely, on demand to individuals and organizations who learn of its existence through referral databases and new types of alerting information services. The Internet already offers instantaneous public access to vast resources of noncommercial information stored in computers around the world.</p><!--[P101]--><p>Similarly, the traditional modes of informal communications—various types of face-to-face encounters such as meetings, conferences, seminars, workshops, and classroom lectures—are being supplemented and in some cases replaced by e-mail, <span id="ref212181"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/bulletin-board-system" class="md-crosslink">electronic bulletin boards</a> (a technique of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/broadcasting" class="md-crosslink autoxref">broadcasting</a> newsworthy textual and multimedia messages between computer users), and electronic teleconferencing and distributed problem-solving (a method of linking remote persons in real time by voice-and-image communication and special software called “groupware”). These technologies are forging virtual societal networks—communities of geographically dispersed individuals who have common professional or social interests.</p><!--[P102]--></section></section><!--[END-OF-CONTENT]--></article><div class="md-learn-more extra-content" data-module="learn-more" data-value="learn-more">        <h2>Learn More <span>in these related Britannica articles:</span></h2>        <ul>        <li class="entry">                <a href="/topic/cryptology#ref392497">                    <div class="image-wrapper text-center">                            				<img src="https://cdn.britannica.com/180x300/50/7850-004-C0E8DB7F.jpg"  alt="The Vigenère tableIn encrypting plaintext, the cipher letter is found at the intersection of the column headed by the plaintext letter and the row indexed by the key letter.
