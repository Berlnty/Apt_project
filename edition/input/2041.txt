          <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="69" data-total-count="69" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-1">Does running a marathon push the body further than it is meant to go?</p><aside id="middle-right-paid-post-container" class="ad middle-right-ad paid-post-ad hidden nocontent robots-nocontent">                                <h2 class="marginalia-heading"></h2>                                    <ul class="story-menu">                                        <li id="MiddleRightPaidPost1" class="story-menu-item ad"></li>                                        <li id="MiddleRightPaidPost2" class="story-menu-item ad"></li>                                        <li id="MiddleRightPaidPost3" class="story-menu-item ad"></li>                                        <li id="MiddleRightPaidPost4" class="story-menu-item ad"></li>                                    </ul>                                </aside><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="197" data-total-count="266" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-2">The conventional wisdom is that distance running leads to debilitating wear and tear, especially on the joints. But that hasn’t stopped runners from flocking to starting lines in record numbers.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="382" data-total-count="648" itemprop="articleBody">Last year in the United States, <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://www.runningusa.org/node/16414" title="Article in Running USA.">425,000 marathoners crossed the finish line</a>, an increase of 20 percent from the beginning of the decade, Running USA says. Next week about 40,000 people will take part in the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/subjects/n/new_york_city_marathon/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="More articles about the New York City Marathon." class="meta-classifier">New York City Marathon</a>. Injury rates have also climbed, with <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://cat.inist.fr/?aModele=afficheN&amp;cpsidt=18800273" title="Abstract of a study.">some studies reporting</a> that 90 percent of those who train for the 26.2-mile race sustain injuries in the process.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="331" data-total-count="979" itemprop="articleBody">But now a best-selling book has reframed the debate about the wisdom of distance running. In “Born to Run” (Knopf), Christopher McDougall, an avid runner who had been vexed by injuries, explores the world of the Tarahumara Indians of Mexico, a tribe known for running extraordinary distances in nothing but thin-soled sandals.</p>    <aside class="marginalia related-coverage-marginalia nocontent robots-nocontent" data-marginalia-type="sprinkled" role="complementary" module="RelatedCoverage-Marginalia">        <div class="nocontent robots-nocontent">            <a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-3">Continue reading the main story</a>                <header>        <h2 class="module-heading">                            Related Coverage                    </h2>    </header>            <ul>                                                                                                <li><article class="story theme-summary">    <a class="story-link" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2009/05/27/health/MARATHON_VOICES.html">                <h2 class="story-heading">                            <i class="icon sprite-icon interactive-icon"></i><span class="visually-hidden">interactive</span>                        <span class="story-heading-text">Running Voices</span><time class="dateline" datetime="2009-08-05">AUG. 5, 2009</time>        </h2>    </a></article></li>                                                                                <li><article class="story theme-summary">    <a class="story-link" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://www.nytimes.com/video/health/1247464987589/barefoot-running.html">                    <div class="thumb">                <img src="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812im_/http://static01.nyt.com/images/2009/10/04/health/barefoot_1x1_new.jpg" alt=""/>                <div class="media-action-overlay"><i class="icon sprite-icon video-icon"></i><span class="visually-hidden">video</span></div>            </div>                <h2 class="story-heading">                        <span class="story-heading-text">Barefoot Running</span><time class="dateline" datetime="2009-10-04">OCT. 4, 2009</time>        </h2>    </a></article></li>                                                </ul>        </div><!-- close nocontent -->    </aside><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="258" data-total-count="1237" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-3">Mr. McDougall makes the case that running isn’t inherently risky. Instead, he argues that the commercialization of urban marathons encourages overzealous training, while the promotion of high-tech shoes has led to poor running form and a rash of injuries.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="201" data-total-count="1438" itemprop="articleBody">“The sense of distance running being crazy is something new to late-20th-century America,” Mr. McDougall told me. “It’s only recently that running has become associated with pain and injury.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="366" data-total-count="1804" itemprop="articleBody">The scientific evidence supports the notion that humans evolved to be runners. In <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://www.fas.harvard.edu/~skeleton/pdfs/2007c.pdf" title="Read the paper (PDF).">a 2007 paper in the journal Sports Medicine,</a> Daniel E. Lieberman, a Harvard evolutionary biologist, and Dennis M. Bramble, a biologist at the University of Utah, wrote that several characteristics unique to humans suggested endurance running played an important role in our evolution.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="387" data-total-count="2191" itemprop="articleBody">Most mammals can sprint faster than humans — having four legs gives them the advantage. But when it comes to long distances, humans can outrun almost any animal. Because we cool by <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://health.nytimes.com/health/guides/symptoms/sweating/overview.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="In-depth reference and news articles about Sweating." class="meta-classifier">sweating</a> rather than panting, we can stay cool at speeds and distances that would overheat other animals. On a hot day, the two scientists wrote, a human could even outrun a horse in a 26.2-mile marathon.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="315" data-total-count="2506" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-4">Why would evolution favor the distance runner? The prevailing theory is that endurance running allowed primitive humans to incorporate meat into their <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://health.nytimes.com/health/guides/specialtopic/food-guide-pyramid/overview.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="In-depth reference and news articles about Diet and Nutrition." class="meta-classifier">diet</a>. They may have watched the sky for scavenging birds and then run long distances to reach a fresh kill and steal the meat from whatever animal was there first.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="352" data-total-count="2858" itemprop="articleBody">Other research suggests that before the development of slingshots or bows, early hunters engaged in persistence hunting, chasing an animal for hours until it overheated, making it easy to kill at close range. <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://www.mattmetzgar.com/matt_metzgar/files/persistence_hunting.pdf" title="Read  the paper (PDF).">A 2006 report in the journal Current Anthropology</a> documents persistence hunting among modern hunter-gatherers, including the Bushmen in Africa.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="183" data-total-count="3041" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-5">“Ancient humans exploited the fact that humans are good runners in the heat,” Dr. Bramble said. “We have such a great cooling system” — many sweat glands, little body hair.</p><div id="MiddleRight1" class="ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent"><div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">    <p>Advertisement</p></div><a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-6">Continue reading the main story</a></div>    	<div id="MiddleRightN" class="ad text-ad middle-right-ad nocontent robots-nocontent"><div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">    <p>Advertisement</p></div><a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-6">Continue reading the main story</a></div><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="443" data-total-count="3484" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-6">There is other evidence that evolution favored endurance running. <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://jeb.biologists.org/cgi/content/abstract/212/5/713" title="Read the abstract.">A study in The Journal of Experimental Biology</a> last February showed that the short toes of the human foot allowed for more efficient running, compared with longer-toed animals. Increasing toe length as little as 20 percent doubles the mechanical work of the foot. Even the fact that the big toe is straight, rather than to the side, suggests that our feet evolved for running.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="236" data-total-count="3720" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-7">“The big toe is lined up with the rest, not divergent, the way you see with apes and our closest nonrunning relatives,” Dr. Bramble said. “It’s the main push-off in running: the last thing to leave the ground is that big toe.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="451" data-total-count="4171" itemprop="articleBody">Springlike ligaments and tendons in the feet and legs are crucial for running. (Our close relatives the chimpanzee and the ape don’t have them.) A narrow waist and a midsection that can turn allow us to swing our arms and prevent us from zigzagging on the trail. Humans also have a far more developed sense of balance, an advantage that keeps the head stable as we run. And most humans can store about 20 miles’ worth of glycogen in their muscles.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="298" data-total-count="4469" itemprop="articleBody">And the <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151105211812/http://jeb.biologists.org/cgi/content/abstract/209/11/2143" title="Abstract of a paper by Dr. Lieberman and colleagues.">gluteus maximus</a>, the largest muscle in the human body, is primarily engaged only during running. “Your butt is a running muscle; you barely use it when you walk,” Dr. Lieberman said. “There are so many features in our bodies from our heads to our toes that make us good at running.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="444" data-total-count="4913" itemprop="articleBody">So if we’re born to run, why are runners so often injured? A combination of factors is likely to play a role, experts say. Exercise early in life can affect the development of tendons and muscles, but many people don’t start running until adulthood, so their bodies may not be as well developed for distance. Running on only artificial surfaces and in high-tech shoes can change the biomechanics of running, increasing the risks of injury.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="282" data-total-count="5195" itemprop="articleBody">What’s the solution? Slower, easier training over a long period would most likely help; so would brief walk breaks, which mimic the behavior of the persistence hunter. And running on a variety of surfaces and in simpler shoes with less cushioning can restore natural running form.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="160" data-total-count="5355" itemprop="articleBody">Mr. McDougall says that while researching his book, he corrected his form and stopped using thickly cushioned shoes. He has run without injury for three years.</p>                                                <footer class="story-footer story-content">    <div class="story-meta">                <div
