density">Next</a></li></ol></div><!--Pagination Section 1--><div id="bs-content-rb-bite"><div id="bs-content-rb-abstract"><p>People are unevenly distributed around the world. The difference in distribution is measured by comparing population density - the number of people per square kilometre (km²).</p><p><strong>Population density</strong> is determined more by environmental factors which make an area more or less attractive to settlers than by economic development.</p></div><h1>Population patterns</h1><div class="bs-content-rb-image layout2"><img src="/web/20170623234027im_/http://www.bbc.co.uk/staticarchive/740bfb5c6c452202e36ffa4cf827b7bd88ad9a8c.jpg" alt="A street in Cairo" title="A street in Cairo - photo courtesy of Janarthanan Mills" height="170" width="226"><p class="bs-content-rb-image_caption">A street in Cairo</p></div><p></p><p>The way in which people are spread across a given area is known as <strong>population distribution</strong>. Geographers study population distribution patterns at different scales: local, regional, national, and global.</p><p>Patterns of population distribution tend to be uneven. For example, in the UK there are more people living in south-east England than in Wales.</p><h2>Population density</h2><p>Population density is the average number of people per square kilometre. It is a way of measuring population distribution and shows whether an area is sparsely or densely populated. Population density is calculated using the following formula:</p><div class="bs-content-rb-keyfact"><p>Population density = total population ÷ total land area in km²</p></div></div><!--Pagination
