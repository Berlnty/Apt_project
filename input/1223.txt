class="speakable-p-1 p-text"> </p><p class="speakable-p-2 p-text">Brian Nuno sees opportunities for his company, DTE Energy, and its employees if Detroit's riverfront becomes easier to access. </p><p class="p-text">The company's headquarters campus is a relatively straight shot up Third Avenue from the river, but getting to the riverfront is not a direct endeavor. That could change. A study commissioned by the City of Detroit envisions a major remake of the area — a bigger opportunity created by demolition of the Joe Louis Arena, which closed last year.</p><p class="p-text">The city has a deadline of May 1 to start demolition of the former Red Wings arena, with help from up to $6 million in state loans. A major hotel had been planned for the site, <a href="/story/money/2018/02/26/ex-creditor-sues-city-detroit-sued-more-time-replace-joe-louis-arena-plan/373991002/">although a firm that is supposed to redevelop it </a>has sued for more time to present a plan.</p><p class="p-text">The study envisions numerous changes, such as creating a continuous riverwalk, improving east-west connectivity, boosting pedestrian access to the riverfront and creating a "holistic development framework."</p><div class="partner-placement partner-spike"></div><p class="p-text">The combination of the Lodge Freeway, Joe Louis Arena and the "gerbil tubes" formerly used by countless concertgoers and hockey fans heading to and from their vehicles creates a clear barrier to a riverfront that has become an increasingly important recreation draw. With a new city park planned west of Riverfront Towers, that demand should only increase.</p><div id="module-position-Q1CKJA1aqPM" class="story-asset oembed-asset"><div class="story-oembed-wrap "><div         class="js-oembed story-oembed story-oembed-detroit-free-press story-oembed-type-link"        data-oembed-type="link"        data-oembed-provider="detroit-free-press"><style>    .oembed-asset-link { border-bottom: 1px solid #e1e1e1; line-height: 120%; }</style><div class="oembed-asset oembed-asset-link oembed-asset-detroit-free-press oembed-simple-link-container"><a href="https://www.freep.com/story/money/business/john-gallagher/2018/02/08/detroit-future-west-riverfront/314678002/" data-track-label="newrecircinline" class="oembed-simple-link oembed-link-anchor"><p class="oembed-link-desc">Designers unveil ideas for west riverfront park</p><div style="clear: both;"></div></a></div></div></div></div><p class="p-text">Nuno, public affairs manager for DTE, said lack of accessibility to the river is a problem, but correcting it will help expand connections for an even greater area, including the DTE-funded Beacon Park, which opened last year. </p><p class="p-text">“It's important that we are able to connect our employees to (the Riverfront and other areas). These are some of the great assets that the city has to offer," Nuno said, noting that such connections can help with recruiting for DTE, too.</p><p class="p-text">The study, put together by the Skidmore, Owings and Merrill urban planning firm, includes numerous near-term infrastructure possibilities beyond demolition of the Joe, including the removal of the Cobo parking helix and Exit 1A from the Lodge.</p><p class="p-text">The project could add a Third Avenue pedestrian bridge for access to the riverfront and an expansion of Second Avenue toward the river, although there are several options for how these would be completed. Some longer-term changes are envisioned farther away, including demolition of the Sixth Street Bridge to Riverfront Towers and of the Joe Louis Parking Garage on West Jefferson.</p><div id="module-position-Q1CKJA1Eyeg" class="story-asset image-asset"><aside itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" class="wide single-photo"><img itemprop="url" src="https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/4b4285c1d64d50de5d52d9f73b4d2a8eb66f33a6/c=326-0-5433-3840&amp;r=x408&amp;c=540x405/local/-/media/2018/03/12/DetroitFreeP/DetroitFreePress/636564744539976684-joe-louis-riverfront.jpg" alt="A 2012 photo shows an aerial view of Joe Louis Arena" width="540" height="405" data-mycapture-src="https://www.gannett-cdn.com/media/2018/03/12/DetroitFreeP/DetroitFreePress/636564744539976684-joe-louis-riverfront.jpg"                    data-mycapture-sm-src="https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/4044b1e7645488621f157de466cf1a990870caa2/r=500x333/local/-/media/2018/03/12/DetroitFreeP/DetroitFreePress/636564744539976684-joe-louis-riverfront.jpg" /><meta itemprop="name" content="joe-louis-riverfront.jpg" /><meta itemprop="height" content="405" /><meta itemprop="width" content="540" /><span class="mycapture-btn-wrap"><span class="mycapture-non-priority-horizontal-image mycapture-btn-with-text js-mycapture-btn js-mycapture-photo-asset">Buy Photo</span></span><p>A 2012 photo shows an aerial view of Joe Louis Arena on the Detroit riverfront. It notes that Riverfront Towers management has agreed to allow continuation of the riverwalk through its property, a major extension of riverfront development.</p><p class="p-text">The study offers numerous infrastructure options and potential costs if implemented, ranging from $8 million to almost $100 million, with the likely range being around $33 million. Funding sources have not been identified, but it would likely come from both public and private sources.</p><p class="p-text">“These changes are really about providing a front door to the Joe Louis Arena (site)," said Sarah Pavelko, senior real estate manager at the Detroit Economic Growth Corp. Pavelko participated in the study process on behalf of the Downtown Development Authority, which had been asked to facilitate the study for the city.</p><div class="partner-outstream"></div><p class="p-text">Pavelko said the city wanted “to show how these improvements could open up that development potential for that site. ... The planning study has really allowed all entities involved to see the full potential in that immediate area."</p><p class="p-text">She noted that it's in the interest of both the city and the developers to "unlock that potential on the west riverfront."</p><p class="p-text">Tina Bassett, a spokeswoman for Wayne County Community College District, said the changes could help the college, which has its Downtown Campus on Fort Street, become more integrated with the surrounding area. The college recently broke ground on a $25 million City Center building at Fort and Fifth, which will provide programs ranging from biotechnology to sports medicine, as well as a new home for the college's basketball team.</p><p class="p-text"><span class="exclude-from-newsgate"><strong>Read more:</strong></span></p><p class="p-text"><span class="exclude-from-newsgate"><a href="/story/money/2018/02/26/ex-creditor-sues-city-detroit-sued-more-time-replace-joe-louis-arena-plan/373991002/">Joe Louis Arena developer sues for delay, may drop plans for first-class hotel</a></span></p><p class="p-text"><span class="exclude-from-newsgate"><a href="/story/news/local/michigan/detroit/2017/12/26/detroit-people-mover-joe-louis-arena-qline/971089001/">Without the Joe, what is Detroit People Mover's future?</a></span></p><p class="p-text">Bassett said the road network and lack of access to the river near the Joe makes everything "very cut off" and "very choppy." </p><p class="p-text">Better access would mean good things, she said.</p><p class="p-text">"More public access would be appreciated by everyone," Bassett said. "We have such a spectacular river, and we've never taken advantage of it to the way it should be taken advantage of or could be taken advantage of."</p><p class="p-text p-text-last" id="article-body-p-last"><em>Contact Eric D.
