alt=""></td><td align="left" bgcolor="#ffffcc" width="100%" valign="top" class="content"><br><h2>Intensive farming v organic farming</h2><p><strong>Intensive farming</strong></p><p>Intensive farming is concerned above all with <strong>productivity</strong> and uses a high level of <strong>inputs</strong> to achieve it.  The inputs are usually in the form of chemicals - fertilisers, pesticides and growth-regulators - and energy in the form of high levels of mechanisation.  Intensive farming does provide large quantities of relatively cheap food - but often at a cost to the environment or animal welfare.  The tables summarise some of the <em>pros</em> and <em>cons</em> of intensive farming.</p><p><strong>Intensive livestock farming:</strong></p><table cellspacing="0" cellpadding="3" border="1"><thead><tr><td><strong>pro</strong></td><td><strong>con</strong></td></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>large numbers of animals kept in 'ideal' conditions for growth</td><td>animals suffer from overcrowding and stress; may also lead to pollution from animal excreta</td></tr><tr><td>growth-promoting chemicals (hormones) boost meat production</td><td>hormones may enter environment and food chains with unpredictable consequences </td></tr><tr><td>high-protein feeds boost meat production</td><td>may trigger disease in animals.  In some cases disease can pass via meat into humans. </td></tr><tr><td>frequent use of drugs (eg antibiotics) keeps animals healthy</td><td>overuse of drugs helps develop antibiotic-resistant strains of disease</td></tr><tr><td>transport of live animals to slaughter means meat is fresh</td><td>practice is stressful to animals and probably cruel</td></tr></tbody></table><p><strong>Intensive arable farming / <a onmouseover="swapJBox(event, 1,'horticulture');" onmouseout="swapJBox(event, 0,'horticulture');" onclick="return badbrowser;" href="#horticulture" class="glossarylink"><strong class="term">horticulture</strong></a>:</strong></p><table cellspacing="0" cellpadding="3" border="1"><thead><tr><td><strong>pro</strong></td><td><strong>con</strong></td></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>chemical fertilisers boost productivity</td><td>fertiliser run-off damages ecosystems </td></tr><tr><td>chemical pesticides boost productivity</td><td>pesticides damage wildlife by getting into food chain or depriving <a onmouseover="swapJBox(event, 1,'predator');" onmouseout="swapJBox(event, 0,'predator');" onclick="return badbrowser;" href="#predator" class="glossarylink"><strong class="term">predator</strong></a> species of food</td></tr><tr><td>machinery makes farming more efficient and boosts yields</td><td>mechanisation increases farmers' energy needs</td></tr><tr><td><a onmouseover="swapJBox(event, 1,'monocultures');" onmouseout="swapJBox(event, 0,'monocultures');" onclick="return badbrowser;" href="#monocultures" class="glossarylink"><strong class="term">monocultures</strong></a> boost crop production</td><td>soil structure and nutrition levels may deteriorate</td></tr><tr><td>genetically modified (GM) crops may be hardier, reducing the need for pesticides and boosting productivity</td><td>genes may cross to other species with unpredictable consequences</td></tr></tbody></table><p><br></p><p><strong>Organic farming</strong></p><p>Organic farming - also sometimes called <strong>eco-agriculture</strong> - is a response to concerns many people have about the impact of modern farming methods on both <strong>food quality</strong> and the <strong>environment</strong>.</p><table border="0"><tr><td><img src="/web/20070503035007im_/http://www.bbc.co.uk/schools/gcsebitesize/img/biorganicveg.jpg" width="234" height="320" border="0" alt="image: market stall with organic veg"></td></tr><tr><td class="caption"></td></tr></table><p>Organic farmers:</p><ul><li>Avoid using man-made or chemical fertilisers.
