in tests on odour sensitivity</SMALL><BR></DIV>	</DIV><!-- /NOLImage -->	<DIV CLASS="bodytext">Women's greater ability to detect odours and aromas could be linked to the oestrogen hormone, according to research.<P>A US study showed women of reproductive age are far better at identifying  odours than men after repeated exposure to the source of the smell.<P>Researchers found female subjects whose odour sensitivity was tested many times, were able to detect the cherry-almond smell of benzaldehyde and a few other odours at progressively much lower concentrations.<P>Male subjects taking part in similar tests never improved their ability to identify odours with experience.<P></FONT><TABLE BGCOLOR="#FFFFCC" CLASS="floatingtable" HSPACE="2" VSPACE="2" CELLSPACING="0" WIDTH="150" BORDER="0" BGCOLOR="CCCC99"><DIV CLASS="boxhead">		Professor Tim Jacobs, Cardiff University			</DIV></TD></TR></TABLE><FONT FACE="sans-serif" SIZE="2">Researchers suggest the study results raise the possibility that female sex hormones have a role in enabling exposure-induced increases in odour sensitivity.<P>Experts say many studies show women out perform men in olfactory (sense of smell) sensitivity.<P>Tim Jacobs, Professor of Physiology at Cardiff University, said: "Some studies have shown that during ovulation, there's a surge of oestrogen which increases sensitivity.<P>"The structure of the nose is the same in women as men.<P>"They don't have any more receptors in the nose.<P>"Studies have also shown smells activate a greater region in the brain in women than men."<P>Psychologist Neil Martin says the study findings are interesting.<P><B>Female 'superiority'</B><P>He said: "There is a wealth of scientific data showing women's superiority at identifying and detecting odours at even very small concentrations.<P>"A recent study showed that although sex differences in olfactory superiority are not found in middle age and beyond (45-87 years), personality and semantic memory were found to be better predictors of odour identification, even when sex, age and education were taken into account."  <P>The tests were originally carried out on six subjects.<P>The same six subjects took part in a second test alongside six new subjects.<P>They used benzaldehyde again and a second substance, isoamyl acetate, which smells like bananas.<P>Sensitivity to benzaldehyde increased substantially for both the experienced women and the new "naive" group, but not for men.<P>However this was not repeated with isoamyl acetate.<P>Scientists at Monell Chemical Senses Center concluded the effect of repeated testing of odours was replicable but odour specific.<P>The tests were then carried out in young girls and post menopausal women to assess whether hormones played a part.<P>An equal number of age-matched boys and men were also tested.<P>Among the younger subjects, sensitivity to benzaldehyde did not change with time for either boys or girls.<P>Similarly, there were no significant gender
