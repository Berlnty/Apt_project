In addition, people have  developed cultural patterns and technologies that help them adjust to extremes of  temperature and humidity.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">In  very cold climates, there is a constant danger of developing 	<font color="#333333"><b>hypothermia</b></font> <a href="sounds/hypothermia.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>, which is a life threatening drop in   core body  temperature to subnormal levels.&nbsp; The normal   temperature for humans is about 98.6&deg; F. (37.0&deg; C.).<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; However, individual differences in </span> <a href="glossary.htm#metabolism">metabolism</a> <a href="sounds/metabolism.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a><span lang="en-us">, 	hormone levels, physical activity, and even the time of day can cause it to be as much as 1</span>&deg; 	F. (<span lang="en-us">.6</span>&deg; C.)<span lang="en-us"> higher or lower in 	healthy individuals.&nbsp; It is also normal for core body temperature to be&nbsp; 	lower in elderly people.</span>&nbsp; Hypothermia begins to occur when the   core body temperature drops to 94&deg; F. (34.4&deg;   C.).&nbsp; Below 85&deg; F.   (29.4&deg;C.), the  body cools  more rapidly because its natural temperature regulating system   (in the <a href="glossary.htm#hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>   <a href="sounds/hypothalamus.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>)   usually fails.&nbsp; The now rapid decline in core body   temperature is likely to result in death.&nbsp; However, there have been rare   cases in which people have been revived after their temperatures had dropped   to 57-60&deg; F. (13.9-15.6&deg;   C.).<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; This happened in 1999 to a Swedish woman who was 	trapped under an ice sheet in freezing water for 80 minutes.&nbsp; She was 	found unconscious, not breathing, and her heart had stopped beating, yet she 	was eventually revived despite the fact that her temperature had dropped to 	56.7</span>�<span lang="en-us"> F. (13.7</span>�<span lang="en-us"> C.).</span></font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">In   extremely hot climates or as a result of uncontrollable   infections, core body temperatures can  rise to equally fatal levels.&nbsp; This is <font color="#333333"><b>hyperthermia</b></font> <a href="sounds/hyperthermia.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>.&nbsp;   Life threatening hyperthermia typically starts in   humans when their temperatures rise to 105-107&deg; F. (40.6-41.7&deg;   C.).&nbsp; Only a few days at this extraordinarily   high temperature level is likely to result in the deterioration of internal   organs and death.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">Body size and  shape are significant factors in how efficiently an individual responds physiologically to  cold and hot climates.&nbsp; Two 19th century naturalists, Carl Bergmann and Joel Allen,  formulated rules concerning these factors.</font></p>  <p align="center"><font face="Times New Roman" color="#008080" size="1"><br>  </font><b><font face="Arial" color="#808080" size="5">Bergmann's  Rule</font></b></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">In 1847,<span lang="en-us"> the German 	biologist</span> <span lang="en-us"><font color="#333333"><b>C</b></font></span><font color="#333333"><b>arl Bergmann</b></font> <a href="sounds/Bergmann.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>  observed that within  the same species of warm-blooded animals, populations having less massive individuals are  more often found in warm climates near the equator, while those with greater   bulk, or mass,  are found further from the equator in colder regions.&nbsp; This is due to the fact that  big animals generally have larger body masses which result in more heat being produced.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;	</span>The greater amount of heat results from there being more cells.&nbsp; A normal  byproduct of metabolism  in cells is heat production.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;	</span>Subsequently, the more cells an animal has, the more internal heat it will  produce.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">In addition,  larger animals usually have a smaller surface area relative to their body mass and,  therefore, are comparatively inefficient at radiating their body heat off into the  surrounding environment.&nbsp; The relationship between 	surface area and volume of objects was described in the 1630's by Galileo.&nbsp; 	It can be demonstrated 	with the cube shaped boxes 	shown below.&nbsp; Note  that the volume increases twice as fast as the surface area.&nbsp; 	<span lang="en-us">This is the reason that </span>relatively less surface  area results in relatively less heat being lost from 	animals.</font></p> </table>  </div>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">We lose heat  to the surrounding environment in several ways, as shown <span lang="en-us">in 	the illustration above on the right</span>. However, simple	<font color="#333333"><b>radiation</b></font>  is the process that is responsible for most of the loss, except in hot dry climates where  <font color="#333333"><b>evaporative cooling</b></font>, or sweating, can be more significant.</font></p>  <p align="center"><strong><em>  <font color="#408080" size="2" face="Times New Roman"><br>  </font></em></strong><b><font face="Arial" color="#808080" size="5">Cold  Climate Responses</font></b></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">Many people  living in freezing climates drink alcohol to warm themselves.&nbsp; This increases blood  flow to the body extremities, thereby providing a feeling of warmth.&nbsp; However, it  results only in a temporary warming and can speed up the loss of heat from the vital  internal organs, resulting in more rapid death from hypothermia.&nbsp;  A much more  effective cultural response to extremely cold temperatures is the use of insulating  clothing, houses, and fires.&nbsp; People all over the world also adapt by limiting  outdoor activities to warmer times of the day.&nbsp; In some societies, sleeping in 	<span lang="en-us">family </span>groups<span lang="en-us"> with bodies 	pushed up against each other</span>  is also <span lang="en-us">done</span> in order to minimize heat loss<span lang="en-us"> 	during the cold months of the year</span>.</font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">When  the environment is very cold, life can depend on the ability of our bodies to  reduce heat loss and to increase internal heat production.&nbsp; As Bergmann  and Allen observed, the human physiological response to cold commonly includes the  evolution of more massive, compact bodies with relatively less surface area.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; 	However, short term acclimatization to the cold also occurs.&nbsp; A normal 	initial physiological response is the narrowing of blood vessels near skin 	surface (</span><b>vasoconstriction</b><span lang="en-us">).&nbsp; This 	preserves core body heat by reducing peripheral blood flow.&nbsp; As a 	consequence, the skin cools and less heat is lost from the body by 	radiation.&nbsp; However, if the environmental temperature is below the 	freezing point, prolonged vasoconstriction can result in dangerous </span> <a href="glossary.htm#frostbite">frostbite</a><span lang="en-us">.&nbsp; 	As a consequence, the body's internal temperature regulating mechanism 	responds by dilating the peripheral blood vessels (<b>vasodilation</b>), 	thereby increasing the flow of warm blood near the skin surface.&nbsp; The 	body normally alternates back and forth between vasoconstriction and 	vasodilation to compensate for the risks of both conditions.&nbsp; This 	cycling is known as the <i>Lewis hunting phenomenon</i>).&nbsp; </span>Shivering  can also <span lang="en-us">cause a </span>short-term warming 	<span lang="en-us">effect</span>. The increased muscle activity in  shivering <span lang="en-us">results</span> in some heat production.&nbsp; </font></p>	<p><font size="3" face="Arial">There are three additional important types of  biological responses to cold conditions found among<span lang="en-us"> some</span> human<span lang="en-us"> 	population</span>s around the world:</font></p>  <div align="center"><center>    <table border="0" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111" cellpadding="2" cellspacing="0">    <tr>      <th align="left" valign="top"><span style="font-weight: 400">		<font size="3" face="Arial">1.&nbsp;&nbsp;</font></span></th>      <td><font size="3" face="Arial">increased <a href="glossary.htm#basal_metabolic_rate">basal metabolic rate</a></font></td>    </tr>    <tr>      <th align="left" valign="top"><span style="font-weight: 400">		<font size="3" face="Arial">2.&nbsp;&nbsp;</font></span></th>      <td><font size="3" face="Arial">fat insulation of vital organs</font></td>    </tr>    <tr>      <th align="left" valign="top"><span style="font-weight: 400">		<font size="3" face="Arial">3.&nbsp;&nbsp;</font></span></th>      <td><font size="3" face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">long term </span>change in blood flow patterns</font></td>    </tr>  </table>  </center></div>  <p></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">People living in harsh subarctic regions, such as the Inuit <a href="sounds/Inuit.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a>  	(Eskimo)<span lang="en-us"> of the far northern regions of the western 	hemisphere</span> and the Indians of Tierra del Fuego<span lang="en-us"> at 	the southern end</span>,  traditionally consumed large quantities of high calorie fatty foods.&nbsp; This  significantly increases  the basal metabolic rate, which, in turn, results in the production of extra body heat.&nbsp;  These peoples also wore heavy clothing, often slept in a huddle with their  bodies next to each other, and remained active when outdoors.</font></p>  <div align="center">    <center>    <table border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111" id="AutoNumber1">      <tr>        <td align="center" rowspan="2">        <p align="center"><font size="5" face="Times New Roman"><strong>  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/map_of_Inuit_and_Tierra_del_Fuego.gif" alt="map of the Americas showing the Inuit territory at the far north of North America  and Tierra del Fuego at the far south of South America" align="left" hspace="0" width="131" height="175"></strong></font></td>        <td align="center" width="30" rowspan="2">&nbsp;</td>        <td align="center">      <img border="0" src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/Inuit_man.jpg" alt="photo of an Inuit man in the winter--he is wearing a traditional parka; his eye brows and mustache are caked with ice" width="127" height="129"></td>      </tr>      <tr>        <td align="center"><font face="Arial"><small>Inuit man</small></font></td>      </tr>    </table>    </center>  </div>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">The Ju/'hoansi <a href="sounds/Ju_hoansi.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a> of Southwestern Africa and the Aborigines  <a href="sounds/Aborigines.mp3">  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" width="20" height="19"></a> of Australia usually respond physiologically to the  cold in a different way.&nbsp; Thick fat insulation develops around the vital organs of  the chest and abdomen. &nbsp; In addition, their skin cools due to vasoconstriction  at night.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;	</span>As a result, heat loss is reduced  and the core body temperature remains at normal levels.&nbsp; However, the  skin feels very cold<span lang="en-us"> throughout the night</span>.</font></p>  <p align="center"><font size="3" face="Times New Roman"><strong>  <img src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/map_of_Ju_hoansi_and_Abos.gif" alt="map of Africa, South Asia, and Australia showing the !Kung and Australian Aborigine Territories--the !Kung are in Southwest Africa, while the Aboringines once occuppied all of Australia" width="255" height="144"></strong></font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">This response  would not be adaptive if the  Ju/'hoansi<span lang="en-us"> </span>and the Aborigines lived in consistently freezing  environments because the concentration of body heat in their torsos would allow the loss  of fingers, toes, and other appendages from frostbite.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; 	The loss of fingers in particular would make it difficult for these hunters 	and gatherers to acquire food.</span>&nbsp;  Their physiological adaptation is to environments that rarely stay below freezing long and  that do not have abundant high calorie fatty foods.</font></p>  <p align="center"><font face="Times New Roman" color="#008080" size="1"><br>  </font><b><font face="Arial" color="#808080" size="5">Hot  Climate Responses</font></b></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">Adapting to  hot environments is as complex as adapting to cold ones.&nbsp; However, cold adaptation is  usually more difficult physiologically for humans since we are not 	<span lang="en-us">sub</span>arctic animals by  nature.&nbsp; We do not grow dense fur coats nor do we usually have thick layers of fat  insulation like polar bears.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; Despite this reality, 	more people die from heat than cold in the United States every year.&nbsp; 	Those who succumb are usually babies left in locked cars on hot days and the 	elderly poor who cannot afford air conditioning.</span></font></p>  <p><font size="3" face="Arial">The effect of  heat on our bodies varies with the relative humidity of the air.&nbsp; High temperatures  with high humidity make it harder to lose excess body heat.&nbsp; This is due to the fact  that when the moisture content of air goes up, it becomes increasingly more difficult for  sweat to evaporate.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; </span>The sweat stays on our skin and we feel clammy.&nbsp; As a  result, we do not get the cooling effect of rapid evaporation.</font></p>  <div align="center">	<table id="table3" border="1" cellpadding="8" cellspacing="1">		<tr>			<td><font face="Arial" size="2">			<img border="0" src="/web/20130106211840im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/adapt/images/hand_icon.gif" width="21" height="20" align="absbottom">&nbsp;			<u>			<font color="#333333"><b>E</b></font></u></font><span lang="en-us"><font face="Arial" size="2"><font color="#333333"><b><u>vaporative 			cooling experiment</u>.</b></font>&nbsp;
