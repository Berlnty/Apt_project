the evening (when everyone gets home from work), before declining at night. </p><p id="sJhFzj">This curve happens to be from New England, in the fall. The curve looks somewhat different in different regions and during different seasons — there are different-size peaks and ramps — but it usually falls within a fairly narrow and predictable range. </p><p id="XRzxeg">Until solar PV comes along!</p><p id="RJZvYT">It may seem counterintuitive, but the people who operate the power grid don’t really think of solar power as conventional supply. They can’t control or “dispatch” it on their own schedule. It comes and goes with the sun; they must accommodate it. So from the grid operator’s point of view, more solar (or wind) power looks like a reduction in demand for their dispatchable power. </p><p id="JrmDjW">Total load minus renewable energy is known as “net load.” That’s the target utilities have to hit with their dispatchable resources.</p><p id="hu3wFE">As more and more solar PV is integrated into the grid, it starts dramatically suppressing net load during midday, when the sun is out. The net load curve sags in the middle of the day (like a belly) and then swoops back up when the sun goes down (like a neck). get solved, things could get ugly. In the near future, utilities could regularly be forced to ramp up their dispatchable plants for a morning peak, then scale back or shut down almost all of those plants while the sun is out, and then bring them all back online (quickly) when the sun goes down.</p><p id="sBs7S3">All that ramping and stop-starting is expensive and unfamiliar to the operators of many fossil fuel power plants. In part thanks to early warnings from energy modelers, utilities and grid operators are beginning to awaken to the issue and take steps to address it.</p><p id="Js40yy">To mark the duck curve’s 10th birthday, I called Paul Denholm, the researcher who led that original group at NREL. He’s been at the lab since 2004, studying electricity systems and solar integration, and has a great deal to say about how best to handle the duck — some of it counterintuitive. (For instance, he’s okay wasting a little solar power here and there.)</p><p id="euVsWz">The conversation, which I’ve edited for length and clarity, gets pretty energy-nerdy. But if you like puzzles, you’ll like this one.</p><h3 id="NEBqHm">The history of the duck curve</h3><h4 id="U3LD5q">David Roberts</h4><p id="UiFxFy">Tell me a little about the history of the duck curve.</p><h4 id="Xm8XSs">Paul Denholm</h4><p id="08Fx4T">One of the first things I did when I joined [NREL] was working with <a href="https://www.nrel.gov/research/robert-margolis.html">Robert Margolis</a> to model how consumers might adopt PV. When we put in the [price] projections we were getting from the [NREL] solar program, it was kind of like, wow, this actually might happen! California might actually start deploying significant amounts of solar in the 2010, ’15, ’20 time frame.</p><p id="Og8LRU">Simultaneous to that, the wind program was supporting some of the first-ever wind integration studies. And the solar program thought, hey, we need to do that too. If this is actually going to happen, we need to start studying solar on the grid as well.</p><p id="iOjFww">So the next major project I worked on, we started simulating solar on the grid, performing some of the first solar integration studies. We started modeling what happens if California gets to 10, 15 percent solar. more flexible</h3><h4 id="V7MGJL">David Roberts</h4><p id="ssRmcO">When you look ahead 10 years to a really severe duck curve, what do you see as the most effective solutions?</p><h4 id="WgE3hW">Paul Denholm</h4><p id="G6fLIg">A lot of people in the industry use this concept of a “flexibility supply curve,” which is you deploy the most cost-effective flexibility option first. </p><p id="yhL94k">Thinking back to the early part of the 2000s, when wind people started worrying about this, storage was nowhere near cost-effective. There was a lot of strong disagreement between the storage people and the wind people, with wind really resisting storage, I think legitimately. They said, wait a second, there are way more cost-effective measures to deal with wind curtailment and integration challenges.</p><p id="vCsrqg">So for the first eight years of my time here, I was focused on more cost-effective measures. A lot of them are pretty boring and don’t get a lot of attention. “Learn how to ramp your coal and gas plants better” — that’s a real thing, it’s really important, but it’s not sexy. </p><h4 id="cofz5n">David Roberts</h4><p id="dF0t69">You mean ramp them better technically or economically speaking?</p><h4 id="rQfsCW">Paul Denholm</h4><p id="KnCxgm">Both. You’ve got coal or gas plants that were designed as baseload, or intermediate load with a little bit of load-following capability, and the operators just weren’t used to moving these plants over a large range. </p><p id="XiaWZk">They technically could. You look at the spec sheets, what the manufacturers say they can do, and they say the plants can comfortably ramp over 50 percent of their range. The higher priority is maximizing the inherent flexibility of the fossil thermal fleet.</p><h3 id="kPhnHA">Covering a wider geographic area with renewables and energy markets</h3><h4 id="7jMwAv">David Roberts</h4><p id="gcnKc2">Faster ramping can’t keep up forever, though, right? </p><h4 id="fto0Ev">Paul Denholm</h4><p id="8HxN60">That’s right. It’s not any single solution; it really is a suite of options. </p><p id="zT1X5b">One thing we talk about is spatial diversity [i.e., spreading renewables out over a larger geographical territory] and the role of larger footprints [i.e., larger connected energy markets] — things like the <a href="http://www.caiso.com/informed/Pages/StakeholderProcesses/ConsolidatedEnergyImbalanceMarketInitiatives.aspx">energy imbalance market</a> (EIM), where California can sell surplus energy to its neighbors. </p><p id="gIwS0m">If there’s a way you can share the sun with places that don’t have as much sun, that helps everybody out. A mine-mouth coal plant, some of those plants used to be able to generate for $5/MWh and they’d still have to ramp because there wasn’t enough demand. Instead of having a 95 percent capacity factor, maybe they’d run with 80 or 85. Nobody was screaming about that, even though we were “curtailing” the capacity of a coal plant. </p><h4 id="fENABr">David Roberts</h4><p id="9jfEuS">When you curtail the coal plant, though, you still have the fuel. You’re not wasting it.</p><h4 id="cAtXU2">Paul Denholm</h4><p id="dEmmdK">But what fraction of the LCOE [levelized cost of energy] of a mine-mouth coal plant is the fuel? It’s a small fraction. The cost of the plant is the major component of the LCOE, so when I curtail generation from that coal plant, I’m losing the opportunity to recover the cost of investing in that really expensive asset. </p><p id="JtdV1L">That’s one of the reasons I say don’t be afraid of curtailment. It can be part of the economics of the power system. It’s when you start curtailing 30 or 40 or 50 percent, that’s a problem. But these small amounts of curtailment here and there, I’m not too worried. </p><p id="i8f5Pw">They are indicators that we need to do something about it, it’s gonna get worse, so let’s be intelligent, let’s get the economists to design appropriate markets and rate structures, let’s get the engineers to come up with solutions, let’s let industry come up with interesting and new ways to use electricity. </p><h3 id="X0aUZb">The holy grail: affordable energy storage</h3><h4 id="pkiC58">David Roberts</h4><p id="FAly6C">It is frequently argued that a system based on wind and solar will need an enormous amount of storage — not just hourly, but daily or even seasonal storage — and that batteries aren’t up to the task. So we’ll either have to limit the scale of renewables or find some other cheap, large-scale, long-term storage. What’s your take? </p><h4 id="iJtCC3">Paul Denholm</h4><p id="wsS13o">We spend a huge amount of time talking about this topic here around the lunch table — a lot of calories are spent on it. So I’ll tell you what I’d say is the informal general consensus about ultra-high-penetration renewables scenarios.</p><p id="Jia2Rn">The consensus is emerging that we can probably do 80 percent [renewables] with some combination of spatial diversity and short-duration storage. </p><p id="4p4TWV">We can deal with diurnal shifts with short-duration storage, and not too much of it. When we did our <a href="https://www.nrel.gov/analysis/re-futures.html">Renewable Electricity Future</a> study back in 2012, we got up to 80 percent renewables with only about 100 GW of additional storage.
