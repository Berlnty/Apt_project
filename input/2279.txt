<b>human</b> (also called a <b>human being</b> or <b>person</b>) is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens</a></i>, which means 'wise man' in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Linnaeus" title="Carolus Linnaeus">Carolus Linnaeus</a> put humans in the <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammalian</a> <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> of <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Humans are a type of <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">hominoid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">chimpanzees</a> are their closest living relatives.</p><p>Humans are mammals. They are also social animals. They usually live in groups. They help and protect each other. They care for their <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">children</a>. Humans are <a href="/wiki/Bipedal" class="mw-redirect" title="Bipedal">bipedal</a>, which means they walk on two <a href="/wiki/Legs" class="mw-redirect" title="Legs">legs</a>. Humans have a very complex <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>, which is much larger than that of the other living apes. They use <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, make <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">ideas</a>, and feel <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotions</a>. This brain, and the fact that <a href="/wiki/Arm" title="Arm">arms</a> are not needed for <a href="/wiki/Walking" class="mw-redirect" title="Walking">walking</a>, lets humans use <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a>. Humans use tools far more than any other species.</p><p>There are humans living on every <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As of 2017, there were over 7.3 <a href="/wiki/Billion" title="Billion">billion</a> people living on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p></p><div id="toc" class="toc"><div class="toctitle" lang="en" Their life depends less on <a href="/wiki/Instinct" title="Instinct">instinct</a> than other animals, and more on <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a>. Humans are also born with their <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brains</a> not so well developed as those of other <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. This makes for an unusually long <a href="/wiki/Childhood" title="Childhood">childhood</a>, and this which makes <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> life important. There is a good reason for this. If their brains were better developed they would be larger, and this would make <a href="/wiki/Birth" title="Birth">birth</a> more difficult. In birth, the baby's head has to get through the 'birth canal', a passageway through the mother's <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvis</a>.</p><p>Many animals use <a href="/wiki/Sign" title="Sign">signs</a> and sounds to communicate with each other. But humans have a complex system called <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>. It allows them to express ideas by using words. Humans are capable of making <a href="/wiki/Abstraction" title="Abstraction">abstract ideas</a> and communicating them to others. Human language can express things that are not present, or talk about events that are not happening at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The things might be elsewhere, and the events may also have occurred at another place or time.<sup id="cite_ref-Revolution_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Revolution-7">[7]</a></sup> No known animals have a system of <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a> that is as elaborate as human language. By using words to communicate with each other, humans make <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/complex" class="extiw" title="wikt:complex">complex</a> <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">communities</a> with <a href="/wiki/Laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws">laws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tradition" class="mw-redirect" title="Tradition">traditions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">customs</a>. Humans like to understand the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a> around them. They try to explain things through <a href="/wiki/Myth" class="mw-redirect" title="Myth">myth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>. Wanting to understand things has helped many humans make important discoveries.</p><p>Humans are the only species living today known to build <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fires</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cook</a> their <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> and wear <a href="/wiki/Clothes" class="mw-redirect" title="Clothes">clothes</a>. Humans use more <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> than any other animal on Earth ever has. Humans like things that are <a href="/wiki/Beauty" title="Beauty">beautiful</a> and like to make <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>. Humans use <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teaching" class="mw-redirect" title="Teaching">teaching</a> to pass on <a href="/wiki/Skills" class="mw-redirect" title="Skills">skills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">ideas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">customs</a> to the next <a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">generations</a>.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Origins">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Origins">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_evolution" title="Human evolution">Human evolution</a></div><p>Humans are part of the animal kingdom. They are <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, which means that they give birth to their young ones, rather than laying eggs like <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, and females feed their babies with breast milk. Humans belong to the order of <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>. Apes like <a href="/wiki/Gorilla" title="Gorilla">gorillas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orangutan" title="Orangutan">orangutans</a> are also primates. The closest living relatives of humans are the two <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">chimpanzee</a> species: the <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">common chimpanzee</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bonobo" title="Bonobo">bonobo</a>. Scientists have examined the <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genes</a> of humans and chimpanzees, and compared their <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>. The studies showed that 95% to 99% of the DNA of humans and chimpanzees is the same.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Biologists explain the similarity between humans and other <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">hominoids</a> by their descent from a common ancestor. In 2001, a <a href="/wiki/Hominid" title="Hominid">hominid</a> skull was discovered in <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>. The skull is about 7 million years old, and has been classified as <i><a href="/wiki/Sahelanthropus_tchadensis" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahelanthropus tchadensis">Sahelanthropus tchadensis</a></i>. This skull may show that the date at which humans started to <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolve</a> (develop differently) from other primates is 2 million years earlier than scientists had previously thought.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Humans are part of a <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" class="mw-redirect" title="Subfamily">subfamily</a> called the Homininae (or <a href="/wiki/Hominin" title="Hominin">hominins</a>), inside the <a href="/wiki/Hominid" title="Hominid">hominids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Great_ape" class="mw-redirect" title="Great ape">great apes</a>.</p><p>Long ago, there used to be other types of hominins on Earth. They were like modern humans, but not the same. <i>Homo sapiens</i> are the only type of hominins who are alive today.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The earliest known fossils of genus <i>Homo</i> have been called <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_habilis" title="Homo habilis">Homo habilis</a></i> (handy man). The first <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> of <i>Homo habilis</i> were found in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>. <i>Homo hablilis</i> is thought to have lived about 2.2 to 1.7 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Another human species thought to be an ancestor of the modern human is <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There are other extinct species of <i>Homo</i> known today. Many of them were likely our 'cousins', as they developed differently than our ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A theory called the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_pump_theory" title="Sahara pump theory">Sahara pump theory</a> has been used to tell how different species of plants and animals moved from Africa to the Middle East, and then elsewhere. Early humans may have moved from Africa to other parts of the world in the same way.</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Out_of_Africa">Out of Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Out of Africa">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Change section: Out of Africa">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Out_of_Africa" title="Out of Africa">Out of Africa</a></div><p>The first truly modern humans seem to have appeared between 300,000,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and 200,000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> These early humans later moved out from Africa. By about 90,000 years ago they had moved into <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. This was the area where <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthals</a>, <i>Homo neanderthalensis</i>, had been living for a long time (at least 350,000 years).</p><p>By about 42 to 44,000 years ago <i>Homo sapiens</i> had reached western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Britain">Britain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In Europe and western Asia, <i>Homo sapiens</i> replaced the neanderthals by about 35,000 years ago. The details of this event are not known.</p><p>At roughly the same time <i>Homo sapiens</i> arrived in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Their arrival in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> was much later, about 15,000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> All these earlier groups of modern man were <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a>.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civilization">Civilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Civilization">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Change section: Civilization">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_history" class="mw-redirect" title="World history">World history</a></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stonehenge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Stonehenge.jpg/250px-Stonehenge.jpg" width="250" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Stonehenge.jpg/375px-Stonehenge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Stonehenge.jpg/500px-Stonehenge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="709" data-file-height="472" /></a><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stonehenge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> was built around 4500-4000 years ago. This time was in the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period of the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a>.</div></div></div><p><a href="/wiki/Pre-history" title="Pre-history">Early human history</a> is commonly divided into three ages. The time periods are labeled with the material used for tools.</p><ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a></li><li>The <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a></li><li>The <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a></li></ul><p>The "Stone Age" is commonly subdivided into the <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleolithic">Paleolithic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> periods.</p><p>Up to about 10 thousand years ago most humans were <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a>. They did not live in one place, but <a href="/wiki/Nomads" class="mw-redirect" title="Nomads">moved around</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a> changed. The start of planting <a href="/wiki/Crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Crops">crops</a> for food, called <a href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">farming</a> made the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_revolution" title="Neolithic revolution">Neolithic revolution</a>. Some people chose to live in <a href="/wiki/Human_settlement" title="Human settlement">settlements</a>. This also led to the invention of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> tools and the training of <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>. About 6000 years ago the first proper civilizations began in places like <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>. The people formed governments and <a href="/wiki/Army" title="Army">armies</a> for protection. They competed for area to live and resources and sometimes they <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">fought with each other</a>. About 4000 years ago some states took over or <span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/conquered"><span style="color: #CC2200;" title="conquered (Page does not exist on Wiktionary)">conquered</span></a></span> other states and made <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empires</a>. Examples include ancient <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> Empire.</p><p>Some modern day religions also began at this time such as <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>. From the Middle Ages and beyond humanity saw an explosion of new technology and inventions. The printing press, the <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">car</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">train</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> are all examples of this kind of invention. As a result of the developments in technology, modern humans live in a world where everyone is connected, for example by <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a>. People now control and change the <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/environment" class="extiw" title="wikt:environment">environment</a> around them in many different ways.</p><h2><span id="Habitats.2C_settlements_and_population"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitats,_settlements_and_population">Habitats, settlements and population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Habitats, settlements and population">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Change section: Habitats, settlements and population">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hong_Kong_Skyline_Restitch_-_Dec_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Hong_Kong_Skyline_Restitch_-_Dec_2007.jpg/300px-Hong_Kong_Skyline_Restitch_-_Dec_2007.jpg" width="300" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Hong_Kong_Skyline_Restitch_-_Dec_2007.jpg/450px-Hong_Kong_Skyline_Restitch_-_Dec_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Hong_Kong_Skyline_Restitch_-_Dec_2007.jpg/600px-Hong_Kong_Skyline_Restitch_-_Dec_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4250" data-file-height="1844" /></a><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hong_Kong_Skyline_Restitch_-_Dec_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Humans can now change their environment to solve problems. The many tall buildings in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> are an example of people solving the problem of <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/overpopulation" class="extiw" title="wikt:overpopulation">too many people in one place</a>.</div></div></div><p>In early times, humans usually settled near to <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">natural</a> <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/resource" class="extiw" title="wikt:resource">resources</a>. In modern times if people need things they can <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> them from somewhere else. So basing a settlement close to resources is no longer as important as it once was. Since 1800, the number of humans, or <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>, has <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/increase" class="extiw" title="wikt:increase">increased</a> by six billion.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Most humans (61%) live in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. The rest live in <a href="/wiki/The_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="The Americas">the Americas</a> (14%), <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> (14%), <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> (11%), and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a> (0.5%).</p><p>Most people live in <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">towns</a> and <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a>. This number is expected to get higher. In 2005 the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> said that by the end of that year, over half the world would be living in cities. This is an important change in human settlement patterns: a century earlier in 1900 only 14% of people lived in cities, in 2000 47% of the world's population lived in cities. In developed countries, like the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, 80% of the population live in cities.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>Humans have a large effect on the world. Humans are at the top of the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> and are generally not eaten by any animals. Humans have been described as super <a href="/wiki/Predator" class="mw-redirect" title="Predator">predators</a> because of this.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Because of <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a> and other reasons humans are said to be a big cause of <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/global" class="extiw" title="wikt:global">global</a> <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a These models had body hair and facial hair removed and head hair trimmed</div></div></div><p>Human body types can be very different from each other. The average <a href="/wiki/Height" title="Height">height</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Adult" title="Adult">adult</a> human is between 5 and 6 1/2 <a href="/wiki/Foot" title="Foot">feet</a>. The average weight is between 76 and 83 <a href="/wiki/Kg" class="mw-redirect" title="Kg">kg</a> for <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">males</a> and 54–64&#160;kg for <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">females</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>Human <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a> grows on the underarms, the <a href="/wiki/Genitalia" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitalia">genitals</a>, legs, arms, and on the top of the <a href="/wiki/Head" title="Head">head</a> in adults of both genders. Hair will usually grow on the <a href="/wiki/Face" title="Face">face</a> of most adult males, and on the <a href="/wiki/Chest" title="Chest">chest</a> and back of many adult males. In human children of both genders, long hair grows only on the top of the head. Although it might look like humans have fewer hairs than most <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>, they actually do not. The average human has more hair <i>follicles</i>, where hair grows from, than most <a href="/wiki/Chimpanzee" title="Chimpanzee">chimpanzees</a> have.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Human hair can be <a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brown" title="Brown">brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> or <a href="/wiki/Blond" class="mw-redirect" title="Blond">blond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Modern humans can also have their hair in many different <a href="/wiki/Color" class="mw-redirect" title="Color">colors</a> by using <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dye</a>. When humans get older hair can turn <a href="/wiki/Grey" class="mw-redirect" title="Grey">grey</a> or even <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a>.</p><p>Human <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> colors vary greatly. They can be a very pale <a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a> all the way to dark <a href="/wiki/Brown" title="Brown">brown</a>. There is a reason why people in tropical areas have dark skins. The dark pigment (<a href="/wiki/Melanin" title="Melanin">melanin</a>) in the skin protects them against <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> rays in <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>. The damage caused by UV rays can and does cause <a href="/wiki/Skin_cancer" title="Skin cancer">skin cancer</a> in some people. Therefore, in more sunny areas, <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> favors darker skin color.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Sun tanning has nothing to do with this issue, because it is just a temporary process which is not inherited. In colder climates the advantage of light-colored skin is that it radiates less heat. Therefore, in less sunny areas, natural selection favors lighter skin color.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>Humans are not as strong as other <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a> of the same size. An average <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a> <a href="/wiki/Orangutan" title="Orangutan">orangutan</a> is at least three times as strong as an average human man.<sup id="cite_ref-Schwartz_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>The average human male needs 7 to 8 hours <a href="/wiki/Sleep" title="Sleep">sleep</a> a day. People who sleep less than this are generally not as <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">healthy</a>. A child needs more sleep, 9 to 10 hours on average.</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Life cycle">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Change section: Life cycle">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tubal_Pregnancy_with_embryo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Tubal_Pregnancy_with_embryo.jpg/125px-Tubal_Pregnancy_with_embryo.jpg" width="125" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Tubal_Pregnancy_with_embryo.jpg/188px-Tubal_Pregnancy_with_embryo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Tubal_Pregnancy_with_embryo.jpg/250px-Tubal_Pregnancy_with_embryo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1874" data-file-height="2000" /></a><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tubal_Pregnancy_with_embryo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A human <a href="/wiki/Foetus" class="mw-redirect" title="Foetus">foetus</a> at 7 weeks old</div></div></div><p>The human life cycle is quite like most other <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. The young grow inside the female <a href="/wiki/Mother" title="Mother">mother</a> for nine <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">months</a>. After this time the <a href="/wiki/Baby" title="Baby">baby</a> is pushed out of the woman's <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vagina</a>.</p><p>Unlike most other animals, human childbirth is somewhat dangerous. Because human brains are so large, babies' heads are wide. The mothers <a href="/wiki/Pelvis" title="Pelvis">pelvis</a> <a href="/wiki/Bones" class="mw-redirect" title="Bones">bones</a> are also not very wide because people walk on two <a href="/wiki/Legs" class="mw-redirect" title="Legs">legs</a>. This combination means that sometimes either mother or baby <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">die</a> in childbirth.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The number of mothers dying in childbirth is less in the 21st century. This is because of better <a href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">medication</a> and treatment. In many poor countries the number of mothers dying is higher. Sometimes it is up to 10 times as many as richer countries.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>The average human baby <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weighs</a> 3 – 4 <a href="/wiki/Kg" class="mw-redirect" title="Kg">kg</a> at birth and is 50–60 <a href="/wiki/Centimetre" title="Centimetre">cm</a> tall. This is often less in poorer countries.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Many babies in poor countries often die early because of this.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>Humans have four stages in their lives: <a href="/wiki/Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Children">childhood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teenager" title="Teenager">adolescence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adult" title="Adult">adulthood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_age" title="Old age">old age</a>.</p><p><a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">Life expectancy</a> is how long you are expected to live for. This depends on many things including where you live. The highest life expectancy is for people from <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a>, 89.52 years. The lowest is for people from <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a> where life expectancy is only 49.81 years.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychology_and_neurology">Psychology and neurology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Psychology and neurology">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Change section: Psychology and neurology">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">Psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Epithalamus.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Epithalamus.png/220px-Epithalamus.png" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Epithalamus.png/330px-Epithalamus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Epithalamus.png/440px-Epithalamus.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="443" /></a><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Epithalamus.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A drawing of part of a human brain</div></div></div><p>Psychology is the study of how the human <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a> works. The human <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> is the main controller of what a person does. Everything from <a href="/wiki/Movement" title="Movement">moving</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a> to <a href="/wiki/Thought" title="Thought">thinking</a> is done by the brain. The human <a href="/wiki/Neocortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Neocortex">neocortex</a> is huge compared with other <a href="/wiki/Mammals" class="mw-redirect" title="Mammals">mammals</a>, and gives us our thinking ability, and the ability to speak and understand <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>. <a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">Neurology</a> is the study of how the brain works, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> is the study of how and why people think and <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">feel</a>. Many aspects of life are also influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Hormone_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Hormone system">hormone system</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Growth" class="mw-disambig" title="Growth">growth</a> and sexual development. The hormonal system (especially the <a href="/wiki/Pituitary_gland" title="Pituitary gland">pituitary gland</a>) is partly controlled by the brain.</p><p>Human behaviour is hard to understand, so sometimes psychologists study <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> because they may be simpler and easier to understand. Psychology overlaps with many other sciences including <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Culture">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Change section: Culture">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=11" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Language">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Change section: Language">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">Language</a></div><p><a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">Language</a> at its most basic is talking, <a href="/wiki/Reading" title="Reading">reading</a> and <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a>. The study of language is called <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>. Humans have the most complicated languages on Earth. Although almost all animals communicate, human language is unique. Its use of <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, and its huge learnt <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">vocabulary</a> are its main features.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Revolution_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Revolution-7">[7]</a></sup> There are over 7,300 languages spoken around the world. The world's most spoken <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">first language</a> is <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a>, and the most spoken language is English.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> This includes speakers of English as a <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">second language</a>.</p><h3><span id="Art.2C_music.2C_and_literature"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Art,_music,_and_literature">Art, music, and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a></div></div></div><p><a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">Art</a> has existed almost as long as humans. Art represents how someone feels in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Photograph" class="mw-redirect" title="Photograph">photograph</a>.</p><p><a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">Music</a> has also been around for thousands of years. Literature is important as without it many of the things we use today, such as <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>, would not exist.</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Race_and_ethnicity">Race and ethnicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=13" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Race and ethnicity">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Change section: Race and ethnicity">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Race_(sociology)" title="Race (sociology)">Race (sociology)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnicity">ethnicity</a></div><p>Humans often categorize themselves by <a href="/wiki/Race_(sociology)" title="Race (sociology)">race</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnicity</a>. Human races are questionable as valid biological categories.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Human racial categories are based on both <a href="/wiki/Ancestor" title="Ancestor">ancestry</a> and visible traits such as <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> color and facial features. These categories may also carry some information on non-visible biological traits, such as the risk of developing particular diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Sickle-cell_disease" title="Sickle-cell disease">sickle-cell disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Current <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological evidence</a> generally support a "recent single origin" of modern humans in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Current genetic studies show that humans from Africa are most genetically diverse.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> But, human <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> sequences are very similar compared to many other animals.<sup id="cite_ref-REGWG_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REGWG-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>Ethnic groups are often linked by linguistic, cultural, ancestral, and national or regional ties. Race and ethnicity can lead to different social treatment called <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>.</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion_and_spirituality">Religion and spirituality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=14" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Religion and spirituality">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Change section: Religion and spirituality">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">Religion</a></div><p><a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">Religion</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">belief</a> of <a href="/wiki/Faith" class="mw-redirect" title="Faith">faith</a> in a higher being, spirit, or any system of ideas that a group of people believe in. To have <a href="/wiki/Faith" class="mw-redirect" title="Faith">faith</a> in a belief is to have the belief without proof that it is true. Faith can bring people together because they all believe in the same thing. Some of the things religions talk about are what happens after <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>, why humans exist, how humans came to exist (<a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation</a>), and what is good to do and not to do (<a href="/wiki/Morality" title="Morality">morality</a>). Some people are very <a href="/wiki/Religious" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious">religious</a>. Many people believe in one all-powerful <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">god</a>; some people believe in more than one god; some people are <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheists</a>, who do not believe in a god; and some people are <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnostics</a>, who are not sure if there is a god. People who believe in one or more gods, but do not belong to a religion, are called <a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">deists</a>.</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_technology">Science and technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=15" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Science and technology">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Change section: Science and technology">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">Science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">Technology</a></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aldrin_Apollo_11_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Aldrin_Apollo_11_cropped.jpg/150px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_cropped.jpg" width="150" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Aldrin_Apollo_11_cropped.jpg/225px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Aldrin_Apollo_11_cropped.jpg/300px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="874" data-file-height="1400" /></a><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aldrin_Apollo_11_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In the 20th century technology moved forward enough to allow a person to land on <a href="/wiki/The_Moon" class="mw-redirect" title="The Moon">the Moon</a>.</div></div></div><p>Technology are the things and methods which humans use to make tasks easier. Science is understanding how the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> and the things in it work. Technology used to be quite simple. It was passed on by people telling others, until <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a> was invented. This allowed technology to <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/develop" class="extiw" title="wikt:develop">develop</a> much quicker. Now people understand more and more about the world and the universe. The use of the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> by <a href="/wiki/Galileo" class="mw-redirect" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein's</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lasers" class="mw-redirect" title="Lasers">lasers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Computing" class="mw-redirect" title="Computing">computing</a> are all scientific discoveries. Technology is of great importance to science, to medicine, and to everyday life.</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War">War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=16" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: War">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Change section: War">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">War</a></div><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nagasakibomb.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Nagasakibomb.jpg/100px-Nagasakibomb.jpg" width="100" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Nagasakibomb.jpg/150px-Nagasakibomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Nagasakibomb.jpg/200px-Nagasakibomb.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3245" data-file-height="3877" /></a><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nagasakibomb.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 'mushroom cloud' from the <a href="/wiki/Nagasaki" title="Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a> <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bomb</a></div></div></div><p>A <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a> is a <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/lethal" class="extiw" title="wikt:lethal">lethal</a> fight between large groups of people, usually <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">countries</a> or <a href="/wiki/State" title="State">states</a>. A war involves the use of lethal weapons as both sides try to kill the other. It is estimated that during the 20th century, between 167 and 188 million humans <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">died</a> because of war.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The people who fight for a state in wars are called <a href="/wiki/Soldiers" class="mw-redirect" title="Soldiers">soldiers</a>. The people who fight in wars, but not for a state, are usually called "fighters".</p><p>Modern wars are very different from wars a thousand or even a hundred years ago. Modern war involves sabotage, <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla warfare</a>. In modern-day wars, civilians (people who are not soldiers) are often targets. An example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear bomb">nuclear bomb</a> dropped on <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nagasaki" title="Nagasaki">Nagasaki</a> at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The bombs killed as many as 140,000 people in Hiroshima and 80,000 in Nagasaki by the end of 1945,<sup id="cite_ref-rerf-deaths_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rerf-deaths-52">[52]</a></sup> about half on the days of the bombings. Since then, thousands more have died from wounds or illness because of <a href="/wiki/Radiation_poisoning" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiation poisoning">exposure to radiation</a> released by the bombs.<sup id="cite_ref-DOE-HIRO_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-HIRO-53">[53]</a></sup> In both cities, the overwhelming majority of the dead were civilians. In Germany, Austria, and Great Britain, conventional bombs were used. About 60,595 British,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and 550,000 German,<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> civilians were killed by planes bombing cities.</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=17" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: References">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Change section: References">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="column-width: 30em; -moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;"><ol class="references"><li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><a href="#cite_ref-1">↑</a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bartleby.com/61/99/H0259900.html">"homo sapiens"</a>.</cite><span title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft.btitle=homo+sapiens&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.bartleby.com%2F61%2F99%2FH0259900.html&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fsimple.wikipedia.org%3AHuman" class="Z3988"><span style="display:none;">&#160;</span></span></span></li><li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><a href="#cite_ref-2">↑</a></span> <span class="reference-text">Groves Colin; Wilson D.E. &amp; Reeder D.M.
