<tr>      <td align="center"><font face="Arial"><small>      Bipedal locomotion</small></font></td>    </tr>  </table>  <p><font face="Arial">The <span lang="en-us">comparatively minor anatomical</span> differences between  humans and apes are largely a result of our habitual <a href="glossary.htm#bipedal">bipedalism</a>  <a href="sounds/bipedalism.mp3">  <img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="/web/20130111004211im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/primate/images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a>.&nbsp; 	<span lang="en-us">A number of changes in our bodies were related to the 	evolution of this form of locomotion.&nbsp; Unlike apes, our</span> arms are  relatively short and weak compared to our legs.&nbsp; Our feet no longer have the ability  to effectively grasp and manipulate objects <span lang="en-us">because</span> the<span lang="en-us"> 	toes became shorter and the</span> big toe moved up into line with the  others.&nbsp; Human feet also have lengthened and acquired an arch, making them better  body supports.&nbsp; The human <a href="glossary.htm#pelvis">pelvis</a> and spinal column also have been modified for an erect posture and 	<span lang="en-us">efficient </span>bipedal locomotion.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; 	The pelvis became shorter, broader, and more bowl shaped.&nbsp; This 	provided greater stability for walking and running.</span>&nbsp;   We are now essentially fully terrestrial animals.&nbsp; <span lang="en-us">Nature 	very likely selected for longer legs with powerful muscles and spring-like 	tendons in humans&nbsp;because it is more 	efficient for walking and especially running bipedally.&nbsp; Research done 	by Herman Pontzer of Washington University in St. Louis, Missouri indicates 	that longer legs require less up-and-down movement while running and, 	therefore, reduce the amount of energy needed to move rapidly.&nbsp; This 	relatively lower rate of energy consumption would also allow humans to travel 	farther with the same calorie expenditure.&nbsp; In addition, the largely 	hairless human body with its abundance of sweat glands allows us to remain 	cooler while running than if we only relied on panting like most other 	mammals.&nbsp; This no doubt was a major advantage for our early human 	ancestors in the competition with other hunters and scavengers for meat in 	warm climates.&nbsp; Humans can easily be outrun by many other animals over 	short distances.&nbsp; However, we are endurance runners and can ultimately 	run down virtually all other land animals.</span></font></p>	<p><font face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">A downside of the evolution of 	efficient bipedalism in humans is that it resulted in changes in the pelvis 	which unfortunately included a narrower birth canal in females.&nbsp; As a 	consequence, giving birth is a more difficult and riskier process for us 	than for most other mammal species.&nbsp; During delivery, human babies must 	partially rotate laterally twice during their passage through the oval birth 	canal in order for their comparatively large heads and then their broad 	shoulders to make it through.&nbsp; This is usually a long, tiring, and 	painful process for the mother as well as a risky one for her baby.&nbsp; 	Because of this, human mothers generally seek help from a &quot;midwife&quot; for the 	delivery.&nbsp; Other primates give birth without assistance.&nbsp; A 	partial evolutionary solution to this birth difficulty for humans was 	fetuses being born at a less mature stage, when their heads and torsos are smaller.&nbsp; 	The trade off is that human infants are more vulnerable.&nbsp; By 	comparison, chimpanzees at birth are neurologically and cognitively ahead of 	human babies of the same age, but the chimps begin to fall behind by about 	six months old because of the more rapid continued development of the human 	brain following birth.</span></font></p>	<p><font face="Arial">With the exception of these differences, we are quite similar to the African apes  anatomically and genetically, especially to the chimpanzees   and bonobos.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; Humans have 46 chromosomes in their 	cells while all of the great apes have 48.&nbsp; In reality, this difference 	is not as great as it would initially seem because the 7th and 19th week after conception.&nbsp; </span>It is not surprising that there are some 	striking differences between the great apes and humans in mental abilities.&nbsp; 	People have much more complex forms of verbal communication than any other primate  species.&nbsp; We are the only animal to create and use <a href="glossary.htm#symbol">symbols</a> as a means of communication.&nbsp; We also have more varied and complex social  organizations.&nbsp; The most distinctive feature of humans is our mental ability to  create new ideas and complex technologies.&nbsp; This has proven invaluable in the  competition for survival.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; However, the great apes are 	also surprisingly intelligent, having mental levels equivalent to a 3-4 year 	old human child.&nbsp; This is sufficient to allow them to learn and use the 	sign language of deaf humans in at least a rudimentary way, but they do not 	have the capability of producing human speech and language.&nbsp; This is 	likely due to the fact that they have a different form of another key 	regulator gene known as FOXP2.</span></font></p>	<p><font face="Arial">There <span lang="en-us">is one</span> additional curious difference between 	humans and all other primates that <span lang="en-us">is</span> worth noting.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; 	O</span>lder<span lang="en-us"> human</span> females go through menopause and become 	sterile<span lang="en-us">,</span> often decades before dying of old age.&nbsp; Female chimpanzees, 	gorillas, and other non-human primates <span lang="en-us">usually </span>remain capable of conception and 	giving birth even when they are very old<span lang="en-us">.&nbsp; In the 	wild, they live only 	a relatively short amount of time following menopause, if they go through it 	at all</span>.&nbsp; One explanation for this 	difference in humans is that<span lang="en-us"> years of life following</span> menopause has proven to have natural selection 	value for our species.&nbsp; Having raised their own children, 	post-menopausal women around the world often take care of their 	grandchildren while their daughters are working.&nbsp; It is argued that 	this increases the chances that the grandchildren will survive to adulthood 	because they receive this additional experienced and caring attention.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; 	Grandmothers helping daughters to raise their children also allows mothers 	to have additional babies before the older ones are mature enough to take 	care of themselves.&nbsp; All other primates normally do not give birth 	again before their current child no longer needs parental care.</span></font></p>	<p align="center"><font size="1"><br>	</font><b><font face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">	<font size="5" color="#808080">Review
