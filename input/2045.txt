Height is also 		on the list. Starting at about age 40, people typically lose about half 		an inch each decade, with some faster shrinkage after age 70. Senior 		citizens may shrink as much as three inches.</p>		<table border="0" cellspacing="1" width="200" align="left" id="table21">			<tr>				<td>&nbsp;</td>				<td bgcolor="#000080">				<p class="CellBanner">Related Stories</td>				<td>&nbsp;</td>			</tr>			<tr>				<td>&nbsp;</td>				<td bgcolor="#FBFE8D">	<font face="Arial" size="2">      		<font class="content" xmlns:fo="http://www.w3.org/1999/XSL/Format">                                                               <font face="Arial, Helvetica, Verdana">		<span style="font-family: Arial, Helvetica, Verdana">                                                                 	<font face="Arial" size="2">		<p class="CellHead"><a href="5-06-10ShrinkingBrain.htm" target="_top">Shrinking Brains of Aging Do Not Impact Mental 		Abilities: New Study</a></p>		<p class="CellText">		June 10, 2005 � As people pass into their 60�s the 		brain usually begins to shrink but new research says this shrinkage has 		no impact on the individual's capacity to think or learn, which 		challenges conventional views. 		<a target="_blank" href="5-06-10ShrinkingBrain.htm">Read more...</a></p>		<p class="CellHead"><a href="5-10-25LessonsAgingBody.htm" target="_top">Seven Lessons You Can Learn From Your Aging Body</a></p>		<p class="CellText"><b>Emotional, Spiritual Riches of the Aging Body 		Explored in New Book</b></p>		<p class="CellText" style="margin-top: 3px; margin-bottom: 2px">				<font style="font-size: 9pt">By Pat Samples,<br>				Author of Body 		Odyssey: Lessons from the Bones and Belly</font></p>		<p class="CellText">Oct. 25, 2005 - Want to feel more at ease in your 		body? Too often we rush through our days, barely noticing the warnings 		our bodies give us or even the pleasures they experience. What might 		they have to tell us if we listened to their wisdom?		<a href="5-10-25LessonsAgingBody.htm" target="_top">Read more...</a></p>		<p class="CellHead"><a href="5-09-25ShrinkingBones.htm" target="_top">Shrinking Bones Could Be Why You Look Older</a></p>		<p class="CellText">Sept. 25, 2005 - Blame your bones if you think you 		look older. Gravity may be pulling at your skin, but the shrinking of 		facial bones plays a surprisingly significant part in facial aging, 		according to a study presented today at the American Society of Plastic 		Surgeons (ASPS) Plastic Surgery 2005 conference in Chicago. 		<a href="5-09-25ShrinkingBones.htm" target="_top">Read more...</a></p>				</font>                                                     		</span>                                                                                     		</font>                                                                          		</font>                                                                                     		</font>                                                                                     		<p class="CellHead" style="text-align: center">		<a href="/web/20130219004303/http://seniorjournal.com/Aging.htm" target="_top">More news on Aging - click here</a></td>				<td>&nbsp;</td>			</tr>		</table>		<p class="NewsStory">Why do we shrink, what are the consequences, and 		what can we do? The December issue of Harvard Health Letter answers 		these questions, says a press release issued today, which led 		SeniorJournal.com to seek other answers to the changing, aging body.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">What causes height loss? Fractures of the bones of 		the spine can contribute, as can slouching. </p>		<p class="NewsStory">What are the consequences? For many people, losing 		a little bit of height doesn�t cause any health problems. But severe 		kyphosis (the medical term for being hunched over) sometimes affects 		breathing and causes neck and back pain.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">What can you do to slow down shrinkage?</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&nbsp; &#9679; Improve your posture. Focus on exercises that 		will strengthen back muscles. One involves lying on your stomach and 		then lifting your head and shoulders. Yoga or tai chi are other options.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&nbsp; &#9679; Strengthen your bones. The other way to limit 		height loss is to keep bones strong to prevent fractures. The single 		best way to do that is to stay active. Getting enough calcium and 		vitamin D is also important.</p>		<p class="NewsStory"><u><b>More Changes to Senior Bodies</b></u></p>		<p class="NewsStory">Getting shorter, however, is just one of the 		changes to our body that happens as we age. This is what Medline Plus, 		the medical encyclopedia for senior citizens by the National Library of 		Medicine and the National Institutes of Health, says about changes in 		our body shape as we age.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;Many people are concerned with changes in their 		body shape as they age. Although some changes inevitably occur with 		aging, your lifestyle choices may slow or accelerate these changes.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;The human body is made up of fat, lean tissue 		(muscles and organs), bones, water, and other substances. As we age, the 		amount and distribution of these materials will change.</p>		<table border="0" width="468" align="right" cellspacing="1" bgcolor="#FFFEDF" height="60" id="table22">			<tr>				<td>				<!--webbot bot="Include" U-Include="../../_adstuff/AD-Links-Sections/AgingButton.htm" TAG="BODY" startspan --><div align="center">	<table border="0" width="468" cellpadding="0" style="border-collapse: collapse" height="0">		<tr>			<td style="border: 3px double #C0C0C0">			<div align="center">  <center>  <table border="0" cellpadding="0" style="border-collapse: collapse" bordercolor="#111111" width="468" height="60">    <tr>      <td align="center" width="100%" bgcolor="#F3F5F5" style="border: 1px solid #000080">      <script type="text/javascript"><!--google_ad_client = "ca-pub-6904530689694087";/* Bnr 468x60-4-18-2011 */google_ad_slot = "3398744682";google_ad_width = 468;google_ad_height = 60;//--></script><script type="text/javascript" src="https://web.archive.org/web/20130219004303js_/http://pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/show_ads.js"></script></td>    </tr>  </table>  </center></div></td>		</tr>	</table></div><!--webbot bot="Include" i-checksum="30449" endspan --></td>			</tr>		</table>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;Fat tissue may become increasingly deposited 		toward the center of the body, including around the abdominal organs. Your 		muscles, liver, kidney, and other organs may lose some of their cells. 		This process is called atrophy. Bones may lose some of their minerals 		and become less dense (a condition called osteopenia, or at its later 		stage, osteoporosis). Tissue loss reduces the amount of water in your 		body.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;<u>You may become shorter</u>. The tendency to 		become shorter occurs among all races and both sexes. Height loss is 		related to		<a style="font-family: Arial; color: #331166; text-decoration: underline; text-underline: single" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130219004303/http://www.nlm.nih.gov/medlineplus/ency/article/004015.htm" target="_top">		aging changes in the bones, muscles, and joints</a>.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;People typically lose about 1 cm (0.4 inches) 		every 10 years after age 40. Height loss is even greater after 70 years 		old. In total, you may lose 1 to 3 inches in height as you age.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;This varies, however. <u>Physical activity, a 		proper diet, and treating osteoporosis can help reduce height loss.</u></p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;Men often gain weight until about age 55, then 		begin to lose weight. This may be related to a drop in the male sex 		hormone testosterone. Women usually gain weight until age 65, then begin 		to lose weight. Weight loss is, in part, caused by a loss of muscle 		tissue.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;Of course, weight loss or gain varies from person 		to person, too. Diet and exercise play a large role in these changes.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;A change in total body water may make older people 		more likely to get dehydrated. Pay attention to how much fluid you 		drink. Body changes also play an important role in how your body uses 		drugs. Thus, medication doses may need to be adjusted in older people.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;The loss of muscle mass in the legs and changes in 		body shape can affect balance, contributing to falls.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;Although many age-related changes cannot be 		prevented, you can take certain steps to help slow or reduce them. These 		steps include exercise, avoiding smoking, and eating healthfully. 		Excessive alcohol use and illicit drug use can accelerate age-related 		changes.</p>		<p class="NewsStory">&quot;The picture of a hunched over, frail man or woman 		with pot belly and skinny arms and legs does not happen to everyone.
