In doing  this, it is important to suspend our own biases as much as possible and to take a  <a href="glossary.htm#cultural_relativism">cultural relativity</a> approach.&nbsp; That is to  say, we must not let our own cultural biases get in the way of understanding the lives of  other people.&nbsp; This is a very difficult task given the emotionally charged feelings  and deep beliefs that <span lang="en-us">most people</span>  have concerning race and ethnicity.&nbsp; However, suspending  these attitudinal barriers in order to gain a better understanding of the phenomena is  worth the effort.</font></p>  <p><font face="Arial">Have you ever   asked yourself what are the defining characteristics of being &quot;white&quot; or   &quot;black&quot; in America today?&nbsp; Is it solely a matter of skin color?&nbsp; Are   other factors as important or even more important?&nbsp; What makes someone   &quot;Hispanic <a href="sounds/Hispanic.mp3">  <img border="0" src="/web/20130106212622im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/ethnicity/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" width="20" height="19"></a>&quot;   or &quot;Latino  <a href="sounds/Latino.mp3">  <img border="0" src="/web/20130106212622im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/ethnicity/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" width="20" height="19"></a>.&quot;&nbsp;  Is it language?&nbsp; Country of origin?&nbsp; Cultural  traditions?&nbsp; Family values?&nbsp; Religion?&nbsp; Skin color?&nbsp; Can you be  &quot;white&quot; or &quot;black&quot; and Hispanic at the same time?&nbsp;   Can you be a non-Spanish speaking member of one of Mexico's Indian communities   and be Hispanic?&nbsp; Are people from Spain and Portuguese speaking   Brazilians Hispanic? The  answers to these questions probably are not entirely clear to you nor are they to most  Americans.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; It addition, the answers very likely will 	differ depending on the region of America in which you live.</span><a name="return_from_ethnic_identity_question"></a></font></p>  <div align="center">    <center>    <table border="0" cellspacing="2" style="border-collapse: others is not always  straight forward.&nbsp; Race and ethnic group labels in America are not clearly based on  criteria that everyone understand, agree with, and can easily use.&nbsp; As a result,  someone else may label you in a way that you consider inaccurate and very offensive.&nbsp;  This can instantly create a barrier to open communication even if the slight was unintended.</font></p>  <p><font face="Arial">To comprehend the human diversity of the United States or any other  <span lang="en-us">country</span>, it is important to first understand the criteria commonly used for making group  distinctions.&nbsp; These generally are based on cultural and/or biological factors.&nbsp;  Americans tend to see each other in terms of age, economic class, religion, <a href="glossary.htm#gender">gender</a>  <a href="sounds/gender.mp3">  <img border="0" src="/web/20130106212622im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/ethnicity/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" width="20" height="19"></a>, ethnicity, and race.&nbsp; We are usually a member  of a particular group for each of these criteria.&nbsp; Which of our group identities is  most important varies with the social situation.&nbsp; In America today, gender,  ethnicity, and race often have the most far ranging impacts on us as individuals.</font></p>  <p><font face="Arial"><font color="#333333"><b>Ethnicity</b></font> refers to selected cultural and sometimes physical  characteristics used to classify people into groups or categories considered to  be significantly different from others.&nbsp; Commonly recognized American ethnic groups  include American Indians, Latinos, Chinese, African Americans, European Americans,  etc.&nbsp; In some cases, ethnicity involves merely a loose group identity with little or  no cultural traditions in common. &nbsp;This is the case with many Irish and German  Americans.&nbsp; In contrast, some ethnic groups are coherent subcultures with a shared  language and body of tradition.&nbsp; Newly arrived immigrant groups often fit this  pattern.</font></p>  <p><font face="Arial">It is important not to confuse the term  <a href="glossary.htm#minority_group">minority</a> with  <a href="glossary.htm#ethnic_group">ethnic  group</a>.&nbsp; Ethnic groups may be either a minority or a majority in a  population.&nbsp; Whether a group is a minority or a majority also is not an absolute fact  but depends on the perspective.&nbsp; For instance, in some towns along the southern  border of the U.S., people of Mexican ancestry are the overwhelming majority population  and control most of the important social and political institutions but are still defined by state  and national governments as a minority. &nbsp; In small  <a href="glossary.htm#homogenous_society">homogenous societies</a>, such as those of  <a href="glossary.htm#hunters_and_gatherers">hunters and gatherers</a> and <a href="glossary.htm#pastoralists">pastoralists</a>  <a href="sounds/pastoralists.mp3">  <img border="0" src="/web/20130106212622im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/ethnicity/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" width="20" height="19"></a>, there is essentially only one ethnic  group and no minorities.</font></p>  <table border="0" id="table4" align="center"></td>	</tr>	</table>  <p><font face="Arial">For many people, ethnic categorization implies a connection between  biological inheritance and culture.&nbsp; They believe  that biological inheritance determines much  of cultural identity.&nbsp; If this were true, for instance, African American cultural  traits, such as &quot;black English&quot;, would stem from genetic inheritance.&nbsp;  <span lang="en-us">We now know that this</span>  is not true<span lang="en-us">--biological</span> race and culture are not the 	same thing<span lang="en-us">.&nbsp; </span>The pioneering<span lang="en-us">	</span>English anthropologist E<span lang="en-us">dward</span> Tylor	<span lang="en-us">may have been the first scientist to understand this fact 	and to state it in print.&nbsp; In 1871, he wrote that cultural traits are 	entirely learned.&nbsp; Subsequently, a baby</span> can be placed into another culture shortly after birth and can be  thoroughly <a href="glossary.htm#enculturation">enculturated</a>  <a href="sounds/enculturated.mp3">  <img border="0" src="/web/20130106212622im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/ethnicity/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" width="20" height="19"></a>  to that culture,  regardless of their skin color, body shape, and other presumed racial features.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;	</span>For example, both women in the photographs	<span lang="en-us">on the right</span> are genetically African, but  they do not speak the same language nor do they share any other significant cultural  patterns due to the fact that they were brought up in very different societies.&nbsp; The  African American woman  is far more similar culturally to her European American  neighbors than to the<span lang="en-us"> West African</span> woman<span lang="en-us"> 	from Senegal</span>.</font></p>  <p><font face="Arial">A <font color="#333333"><b>race</b></font> is a 	biological subspecies <a href="sounds/subspecies.mp3">  <img border="0" src="/web/20130106212622im_/http://anthro.palomar.edu/ethnicity/images/horn.gif" alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" width="20" height="19"></a>, or variety of a species,  consisting of a more or less distinct population with anatomical traits that distinguish  it clearly from other races.&nbsp; This biologist's definition does not fit the reality of  human genetic variation today.&nbsp; We are an extremely homogenous  species<span lang="en-us"> genetically</span>.&nbsp; As a matter of fact, all humans today are   99.9% genetically identical, and most of the  variation that does occur is   in the difference between males and females and our unique personal traits.&nbsp;   This homogeneity is very unusual in the animal kingdom.&nbsp; Even our closest   <span lang="en-us">biological </span>relatives, the chimpanzees have 2-3 times more genetic variation than people.&nbsp;   Orangutans have 8-10 times more variation.</font></p>  <p><font face="Arial">It is now clear that our human &quot;races&quot; are  	<span lang="en-us">primarily </span>cultural creations, not  biological realities.&nbsp; The <span lang="en-us">commonly held belief in the 	existence</span> of human biological races is based on the false  assumption that anatomical traits, such as skin color and   specific facial  characteristics, cluster together in single distinct groups of people.&nbsp;  They do  not.&nbsp; There are no clearly distinct &quot;black&quot;, &quot;white&quot;, or other  races.</font></p>  <p><font face="Arial">The popularly held view of human races ignores the fact that anatomical  traits supposedly identifying a particular race are often found extensively in other  populations as well.&nbsp; This is due to the fact that similar natural selection factors  in different parts of the world often result in the evolution of similar  adaptations.&nbsp; For instance, intense sunlight in tropical latitudes has selected for  darker skin color as a protection from intense ultraviolet radiation.&nbsp; As a result,  the dark brown skin color characteristic of  <a href="glossary.htm#sub-Saharan_Africa">sub-Saharan  Africans</a> is also found among unrelated populations in the Indian subcontinent,  Australia, New Guinea, and elsewhere in the Southwest Pacific.</font></p>  <div align="center">	<table border="0" id="table3">		<tr>			<td align="center">			<img border="0" face="Arial">Historically<span lang="en-us"> in the Western World</span>, human  &quot;races&quot; have been defined on the basis of a small number of superficial  anatomical characteristics that can be readily identified at a distance, thereby making discrimination easier.&nbsp;  Focusing on such  deceptive distinguishing traits as skin color, body shape, and hair texture causes us to  magnify differences and ignore similarities between people.&nbsp;  It is also important to  remember that these traits are no more accurate in making distinctions between human  groups than any other genetically inherited characteristics.&nbsp;  All such attempts to  scientifically divide humanity into biological races have proven fruitless.</font></p>  <p><font face="Arial">In the final analysis,
